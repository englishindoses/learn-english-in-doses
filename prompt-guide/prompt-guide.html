üéì Who This Is For

This prompt pack is aimed at ESL teachers and content creators who want to use AI as a tool to help them enhance their lessons and save time. It's designed for people who may not be very tech savvy and are uncertain about how to start using AI in their work. This pack was created with ESL teachers in mind, but it can be used by any language teacher or course content creator.

Technology is becoming an increasingly big part of our lives and that includes the classroom. From having to suddenly abandon books and whiteboards during the pandemic and use online platforms, video conferencing and digital tools, to the fear and uncertainty caused by AI. It sometimes feels like teachers are left to fend for themselves in the deep end.

With this guide you don't need any coding knowledge or experience with AI. You will get simple, reusable prompts that generate full lessons, curriculum outlines, and student-friendly learning activities that can be used both online and in-person. The prompts are accompanied by notes and follow-up prompts to help you edit, customise and adapt what AI has provided you with to suit your specific needs.

This doesn't mean that anybody can create high quality materials or that anybody can do a teacher's job. Teachers have invaluable experience and expertise. Teachers have studied hard and trained to be in the position they are in today, and AI cannot yet substitute this expertise. What AI does when you give it a prompt is create a lesson (for example) with activities for the student to do etc. However, there can be a variety of issues with AI's first output. As a teacher, you need to use your professional knowledge and experience to guide AI and edit the first output until it meets your professional standards.

AI helps you create high quality content much faster, but it can't do it without your guidance and instructions. Think of AI as your assistant, and yourself as a supervisor - you have to give clear instructions, check the output and provide feedback with further instructions until it's perfect.

This prompt pack will provide you with samples and templates for these instructions and help you familiarise yourself with AI.

Save time by copying your prompt into your chosen AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.). You can adjust tone, language level, or structure as needed.

ü§ñ Why Use AI for Lesson Creation?

Using AI to create ESL lessons has huge benefits for both teachers and students:

For Teachers:

  • Quickly generate fresh ideas tailored to each student.
  • Create professional looking content.
  • Stay consistent with lesson structure while adding variety.
  • Save time on planning and resource creation.
  • Reduce stress and burnout.

For Students:

  • Get personalised, engaging lessons designed for their level and interests.
  • Enjoy interactive activities and visuals that AI can help generate.
  • Benefit from a structured learning path that still feels fun and creative.

One of the biggest benefits of using AI to help you prepare lessons is that the speed allows you to tailor each one to each student, helping you enhance your lessons and provide higher quality learning. So if you're sceptical about AI or even afraid to try it, don't worry. Use this guide to do a trial run and experiment with it. Then you can decide exactly how AI can benefit you and your students.

üîÑ How to Use This Pack

This guide will provide you with ready-made prompts that you can copy and paste into an AI tool (like ChatGPT or Claude) to generate original content. Use follow-up prompts to customise, edit or fix errors and make the content your own. Think of prompts as "instructions" that you type into the chatbox. Typing prompts is exactly the same as sending an email or typed message with instructions to a colleague.

There are 3 main steps to follow:

  1. Start with a prompt to generate content.
  2. Check the content thoroughly.
  3. Follow up with instructions on what you want to change.

Then simply repeat steps 2 and 3 until you are satisfied with the results.

‚úèÔ∏è Prompt 4: Grammar Explanation (with quiz)
Prompt: "You are a professional ESL teacher, teaching adult ESL students. Please give me a clear, comprehensive, but concise explanation of the present perfect tense. It's aimed at an intermediate level adult student who is starting to study the present perfect now.
The explanation must be clear and concise.
Don't make it unnecessarily wordy.
Use realistic examples that sound natural to native English speakers.
Use examples from daily life, study and work.
Explain the "present perfect simple" fully. Provide form, use and plenty of examples.
Write a "Native Speaker Note" that shows how native speakers really use the grammar in real life. At the end include a 5 question quiz that checks the student's understanding of the grammar. Make sure it checks that the student knows how to use the grammar, not recognise it. Use an informative, professional but friendly tone. Don't use overly complicated explanations and use language that's easy for an intermediate student to understand. Use colours and layout that look professional but engaging."

What It Does: It creates a full grammar explanation that should include all the points you mentioned. It also creates a quiz at the end of the grammar explanation that tests the student's understanding of what they have studied.

How to use it: Again, copy and paste the prompt into your AI tool. You can edit the information and change things like student age, level, grammar topic. You can specify how many examples you want.

When AI has finished creating the explanation, always read it carefully, including the examples and quiz questions. AI can produce brilliant results, but it can make mistakes. It is our responsibility to check the content and make sure it's error free and meets the right needs.

How to add images

Most common AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude etc.) can't simply add images to the files they create. They can add icons and emojis, which are fine for some kinds of content and for titles, etc. But this is usually not enough for vocabulary activities where you want images to show the meaning of the word.

So how can we add images to our worksheets? Here are easy methods:

Method 1 - Using editing tools you're familiar with

If you already create your own materials and use tools like pdf editors, Canva, Microsoft Office etc, you can use these to add images the way you usually do.

Method 1.1 - Annotate with Kami - Or other PDF editor you already use:

Personally, I use Kami Google extension to share and collaborate on worksheets with my students. So what I do sometimes is while in the lesson, when the student asks me what a word means, I google an image and then copy and paste it onto the pdf in Kami. Then I resize it, draw lines or add a note to make the connection. You can also do this before the lesson with all the vocabulary if you prefer.

You can ask AI to leave a placeholder box in place of the images for the matching activity (for example) and then paste the images over those boxes.

Method 1.2 - Use Canva, powerpoint or other editing tools:

You can copy and paste your content into editing tools like powerpoint or Canva (or whatever you currently use) and add images your usual way.

Method 2 - Using AI:

Although AI can't simply add images on it's own, there are a few way you can provide it with the images for it to add to the html code. You can even do this yourself if you're a little tech savvy, but if you're like me then you need AI's help.

2.1 - Use the image address:

Step 1 - You find an image you want to use on the internet (for example, through Google search or websites that have images available for free use).

Step 2 - Right click on the image and click on "copy image address" (not copy link address)

Step 3 - Paste the image address into the AI chat and ask it to add the image to the document. You can even specify size and position.

2.2 - Local file reference.

You can save the image on your computer and then send AI the path reference. This is much simpler than it sounds. Follow these simple steps:

On Windows:

Step 1 - Hold down shift on your keyboard and right click on the image.

Step 2 - Click on "copy as path"

Step 3 - Paste into the AI chat and ask it to add this image to your document. You can add specifications like size or position.

On Mac:

  1. Select the image file
  2. Press Cmd + Option + C
  3. Paste into AI chat and ask it to add this image to your document. You can add specifications like size or position.

This method is an easy way to add images you have downloaded from Canva or other online image sources.

Copyright and plagiarism rules still apply. Check the Copyright and Plagiarism section for more details.

Always check copyright rules of resources and tools you use.

If you're unsure about how to do any of the above, ask AI to give you step by step instructions.

Bumps and Fixes When Using AI

As helpful as AI can be, it can still make mistakes or fail to follow all the instructions you gave. It's vital that you always check what AI has produced to ensure the quality and authenticity of your work. These bumps can sometimes be difficult to deal with, especially if you haven't faced them before.

Here are some common issues that I have come across and how to deal with them quickly and efficiently.

üîÅ 1. AI Doesn't Follow All Instructions First Time

Example: You asked for a grammar explanation with exactly 5 examples, but it gave 3.

Solution: Follow up with a specific prompt like:

"Please give me 2 more examples using natural-sounding language from work and daily life."
Example: You asked AI to format your document and gave it a list of requirements but it didn't meet these requirements.
Solution: Sometimes when there are many requirements, you might get one that slips through the cracks. In this case, edit the prompt you sent to AI, cut the requirement it missed and paste it higher on the list. Usually this will solve the problem.

‚ùå 2. Inaccurate or Incorrect Content

Example: AI may get grammar rules wrong or give false information.

Solution: Always read through your content carefully, especially when dealing with grammar and examples. If there's an error, reply with:

"There's a mistake in the example. 'She has ate' is incorrect. Please fix it."
"This sentence is not true 'When writing academic articles the passive voice is common but active voice is preferred for clarity' - Please replace it with something else."

You can also give it the exact sentence you want to replace it with.

You can also ask AI to double-check:

"Please revise the grammar explanation and ensure all examples follow correct rules."
"Please proofread the worksheet and look for any errors or inconsistencies."

üß± 3. Clunky Layout or Visual Output

Example: The layout looks crowded, colours are too bright for print, or headings are missing.

Solution: Point these issues out using a follow-up like this:

"Please format this with clear headings, space between sections, and a print-friendly background."

Or:

"Use pastel colours and boxes to separate sections. Keep the background white for printing."

You can also click on "retry" next to the "edit" button on the previous prompt. Sometimes the second try is better, sometimes not much changes. In that case use a prompt similar to the ones above and give the requirements you want it too meet.

üß† 4. Content Is Too Complicated or Too Simple

Example: A worksheet for A1 students includes vocabulary like "environmentally conscious" or complex instructions. - This one happens to me often, especially when making material for beginner young learners who have very limited vocabulary.

Solution: Ask for adjustments:

"Please simplify this for A1 level students."
"Rewrite the instructions using simple language."
"Make this more appropriate for a B2 level student."

You could also specify in your original prompt that
"the students have very limited vocabulary so make sure to use simple instructions"

üéØ 5. It can't actually do something that you've asked

Example: You asked it to create an activity the presents new vocabulary with images (e.g. jobs and professions). Unable to actually include pictures on its own, AI either uses emojis or placeholders or image descriptions instead of images.

Note: This doesn't happen very often as there aren't that many things that AI can't do for you in this context. Being aware of limitations (such as the ability to add images) and how to get around them can help you avoid this situation all together.

But, should it come up, here's what to do:

Solution: Double check the limitations of the AI tool you're using. You can simply ask it:

"You added image descriptions instead of actual images. Please explain why you did this."

Sometimes, depending on the tool, it will explain that it can't do what you've asked and may even give you alternative options. You can ask:

"What alternative options do I have?"
"How can I add images using AI?"

Sometimes, it may say something along the lines of "You're absolutely right to point that out!" and proceed to admit to its mistake, promise to fix it, but then the results are exactly the same as before. This can cause a bit of a spiral, in which case I like to ask another AI with something like:
"Can claude.ai add images to my worksheet on its own? What options do I have?"

üìÑ 6.1. Page Break Issues in PDFs

Example: When saving an AI-generated file as a PDF, activities may be cut in half.

Solution: Use this in your prompt:

"Please format it so that page breaks do not separate questions from their answer space. Optimise for A4 with full pages and proper margins."

If you want to keep background colours in the PDF, specify:

"Please make sure that when I save this as a PDF, the colours are retained."

If you've already included this requirement in your prompt but it didn't follow it, replying with a follow-up prompt should solve the issue.

üìÑ 6.2. Pages With Big Gaps

Example: When creating page breaks and keeping content together, sometimes you may end up with a page that has one little section that doesn't even take up half of it because the next section was too big and needed a page on its own.

Solution 1: Ask AI to resize sections:

"Page 3 has one small section and the rest of the page is blank. Please resize sections in order to avoid this issue."

Solution 2: Specify yourself where you want the page breaks.
"Please change the page breaks as follows: A break between Activity 1 Question 3 and Question 4, and a break before the Wrap up section."

Solution 3: Add a small section of content to fill the gap:

"On the first page there is the title and the introduction to the grammar rule, and then there is a big gap. Please include a new section, with examples of the grammar rule in use, to fill this gap."

Alternative Page Break Solution:

Sometimes I find that, perhaps depending on the size of each section, AI finds it difficult to get the page breaks right. In these cases I tell it specifically where to put the page breaks. Here is an example prompt you can use:

"Please put the page breaks in the following positions:
- Before Activity 2.
- Between questions 4 and 5 in activity 3.
- Resize activity 4 to fit on the same page as the rest of activity 3.
- Fit the homework and encouraging message on the last page."

This prompt might be a little wordy but it specifies exactly where the page breaks should go and makes it easier for AI to do it how you want. Sometimes this is an easier solution than giving it the choice.

üéì Tips for Best Results

Here are some tips and advice to help you optimise your results and be able to reuse the same prompts for a variety of content.

  • Think of AI as a human assistant (who works really fast and knows almost everything). If you were asking your assistant to prepare a lesson plan for you, what information would you give them?
  • You can ask AI for advice and suggestions. You can talk to it as if you were messaging a colleague. Explain what you want to do and say "Please give me some suggestions".
  • If you have a ready document with specifications you want to follow (such as guidelines you already use) you can upload the document to AI, tell it to read the guidelines carefully and use them to produce the content you want.
  • Upload samples of content, such as lesson plans, you already have and ask AI to use them as a template.
  • Add the student's gender, age, or interests to personalize prompts and outputs. For example, if you have a student who likes sports, just type "my student is a 14 year old girl who likes football" or "My student is an adult who's living in the UK and needs to communicate at work and in daily life."
  • Reuse your own best-performing prompts as templates. Especially the "style it up!" prompt. This can help you maintain a visual consistency across your content and build your brand. Visual consistency across lessons is also important for students.
  • Create your own prompt list or prompt library. When you have a prompt that gave you something great, save it with a clear title so you can copy and paste it again.
    For example, you edit the prompt to produce a nice looking lesson for your beginner students - save it. Then you edit the prompt for advanced adult students - save that one too.
  • Use follow-up prompts like "Make this more visual" or "Simplify for A1 level". AI rarely gives you perfect results first time, so always check the outcome and follow up.
  • Sometimes AI might miss one of your requirements. If this happens, simply repeat that requirement in a follow up.
  • Combine with Canva, Google Slides, or Notion for easy formatting. If you already use editing tools like Canva, you can copy the content AI gives you and paste it into your Canva worksheet, for example, and continue editing it as you normally would. This way you can do the formatting yourself instead of AI.
  • AI is changing and developing all the time. Some models allow you to link other online tools to the AI. At the time this prompt pack was being created, for example, Claude had a new feature and could link with Canva to create designs. After you become more familiar with AI, it might be worth checking out these kinds of features and testing them to see what you can do with them.

‚úÖ Final Tip: Think of AI as your assistant, and yourself as the editor-in-chief.

AI's output is like a first draft - it's not ready yet. You need to use your expertise and spend time to check it, edit it and ensure the quality of your final product. It's your expert knowledge and opinion that you have developed throughout your career that makes the final product yours and suitable for your students.
