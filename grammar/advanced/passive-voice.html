Passive Voice: Fundamentals

Introduction

The passive voice is a grammatical structure used to emphasise the person or thing affected by an action rather than the person or thing performing the action. It's particularly useful in formal writing, scientific texts, news reports, and situations where the performer of the action is unknown, unimportant, or obvious.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form passive sentences in different tenses correctly.
  • Understand when to use passive voice appropriately.
  • Recognise the differences between active and passive sentences.

Basic Passive Structure

Form: subject + be (conjugated) + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use it: Use passive voice when:

  • The focus is on the action receiver, not the doer
  • The person doing the action is unknown or unimportant
  • You want to be more formal or objective
  1. The report was written by Sarah yesterday. (past simple passive)
  2. These cakes are made with high-quality ingredients. (present simple passive)
  3. The flight has been delayed due to bad weather. (present perfect passive)

Native speaker note: In everyday conversation, native speakers often use active voice more frequently than passive. Passive voice is common in news reports, scientific writing, and formal documents.

Passive Voice in Different Tenses

Form: The only part that changes in different tenses is the form of "be"

When to use it: The choice of tense depends on when the action happened, just as in active voice.

  1. The company is being reorganised by new management. (present continuous passive)
  2. All the tickets had been sold before we arrived. (past perfect passive)
  3. The new bridge will be opened by the mayor next month. (future simple passive)

Native speaker note: Some tense forms in the passive are rarely used in conversation, such as past perfect continuous passive or future continuous passive.

Using "By" with Passive Voice

Form: passive sentence + by + agent (doer of the action)

When to use it: Include "by + agent" when:

  • The agent is important information
  • You want to clarify who performed the action
  • The agent is unexpected or surprising
  1. The novel was written by a famous author. (agent = a famous author)
  2. My car is being repaired by a new mechanic. (agent = a new mechanic)
  3. The traditional dance was performed by local children. (agent = local children)

Native speaker note: In many passive sentences, English speakers omit the "by phrase" when the agent is obvious or unimportant: "My bike was stolen" (we don't need to say "by someone").

Summary: Passive vs Active Voice

Active Voice Passive Voice Focus Example
Present Simple is/are + V3 Regular actions The room is cleaned daily.
Present Continuous is/are being + V3 Actions happening now The room is being cleaned now.
Past Simple was/were + V3 Completed actions The room was cleaned yesterday.
Past Continuous was/were being + V3 Actions in progress in the past The room was being cleaned when I arrived.
Present Perfect has/have been + V3 Completed actions with present relevance The room has been cleaned already.
Past Perfect had been + V3 Actions completed before another past action The room had been cleaned before the guests arrived.
Future Simple will be + V3 Future actions The room will be cleaned tomorrow.

Common Errors

1: Using passive voice with intransitive verbs

The mountain was arrived by the hikers.


The mountain was reached by the hikers. ‚úì

Intransitive verbs (like arrive, happen, sleep) don't take direct objects, so they cannot be used in passive voice. Use a transitive verb (like reach) instead.

2: Incorrect form of the past participle (V3)

The window was breaked during the storm.


The window was broken during the storm. ‚úì

Make sure to use the correct past participle form (V3) of irregular verbs in passive constructions.

3: Forgetting to change the verb tense

The house is build last year.


The house was built last year. ‚úì

Remember to change the form of "be" to match the appropriate tense (past simple in this example).

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Remember that passive voice requires a transitive verb (a verb that can take a direct object).

üìù TIP 2: To convert from active to passive, move the object to the subject position, add the appropriate form of "be", use the past participle of the main verb, and optionally add "by + original subject".

üìù TIP 3: Use passive voice when you want to emphasise what happened rather than who did it. This is particularly useful in academic writing, news reporting, and scientific papers.
