Multiple Adverbs

Introduction

When a sentence contains more than one adverb, the order in which they appear matters. Putting adverbs in the wrong order can make a sentence sound awkward or unnatural. English follows a general pattern for ordering multiple adverbs, especially when they appear at the end of a sentence. This page builds on the Adverb Position page and focuses on how to arrange two or more adverbs correctly.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Arrange multiple adverbs in the correct order at the end of a sentence.
  • Apply the manner‚Äìplace‚Äìtime pattern to sentences with two or more adverbs.
  • Adjust adverb order for emphasis and natural style.

The Manner‚ÄìPlace‚ÄìTime Rule

Form: subject + verb + (object) + manner adverb + place adverb + time adverb

When to use it: When two or more adverbs appear at the end of a sentence, they generally follow this order: manner (how), then place (where), then time (when). This is sometimes called the MPT rule. Not every sentence will have all three types, but when more than one is present, this order sounds the most natural.

  1. She worked quietly in the library all afternoon.
    (= manner + place + time.)
  2. He drove carefully through the city centre this morning.
    (= manner + place + time.)
  3. They walked slowly along the beach after dinner.
    (= manner + place + time.)

Native speaker note: Native speakers follow this order instinctively. If you ever feel uncertain, try saying the sentence aloud with manner first, then place, then time ‚Äî it will almost always sound right.

Two Adverbs: Common Combinations

When to use it: Most sentences with multiple adverbs use just two, not three. The most common combinations are manner + place, manner + time, and place + time. The same order rule applies ‚Äî follow MPT and simply skip the missing type.

Manner + place:

  1. She spoke confidently at the meeting.
    (= how + where.)
  2. He waited patiently at the airport.
    (= how + where.)
  3. They practised hard in the training room.
    (= how + where.)

Place + time:

  1. We arrived at the hotel late last night.
    (= where + when.)
  2. She works from home every Friday.
    (= where + when.)
  3. He stayed in the office until 8 p.m.
    (= where + when.)

Manner + time:

  1. She finished the task efficiently before the deadline.
    (= how + when.)
  2. He responded politely straight away.
    (= how + when.)
  3. They prepared carefully the night before.
    (= how + when.)

Adverbs of Frequency with Other Adverbs

Form: subject + frequency adverb (mid position) + verb + (object) + other adverbs (end position)

When to use it: Frequency adverbs (how often) stay in their usual mid position ‚Äî before the main verb. Other adverbs (manner, place, time) go in end position as normal. This means frequency adverbs do not compete for end position with manner, place, or time adverbs.

  1. She always works efficiently at the office.
    (= frequency in mid + manner and place at end.)
  2. He usually arrives at the station early.
    (= frequency in mid + place and time at end.)
  3. They often meet in the caf√© after work.
    (= frequency in mid + place and time at end.)

Changing Adverb Order for Emphasis

When to use it: The MPT rule is the standard order, but English allows some flexibility when you want to emphasise a particular adverb. You can move an adverb to front position to give it extra emphasis. The adverb you move to the front becomes the focus of the sentence.

Key rules:

  • Moving a time adverb to the front is very common and always sounds natural. It sets the scene for what follows.
  • Moving a place adverb to the front is also possible, especially in storytelling and descriptions.
  • Moving a manner adverb to the front is less common and can sound formal or dramatic. Use this sparingly.
  1. Every morning, she exercises at the gym.
    (= time moved to front for emphasis.)
  2. In the conference room, they discussed the plan quietly.
    (= place moved to front to set the scene.)
  3. Carefully, he opened the old suitcase.
    (= manner moved to front for dramatic effect.)

Key Differences

When deciding where to place multiple adverbs, the main question is which type each adverb belongs to.

  • Manner answers "how?" and goes first at the end.
  • Place answers "where?" and goes second.
  • Time answers "when?" and goes last.
  • Frequency answers "how often?" and stays in mid position.

Compare these sentences:

  • She studied hard in the library yesterday.
    (= MPT order ‚Äî sounds natural.)
  • She studied yesterday hard in the library.
    (= wrong order ‚Äî sounds awkward and unnatural.)
  • She always studies hard in the library.
    (= frequency in mid position + manner and place at end ‚Äî correct.)

Common Errors

1: Putting time before manner at the end of a sentence

‚ùå She finished yesterday quickly the report.


‚úì She finished the report quickly yesterday.

When two or more adverbs appear at the end of a sentence, follow the manner‚Äìplace‚Äìtime rule. Manner (how) comes before time (when).

2: Putting a frequency adverb at the end with other adverbs

‚ùå He arrives at the office early always.


‚úì He always arrives at the office early.

Frequency adverbs (always, usually, often) stay in mid position. They do not move to end position, even when other adverbs are there.

3: Placing a manner adverb between the verb and its object

‚ùå She read carefully the instructions at home.


‚úì She read the instructions carefully at home.

Manner adverbs go after the object, not between the verb and the object. Then place and time adverbs follow.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Think of the end of a sentence as having invisible slots: HOW ‚Üí WHERE ‚Üí WHEN. When you write a sentence with multiple adverbs, mentally fill in these slots in order. You do not need all three every time ‚Äî just keep the ones you use in this order.

üìå TIP 2: If a sentence with multiple adverbs at the end feels too long or heavy, move the time adverb to front position. This is a natural and common technique in English writing. For example, "Yesterday, she worked hard at the office" feels lighter than putting everything at the end.

üìå TIP 3: Read English news articles and highlight sentences with two or more adverbs. Notice the order the writer uses. You will see the MPT pattern appearing again and again, which will help you internalise the rule without memorising it.
