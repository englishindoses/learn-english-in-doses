Passive Reporting Verbs

Introduction

Passive reporting verbs are used to report information, opinions, or beliefs without stating the source directly. These structures are particularly common in news reports, academic writing, and formal discussions where the emphasis is on the information rather than who provided it. Using these structures effectively adds sophistication to your English and helps you sound more like a native speaker in formal contexts.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use passive reporting structures with present and past reference
  • Form complex passive constructions with reporting verbs
  • Incorporate these advanced structures appropriately in formal writing and speaking

Basic Passive Reporting Structures

Form: subject + passive reporting verb + infinitive

Common reporting verbs in passive form (ordered from high to low certainty):

  • is/are known to... (highest certainty)
  • is/are understood to...
  • is/are considered to...
  • is/are expected to...
  • is/are believed to...
  • is/are thought to...
  • is/are reported to...
  • is/are said to...
  • is/are supposed to...
  • is/are alleged to... (lowest certainty)

When to use it: Use these structures when:

  • Reporting general opinions or information
  • The source of information is unknown or unimportant
  • Writing formally, especially in academic or journalistic contexts
  • Discussing widely accepted facts

Different levels of certainty:

  • High certainty: is known to, is understood to
  • Medium certainty: is believed to, is considered to
  • Low certainty: is said to, is alleged to
  1. The company is said to be developing a new smartphone. (when information comes from rumors or unofficial sources)
  2. The treatment is believed to reduce symptoms in most patients. (when there is evidence but not complete proof)
  3. The manuscript is thought to date back to the 14th century. (when experts have this opinion)

Complete example with context:
Although no official announcement has been made, the company is said to be planning major layoffs next month. (shows how this structure is used to report unofficial information)

Native speaker note: In casual conversation, native speakers rarely use these structures. Instead, they use phrases like "I heard that...", "Apparently...", or "They say that..."

Passive Reporting with Past Reference

Form: subject + passive reporting verb + to have + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use this structure when:

  • Reporting something that happened in the past
  • Distancing yourself from the reliability of the information
  • Discussing historical events or past actions
  • Reporting news about past events
  1. The politician is said to have accepted bribes from business leaders. (when reporting allegations)
  2. The painting is believed to have been stolen during the war. (when discussing a historical event with some uncertainty)
  3. The patient is reported to have recovered completely after the treatment. (when citing medical reports)

Complete example with context:
Despite his denials, the minister is alleged to have received illegal campaign funds during the last election. (shows how media might report accusations)

Native speaker note: This structure is common in news reporting when facts need to be presented without the reporter taking responsibility for their accuracy. In everyday speech, people often say "I heard he did..." or "Apparently she went..."

Complex Passive Reporting Structures

Form for ongoing actions: subject + passive reporting verb + to be + verb-ing

Form for passive states: subject + passive reporting verb + to be + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use these complex forms when:

  • Reporting current ongoing actions (to be verb-ing)
  • Reporting a passive situation or state (to be + past participle)
  • Discussing formal or academic topics
  • When precise timing or state is important
  1. The prime minister is reported to be considering a cabinet reshuffle. (ongoing action with political significance - medium certainty)
  2. The suspect is believed to be hiding somewhere in the mountains. (current ongoing situation in news reporting - medium-high certainty)
  3. The ancient temple is considered to be preserved in its original state. (passive state with historical importance - high certainty)

Complete example with context:
According to unofficial sources, the two companies are understood to be negotiating a merger that could change the industry landscape. (shows how business news reports unconfirmed ongoing processes)

Native speaker note: These complex forms are almost exclusively used in formal writing. In conversation, native speakers simplify to "They're apparently working on..." or "I heard it's being built by..."

Summary: Passive Reporting Verbs

Structure Form Time Reference Example
Present simple is/are + reported verb + to + infinitive Present facts or states He is said to live in Paris.
Perfect form is/are + reported verb + to have + past participle Past events She is believed to have left the country.
Continuous form is/are + reported verb + to be + verb-ing Current ongoing actions They are thought to be working on a solution.
Passive infinitive is/are + reported verb + to be + past participle Present passive situations The document is believed to be signed by the president.
Perfect passive is/are + reported verb + to have been + past participle Past passive actions The painting is thought to have been stolen.
Active equivalent People + reporting verb + that + subject + verb Varies with tense People say that he lives in Paris.

Common Errors

1: Using "that" instead of infinitive

The company is expected that will increase profits.


The company is expected to increase profits. ‚úì

After passive reporting verbs, use the infinitive form (to + verb) not a "that" clause.

2: Wrong form for past reference

The suspect is believed to steal the painting last month.


The suspect is believed to have stolen the painting last month. ‚úì

For past actions, use "to have + past participle (V3)" after the reporting verb.

3: Incorrect passive structure in complex forms

The treatment is thought to been working well.


The treatment is thought to be working well. ‚úì

Remember to use "to be + verb-ing" for ongoing actions, not "to been".

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Choose the right reporting verb based on certainty level. Use "is known to" for established facts, "is believed to" for general consensus, and "is alleged to" for unproven claims that might be controversial.

üìù TIP 2: To convert an active reporting sentence to passive, follow this pattern:
- Active: "People say that he works hard." ‚Üí Passive: "He is said to work hard."
- Active: "Scientists believe that she discovered a new species." ‚Üí Passive: "She is believed to have discovered a new species."

üìù TIP 3: In academic writing, use passive reporting verbs to maintain an objective tone and avoid direct attribution. However, in personal communication, use simpler alternatives like "apparently" or "I've heard that" which sound more natural.
