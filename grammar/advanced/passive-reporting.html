Passive Reporting Verbs

Introduction

Passive reporting verbs are used to report information, opinions, or beliefs without stating the source directly. These structures are particularly common in news reports, academic writing, and formal discussions where the emphasis is on the information rather than who provided it. Using these structures effectively adds sophistication to your English and helps you sound more like a native speaker in formal contexts.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use passive reporting structures with present and past reference
  • Form complex passive constructions with reporting verbs
  • Incorporate these advanced structures appropriately in formal writing and speaking

Basic Passive Reporting Structures

Form: subject + passive reporting verb + infinitive

Common reporting verbs in passive form:

  • is/are said to...
  • is/are thought to...
  • is/are believed to...
  • is/are considered to...
  • is/are reported to...
  • is/are known to...
  • is/are understood to...
  • is/are expected to...
  • is/are supposed to...
  • is/are alleged to...

When to use it: Use these structures when:

  • Reporting general opinions or information
  • The source of information is unknown or unimportant
  • Writing formally, especially in academic or journalistic contexts
  1. The company is said to be developing a new smartphone. (present situation)
  2. The treatment is believed to reduce symptoms in most patients. (general fact)
  3. The manuscript is thought to date back to the 14th century. (present state)

Native speaker note: These structures are much more common in written English, especially in newspapers and academic writing, than in casual conversation.

Passive Reporting with Past Reference

Form: subject + passive reporting verb + to have + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use this structure when:

  • Reporting something that happened in the past
  • Distancing yourself from the reliability of the information
  • Discussing historical events or past actions
  1. The politician is said to have accepted bribes from business leaders. (past action)
  2. The painting is believed to have been stolen during the war. (past passive)
  3. Several witnesses are reported to have seen the accident. (past experience)

Native speaker note: This structure allows you to report past events without committing to their truth. It's especially useful in journalism when facts haven't been fully verified.

Complex Passive Reporting Structures

Form for ongoing actions: subject + passive reporting verb + to be + verb-ing

Form for passive infinitives: subject + passive reporting verb + to be + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use these complex forms when:

  • Reporting current ongoing actions (to be verb-ing)
  • Reporting a passive situation or state (to be + past participle)
  • Discussing formal or academic topics
  1. The prime minister is reported to be considering a cabinet reshuffle. (ongoing action)
  2. The suspect is believed to be hiding somewhere in the mountains. (current situation)
  3. The ancient text is thought to be written in a previously unknown language. (passive state)

Native speaker note: These complex forms sound very formal and are typically found in news reports and academic writing rather than everyday speech.

Summary: Passive Reporting Verbs

Structure Form Time Reference Example
Present simple is/are + reported verb + to + infinitive Present facts or states He is said to live in Paris.
Perfect form is/are + reported verb + to have + past participle Past events She is believed to have left the country.
Continuous form is/are + reported verb + to be + verb-ing Current ongoing actions They are thought to be working on a solution.
Passive infinitive is/are + reported verb + to be + past participle Present passive situations The document is believed to be signed by the president.
Perfect passive is/are + reported verb + to have been + past participle Past passive actions The painting is thought to have been stolen.
Active equivalent People + reporting verb + that + subject + verb Varies with tense People say that he lives in Paris.
Modal + passive reporting Subject + may/might + be + reported verb + to + verb Uncertain information He may be thought to have valuable information.

Common Errors

1: Using "that" instead of infinitive

The company is expected that will increase profits.


The company is expected to increase profits. ‚úì

After passive reporting verbs, use the infinitive form (to + verb) not a "that" clause.

2: Wrong form for past reference

The suspect is believed to steal the painting last month.


The suspect is believed to have stolen the painting last month. ‚úì

For past actions, use "to have + past participle (V3)" after the reporting verb.

3: Incorrect passive structure in complex forms

The treatment is thought to been working well.


The treatment is thought to be working well. ‚úì

Remember to use "to be + verb-ing" for ongoing actions, not "to been".

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Passive reporting structures are excellent for academic writing and formal reports when you want to distance yourself from the information or when the source isn't important.

üìù TIP 2: To remember the structure, think of the pattern: subject + is/are + reporting verb + to + verb form. The verb form changes depending on whether you're talking about present (to be), past (to have been), or ongoing actions (to be verb-ing).

üìù TIP 3: Practice identifying these structures in newspaper articles, academic journals, and formal reports, as they appear frequently in these types of texts.
