Adverb Position

Introduction

Adverbs add important information to sentences, such as how, when, where, or how often something happens. However, the position of an adverb in a sentence can change the meaning or make the sentence sound unnatural. In English, adverbs can go in three main positions: front, mid, or end. Knowing where to place an adverb correctly is essential for clear and natural-sounding English.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify and use the three main adverb positions in English sentences.
  • Choose the correct position for different types of adverbs.
  • Use front-position adverbs to connect ideas and set the scene in writing and speech.

Front Position

Form: adverb + comma + subject + verb

When to use it: Front position means placing the adverb at the beginning of the sentence, usually followed by a comma. This position is common for certain types of adverbs.

Key rules:

  • Connecting adverbs (linking ideas between sentences) usually go in front position. These include words like however, therefore, furthermore, nevertheless, and meanwhile.
  • Time adverbs often go in front position to set the scene. These include words like yesterday, last week, in the morning, and recently.
  • Comment adverbs (giving the speaker's opinion) can also go in front position. These include words like unfortunately, surprisingly, and clearly. (These are covered in detail on the Comment Adverbs page.)
  1. Unfortunately, the flight was delayed by three hours.
    (= the speaker feels this is bad news.)
  2. Yesterday, we had an important meeting with the new client.
    (= sets the time for the event.)
  3. However, the hotel was much better than we expected.
    (= contrasts with a previous idea.)

Native speaker note: In formal writing, front-position connecting adverbs (like however and therefore) are very common. In casual speech, people often use simpler words like but and so instead.

Mid Position

Form: subject + (auxiliary verb) + adverb + main verb

When to use it: Mid position means placing the adverb between the subject and the main verb, or after the first auxiliary verb. This is the most common position for several important types of adverbs.

Key rules:

  • Frequency adverbs (how often) almost always go in mid position. These include always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never, and seldom.
  • Certainty adverbs (how sure) often go in mid position. These include probably, certainly, definitely, and clearly.
  • Degree adverbs that modify verbs often go in mid position. These include almost, nearly, hardly, completely, and just.
  • With the verb be (used alone, not as an auxiliary), the adverb goes after be, not before it.
  • With auxiliary verbs, the adverb goes after the first auxiliary.
  1. She always arrives at the office before 9 a.m.
    (= frequency ‚Äî every day without exception.)
  2. They have probably forgotten about the reservation.
    (= certainty ‚Äî it is likely.)
  3. I nearly missed the train to the airport.
    (= degree ‚Äî it almost happened.)

Native speaker note: With the verb be used alone, the adverb comes after it: "She is always on time," not "She always is on time." This is a common mistake to watch out for.

End Position

Form: subject + verb + (object) + adverb

When to use it: End position means placing the adverb at the end of the sentence or clause, after the verb and its object (if there is one). This is the most common position for manner adverbs and place adverbs.

Key rules:

  • Manner adverbs (how something is done) usually go in end position. These include quickly, carefully, well, badly, and loudly.
  • Place adverbs (where) go in end position. These include here, there, everywhere, abroad, and outside.
  • Time adverbs can also go in end position. This is more common in informal speech and everyday conversation.
  • If there is an object, the adverb goes after the object, not between the verb and the object.
  1. She completed the report quickly.
    (= manner ‚Äî describes how she completed it.)
  2. We looked for a pharmacy everywhere.
    (= place ‚Äî describes where we looked.)
  3. He called the hotel yesterday.
    (= time ‚Äî describes when he called.)

Native speaker note: Placing a manner adverb between the verb and object sounds unnatural in most cases. Say "She speaks English well," not "She speaks well English."

Adverbs with Fixed or Restricted Positions

When to use it: Some adverbs have fixed or restricted positions. They sound wrong or change meaning if placed in a different position.

Key rules:

  • Still goes in mid position (before the main verb or after be). It does not normally go at the end.
  • Yet goes at the end of negative sentences and questions.
  • Already goes in mid position in British English. It can also go at the end for emphasis.
  • Enough goes after the adjective or adverb it modifies, not before it.
  • Also goes in mid position. Avoid placing it at the start of a sentence in formal writing.
  1. She still hasn't replied to my email.
    (= still goes before the main verb.)
  2. Have you booked the flights yet?
    (= yet goes at the end of a question.)
  3. He has already finished the presentation.
    (= already in mid position in British English.)

Key Differences

Choosing the correct adverb position depends on the type of adverb. Here is a quick summary:

  • Front position is for connecting adverbs and time-setting adverbs.
  • Mid position is for frequency, certainty, and degree adverbs.
  • End position is for manner and place adverbs.

Compare these examples:

  • Unfortunately, he lost his passport.
    (= front ‚Äî comment adverb giving the speaker's attitude.)
  • He probably lost his passport.
    (= mid ‚Äî certainty adverb showing likelihood.)
  • He lost his passport yesterday.
    (= end ‚Äî time adverb in casual speech.)

Summary: Adverb Position

Adverb Type Position Rule Example
Connecting adverbs (however, therefore) Front Start of sentence + comma However, the plan changed.
Time adverbs (yesterday, recently) Front or end Front to set the scene, end in casual speech Yesterday, we arrived. / We arrived yesterday.
Frequency adverbs (always, never) Mid Before the main verb or after be She always checks her emails.
Certainty adverbs (probably, definitely) Mid After the first auxiliary verb They will probably agree.
Manner adverbs (quickly, carefully) End After the verb and object He finished the task quickly.
Place adverbs (here, everywhere) End After the verb and object We looked everywhere.
Still Mid (fixed) Before the main verb or after be She is still working.
Yet End (fixed) End of negatives and questions Have you finished yet?

Common Errors

1: Placing a manner adverb between the verb and object

‚ùå She speaks fluently English.


‚úì She speaks English fluently.

Manner adverbs go after the object, not between the verb and the object. This is one of the most common word-order mistakes for English learners.

2: Putting a frequency adverb after the main verb instead of before it

‚ùå She goes always to the gym on Mondays.


‚úì She always goes to the gym on Mondays.

Frequency adverbs go before the main verb. The exception is the verb be, where the adverb goes after it.

3: Placing still at the end of the sentence

‚ùå He hasn't called me still.


‚úì He still hasn't called me.

Still goes in mid position ‚Äî before the main verb or the auxiliary. It does not go at the end of the sentence. Use yet at the end of negative sentences instead.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Remember the shortcut F-M-E: Front for connecting and scene-setting adverbs, Mid for frequency and certainty adverbs, End for manner and place adverbs. This simple pattern covers most adverb position decisions you need to make.

üìå TIP 2: When in doubt about where to put an adverb, try reading the sentence aloud in different positions. Native speakers often rely on what "sounds right," and with practice, you will develop the same instinct. Pay attention to adverb position when you read English articles or listen to podcasts.

üìå TIP 3: Keep a notebook of adverbs you learn and note which position they use. Group them by position (front, mid, end) rather than by meaning. Over time, you will build a mental map of which adverbs go where.
