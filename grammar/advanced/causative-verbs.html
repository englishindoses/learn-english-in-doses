Causative Verbs: Make, Let, Have, Get, Help

Introduction

Causative verbs are used when one person causes another person to do something. In English, the main causative verbs are make, let, have, get, and help. Each of these verbs has a different meaning and follows a different grammatical pattern. Understanding these differences will help you express ideas about permission, obligation, requests, and assistance more precisely.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the correct structure after each causative verb (make, let, have, get, help).
  • Choose the right causative verb based on the meaning you want to express.
  • Avoid common mistakes that ESL learners make with causative structures.

Make

Meaning: To force or require someone to do something. The person has no choice.

Form: make + person + bare infinitive (infinitive without "to")

  1. The manager made the team rewrite the entire report.
    (= The team had to rewrite it. They had no choice.)
  2. Airport security made me remove my shoes and belt.
    (= I was required to remove them.)
  3. The company policy makes all employees attend safety training.
    (= Everyone must attend. It's required.)

Important: In the passive form, "make" changes to use "to + infinitive": "The team was made to rewrite the report." Don't forget the "to" in passive sentences.

Let

Meaning: To allow or permit someone to do something. The person has permission.

Form: let + person + bare infinitive (infinitive without "to")

  1. My boss let me leave early for my appointment.
    (= My boss gave me permission to leave.)
  2. The hotel let us check in before the official time.
    (= The hotel allowed us to check in early.)
  3. Please let me explain my proposal.
    (= Please allow me to explain.)

Important: "Let" cannot be used in the passive voice. Instead, we use "be allowed to": "I was allowed to leave early" (NOT "I was let leave early").

Have

Meaning: To arrange for someone to do something (often a service or task). There is no force - it is usually a neutral request or arrangement.

Form: have + person + bare infinitive (infinitive without "to")

  1. I'll have my assistant book the conference room.
    (= I'll arrange for my assistant to book it.)
  2. She had the IT department install the new software.
    (= She arranged for them to install it.)
  3. We'll have reception forward the documents to your office.
    (= We'll arrange for reception to forward them.)

Native speaker note: "Have" is also used in the structure "have something done" (e.g., "I had my car serviced"). This is a passive causative structure and is covered in the Advanced Passive Constructions with Get and Have lesson.

Get

Meaning: To persuade or convince someone to do something. It often suggests that some effort or persuasion was needed.

Form: get + person + to-infinitive

  1. I finally got the supplier to reduce the price.
    (= I persuaded them. It took some negotiation.)
  2. She got her manager to approve the budget.
    (= She convinced her manager.)
  3. How did you get the client to sign the contract?
    (= How did you persuade them?)

Native speaker note: In formal English, "have" and "get" have distinct meanings: "have" is for neutral arrangements, while "get" implies persuasion or effort.

However, in informal spoken English, native speakers often use "get" in place of "have" even when no persuasion is involved (e.g., "I'll get my assistant to send it" instead of "I'll have my assistant send it").

In professional or formal contexts, it's best to distinguish between the two.

Help

Meaning: To assist someone in doing something.

Form: help + person + bare infinitive OR to-infinitive (both are correct)

  1. My colleague helped me prepare the presentation. ‚úì
    My colleague helped me to prepare the presentation. ‚úì
    (= Both forms are correct.)
  2. The consultant helped us identify the problem. ‚úì
    The consultant helped us to identify the problem. ‚úì
    (= Both forms are correct.)
  3. Can you help me carry these boxes to the meeting room? ‚úì
    Can you help me to carry these boxes to the meeting room? ‚úì
    (= Both forms are correct.)

Native speaker note: Both forms are grammatically correct, but the bare infinitive (without "to") is more common in everyday spoken English.

Key Differences: Choosing the Right Verb

Here is a quick guide to help you choose the right causative verb:

  • Make = force (no choice) ‚Üí "The delay made us miss the connecting flight."
  • Let = allow (give permission) ‚Üí "The airline let us change our seats."
  • Have = arrange (neutral) ‚Üí "I'll have my secretary reschedule the meeting."
  • Get = persuade (effort needed) ‚Üí "We got the landlord to fix the heating."
  • Help = assist ‚Üí "She helped me set up the new equipment."

Summary: Causative Verbs

Verb Meaning Structure Example
Make Force / require (no choice) make + person + bare infinitive The airline made us wait for hours.
Let Allow / permit let + person + bare infinitive They let us use the meeting room.
Have Arrange (neutral) have + person + bare infinitive I had my assistant book the flights.
Get Persuade / convince get + person + to-infinitive I got the supplier to lower the price.
Help Assist help + person + (to) + infinitive She helped me (to) finish the report.

Common Errors

1: Adding "to" after make, let, or have

‚ùå The manager made us to stay late.


‚úì The manager made us stay late.

After make, let, and have, we use the bare infinitive (without "to"). Only "get" uses the to-infinitive.

2: Forgetting "to" after get

‚ùå I got the client sign the contract.


‚úì I got the client to sign the contract.

"Get" is different from the other causative verbs. It always needs "to" before the infinitive.

3: Forgetting "to" in passive sentences with "make"

‚ùå All staff were made attend the training.


‚úì All staff were made to attend the training.

In the passive form of "make", we add "to" before the infinitive. This is different from active sentences.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Remember this pattern: make, let, have = bare infinitive. Get = to-infinitive. Help = both are OK.

üìå TIP 2: Think about the meaning: Did you force them? (make) Did you allow them? (let) Did you persuade them? (get) Did you arrange it? (have) Did you assist them? (help)

üìå TIP 3: For passive forms, remember: "make" changes to include "to" (was made to do), and "let" cannot be used in the passive - use "be allowed to" instead.
