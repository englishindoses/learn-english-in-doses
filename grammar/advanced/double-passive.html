Double Passive Constructions

Introduction

Double passive constructions occur when two passive structures are used together in the same sentence. These advanced grammatical forms are used in formal and academic writing to create complex and sophisticated expressions. While not common in everyday conversation, understanding double passives will help you comprehend academic texts and express complex ideas in formal writing.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Recognise and understand double passive constructions
  • Form double passives using different combinations of passive structures
  • Use double passives appropriately in formal and academic contexts

What Are Double Passive Constructions?

Form: There are several possible combinations:

  1. Passive reporting verb + passive infinitive
  2. Passive voice + passive relative clause
  3. Passive causative + passive main verb

When to use it: Use double passive constructions when:

  • Writing in a very formal or academic style
  • Needing to emphasise the action and result, not the agent
  • Creating complex sentences where both actions are in passive voice

Native speaker note: Double passives are rare in conversation but common in academic papers, legal documents, and formal reports. They often sound stilted if used in everyday speech.

Passive Reporting Verb + Passive Infinitive

Form: subject + is/are + reporting verb in past participle (V3) + to be + past participle (V3)

Common reporting verbs: said, thought, believed, considered, reported, known, understood, expected

  1. The manuscript is believed to have been written in the 15th century. (Passive reporting + passive perfect infinitive)
  2. The bridge is expected to be completed by next year. (Passive reporting + passive infinitive)
  3. The suspect is reported to have been seen near the crime scene. (Passive reporting + passive perfect infinitive)

Native speaker note: This is one of the most common types of double passive, especially in news reports and academic writing.

Passive Voice + Passive Relative Clause

Form: subject + be + past participle (V3) + relative pronoun + be + past participle (V3)

  1. The problem was solved using a method that was developed by researchers at Oxford. (Main clause passive + relative clause passive)
  2. Many applications are rejected which are submitted after the deadline. (Main clause passive + relative clause passive)
  3. The building was renovated using materials that were sourced locally. (Main clause passive + relative clause passive)

Native speaker note: This structure is common in technical and academic writing where multiple processes need to be described.

Other Complex Passive Combinations

Passive causative + passive infinitive: subject + have/get + object + past participle (V3) + to be + past participle (V3)

  1. The company had its products certified to be sold in international markets. (Causative passive + infinitive passive)
  2. We got our proposal approved to be implemented next quarter. (Causative passive + infinitive passive)
  3. She had her thesis reviewed to be submitted to the journal. (Causative passive + infinitive passive)

Passive modal + passive infinitive:

  • The documents must be signed to be processed further.
  • The application should be completed to be considered for the position.

Passive with perfect infinitive:

  • The policy appears to have been implemented without proper consultation.
  • The error seems to have been caused by a software malfunction.

Native speaker note: These complex forms are primarily used in writing, especially in business, legal, and academic contexts. They help create precise expressions when multiple passive actions are related.

Summary: Double Passive Constructions

Double Passive Type Structure Common Context Example
Passive reporting + passive infinitive is/are + V3 + to be + V3 News reports, research The technology is believed to be developed in Japan.
Passive reporting + passive perfect is/are + V3 + to have been + V3 Historical reports The painting is thought to have been stolen during the war.
Passive + passive relative clause is/was + V3 + that/which + is/was + V3 Technical descriptions The patient was treated with medicine that was developed recently.
Passive causative + passive infinitive had/got + object + V3 + to be + V3 Business processes They had their products certified to be sold internationally.
Passive modal + passive infinitive modal + be + V3 + to be + V3 Instructions, requirements The form must be signed to be processed.
Passive with -ing + passive passive + being + V3 Academic writing Having been informed of the changes, the staff was prepared.
Passive perfect + passive has/have been + V3 + to be + V3 Formal reporting The project has been designed to be completed in phases.

Common Errors

1: Overusing passive constructions

The report is expected to be completed to be submitted by Friday.


The report is expected to be completed and submitted by Friday. ‚úì

While double passives are grammatically correct, sometimes they create unnecessarily complex sentences. Simplify when possible.

2: Unclear agency in complex passives

The equipment was reported to have been stolen to be sold illegally.


The equipment was reportedly stolen with the intention of selling it illegally. ‚úì

Multiple passive constructions can make it unclear who did what. If clarity is more important than formality, consider restructuring.

3: Inconsistent tense in double passives

The building is believed to be designed by a famous architect.


The building is believed to have been designed by a famous architect. ‚úì

With past events, use "to have been + past participle" rather than "to be + past participle".

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Use double passives sparingly and mainly in formal or academic writing. In everyday communication, simpler structures are usually more effective.

üìù TIP 2: When using double passives, pay special attention to the tense relationships between the two passive forms to ensure logical and grammatical consistency.

üìù TIP 3: If a double passive construction sounds awkward or is difficult to understand, try rewriting part of it in active voice. Good writing balances passive and active constructions for clarity and style.

1. Which sentence contains a double passive construction?

A. The book was written by a famous author.
B. The house is being painted right now.
C. The letter was sent to be delivered by courier.
D. The manager has approved your request.

A. The suspect is known to have left the country.
B. The suspect is believed to have been seen at the airport.
C. The suspect was arrested by the police.
D. The police are believed to have arrested the suspect.

3. Which sentence contains a passive voice with a passive relative clause?

A. The building that was destroyed in the fire has been rebuilt.
B. The building was designed by an architect who won many awards.
C. The building was designed using techniques that were developed in Japan.
D. The building that the famous architect designed was renovated last year.

4. In which context would a double passive construction be MOST appropriate?

A. Casual conversation with friends
B. A personal email to a family member
C. An academic research paper
D. A text message

5. Which sentence contains a passive causative with a passive infinitive?

A. She had her car repaired yesterday.
B. She got her article published in a journal.
C. She had her thesis reviewed to be submitted to the committee.
D. She was given an award for her research.

6. What's the main purpose of using double passive constructions?

A. To make sentences shorter and simpler
B. To create emphasis in conversational English

D. To avoid using past tense forms

7. Which sentence shows an ERROR in using double passive constructions?

A. The research is expected to be published next month.
B. The policy was implemented to be reviewed after six months.
C. The document is believed to been altered by someone.

B. are thought / present
