Double Passive Constructions

Introduction

Double passive constructions occur when two passive structures are used together in the same sentence. These advanced grammatical forms are used in formal and academic writing to create complex and sophisticated expressions. While not common in everyday conversation, understanding double passives will help you comprehend academic texts and express complex ideas in formal writing.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Recognise and understand double passive constructions.
  • Form double passives using different combinations of passive structures.
  • Use double passives appropriately in formal and academic contexts.

What Are Double Passive Constructions?

Form: There are several possible combinations:

  • Passive reporting verb + passive infinitive
    (The book is believed to be written by Shakespeare.)
  • Passive voice + passive relative clause
    (The medicine was tested using methods that were approved by scientists.)
  • Passive modal + passive infinitive (The form must be signed before it can be processed.)
  • Passive causative structures
    (The products were had tested before they were approved for sale.)

When to use it: Use double passive constructions when:

  • Writing in a very formal or academic style, including news reports, research papers, or legal documents.
  • Needing to emphasise the action and result, not the agent.
  • Creating complex sentences where both actions need passive voice.

Native speaker note: Double passives are rare in conversation but common in academic papers, news reports, and formal documents. They often sound too formal for everyday speech.

Passive Reporting Verb + Passive Infinitive

Note: This structure has been covered in detail in a previous lesson. For more information, see passive-reporting.html

Form 1: subject + is/are + reporting verb in past participle (V3) + to be + past participle (V3)
When to use: For present or future actions/states.

Form 2: subject + is/are + reporting verb in past participle (V3) + to have been + past participle (V3)
When to use: For past actions or completed actions.

Common reporting verbs: said, thought, believed, considered, reported, known, understood, expected

  1. The manuscript is believed to have been written in the 15th century. (people think someone wrote this manuscript long ago)
  2. The bridge is expected to be completed by next year. (people think the bridge will be finished in the future)
  3. The suspect is reported to have been seen near the crime scene. (witnesses say they saw the suspect there in the past)

Native speaker note: This is the most common type of double passive, especially in news reports and academic writing. Use the perfect infinitive (to have been + V3) for past events.

Passive Voice + Passive Relative Clause

Form: subject + be + past participle (V3) + relative pronoun + be + past participle (V3)

Remember that relative clauses can be reduced by leaving out the relative pronoun.

Form: subject + be + past participle (V3) + past participle (V3)

  1. The problem was solved using a method that was developed by researchers at Oxford. (someone solved the problem using Oxford's method)
  2. Applications that are submitted after the deadline are often rejected. (late applications usually get refused)
  3. The building was renovated using materials that were sourced locally. (someone renovated the building with local materials)

Native speaker note: This structure is common in technical and academic writing where multiple processes need to be described. The relative clause can come before or after the main clause.

Passive Modal + Passive Infinitive

Form: modal + be + past participle (V3) + before/so that + passive structure

  1. The documents must be signed before they can be processed further. (signing is required for processing to happen)
  2. The application should be completed so it can be considered for the position. (completion is necessary for consideration)
  3. The equipment needs to be tested before it is installed. (testing is required before installation)

Native speaker note: This structure is common in instructions and formal requirements where one action must happen before another can take place.

Passive Causative Structures

Note: Causative structures have been covered in a previous lesson. For more information, see causatives.html

What is a causative? A causative structure means you arrange for someone else to do something for you. With "have" or "get", you don't do the action yourself.

Form: subject + have/get + object + past participle (V3) + before/so + passive structure

How it becomes double passive: The causative part uses passive voice (have/get something done), and the result is also passive (something is done).

  1. The company had its products tested before they were approved for sale. (the company arranged testing, then authorities approved the products)
  2. We got our proposal reviewed so it could be submitted on time. (we arranged for someone to review it, then it could be officially submitted)
  3. She had her thesis examined before it was published. (she arranged for examination, then the journal published it)

Native speaker note: Pure double passive causatives (like "had something done to be done") are very rare and sound unnatural. Native speakers prefer the structures shown above with connecting words like "before" or "so that".

Summary: Double Passive Constructions

Double Passive Type Structure Common Context Example
Passive reporting + passive infinitive is/are + V3 + to be + V3 News reports, research The technology is believed to have been developed in Japan.
Passive reporting + passive perfect is/are + V3 + to have been + V3 Historical reports The painting is thought to have been stolen during the war.
Passive + passive relative clause is/was + V3 + that/which + is/was + V3 Technical descriptions The medicine that was developed recently was used to treat the patient.
Passive modal + passive modal + be + V3 + before/so + passive Instructions, requirements The form must be signed before it can be processed.
Passive causative + passive had/got + object + V3 + before/so + passive Business processes They had their products tested before they were sold internationally.

Common Errors

1: Connecting two passives incorrectly

The report is expected to be completed to be submitted by Friday.


The report is expected to be completed and submitted by Friday. ‚úì

When two passive actions happen together, use "and" rather than "to be" twice.

2: Wrong tense in passive reporting

The building is believed to be designed by a famous architect.


The building is believed to have been designed by a famous architect. ‚úì

With past events, use "to have been + past participle (V3)" rather than "to be + past participle (V3)".

3: Using unnatural causative double passives

We had our products certified to be sold internationally.


We had our products certified so they could be sold internationally. ‚úì

Use connecting words like "so that" or "before" rather than stacking infinitives.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Use double passives sparingly and mainly in formal or academic writing. In everyday communication, simpler structures are usually more effective.

üìù TIP 2: When using double passives, pay special attention to the tense relationships between the two passive forms to ensure logical and grammatical consistency.

üìù TIP 3: If a double passive construction sounds awkward or is difficult to understand, try using connecting words like "before", "so that", or "after" to make the relationship between the two actions clearer. In academic writing, passive voice is common, but clarity should always be the priority.
