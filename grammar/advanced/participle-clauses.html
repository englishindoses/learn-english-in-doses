Participle Clauses: Present and Past

Introduction

Participle clauses are shortened sentences that use a participle (verb-ing or past participle (V3)) instead of a full clause with a subject and finite verb. They allow us to combine two pieces of information efficiently and elegantly. You will find participle clauses in both written and spoken English, though they are more common in writing and formal speech. In casual conversation, speakers often prefer full clauses with words like "when," "while," or "because." Understanding participle clauses will help you write more concisely and sound more sophisticated when speaking in formal situations.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use present participle clauses (verb-ing) to describe actions and give reasons.
  • Use past participle (V3) clauses to express passive meaning and states.
  • Choose between present and past participle clauses based on whether the subject is active or passive.

Present Participle Clauses (verb-ing)

Form: verb-ing + rest of clause, + main clause

Or: main clause, + verb-ing + rest of clause

When to use it: Use present participle clauses when the subject of both clauses is the same and the subject is doing the action (active meaning).

Actions at the same time:

  1. Walking to work, I listened to a podcast.
    (I walked and listened at the same time)
  2. She sat at her desk, checking her emails.
    (She sat and checked at the same time)
  3. He waited for the train, reading the news on his phone.
    (He waited and read at the same time)

One action before another:

  1. Turning the corner, I bumped into an old friend.
    (First I turned the corner, then I bumped into someone)
  2. Finishing her presentation, she answered questions from the audience.
    (First she finished, then she answered)
  3. Putting on his coat, he headed out the door.
    (First he put on his coat, then he headed out)

Giving a reason:

  1. Feeling tired, I went to bed early.
    (= Because I felt tired, I went to bed early)
  2. Not wanting to be late, we took a taxi.
    (= Because we didn't want to be late, we took a taxi)
  3. Knowing the area well, she offered to show us around.
    (= Because she knew the area well, she offered)

Showing a result:

  1. The storm damaged the roof, causing a leak.
    (The damage resulted in a leak)
  2. He missed his flight, ruining his holiday plans.
    (Missing the flight resulted in ruined plans)
  3. She got the promotion, making her the youngest manager in the company.
    (The promotion resulted in her becoming the youngest manager)

Native speaker note: Of these uses, "giving a reason" and "actions at the same time" are the most common in everyday speech. You might hear someone say "Not knowing what to do, I just waited" or "I sat there thinking about it." The "result" structure (e.g., "causing a leak") is more typical of news reports and formal writing.

Past Participle Clauses (V3)

Form: past participle (V3) + rest of clause, + main clause

When to use it: Use past participle (V3) clauses when the subject receives the action or is in a particular state (passive meaning). The participle clause replaces a passive construction.

Passive meaning:

  1. Built in 1900, the house is still in excellent condition.
    (= The house was built in 1900)
  2. Written in French, the novel was difficult for me to read.
    (= The novel was written in French)
  3. Made from recycled materials, these bags are environmentally friendly.
    (= The bags are made from recycled materials)

Describing a state or condition:

  1. Exhausted from the journey, we went straight to sleep.
    (= We were exhausted from the journey)
  2. Impressed by her CV, they invited her for an interview.
    (= They were impressed by her CV)
  3. Disappointed by the result, the team left quietly.
    (= The team was disappointed by the result)

Native speaker note: Past participle clauses describing feelings (e.g., "Exhausted from work...", "Surprised by the news...") are fairly common in speech. However, passive descriptions like "Built in 1900..." or "Written in French..." are much more typical of written English ‚Äî guidebooks, news articles, and formal descriptions.

Choosing Between Present and Past Participles

The key difference between present and past participle clauses is whether the subject is active (doing the action) or passive (receiving the action or in a state).

Compare these pairs:

  1. Surprising everyone, she won the competition.
    (She surprised everyone ‚Äî she did the action = active = verb-ing)
  2. Surprised by the news, she didn't know what to say.
    (She was surprised ‚Äî she received the feeling = passive = V3)

  1. Boring the audience, the speaker continued for another hour.
    (The speaker bored the audience = active = verb-ing)
  2. Bored by the lecture, the students started checking their phones.
    (The students were bored = passive = V3)

  1. Confusing everyone, he changed the plan at the last minute.
    (He confused everyone = active = verb-ing)
  2. Confused by the instructions, she asked for help.
    (She was confused = passive = V3)

Quick test: Ask yourself: "Is the subject doing the action, or is the action happening to the subject?"

  • Subject does the action ‚Üí use verb-ing
  • Action happens to the subject ‚Üí use past participle (V3)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: The subject of the participle clause must be the same as the subject of the main clause. Compare: ‚úì "Walking home, I saw my old teacher" (I was walking, I saw) vs. ‚úó "Walking home, the rain started" (the rain wasn't walking!). If the subjects are different, use a full clause: "While I was walking home, the rain started."

Summary: Participle Clauses

Type Form Meaning Example
Present Participle verb-ing + clause Active (subject does action) Feeling tired, I went home early.
Past Participle past participle (V3) + clause Passive (subject receives action/state) Exhausted, I went home early.
Present (simultaneous) verb-ing + clause Two actions at the same time She worked, listening to music.
Present (result) main clause, + verb-ing One action causes another He fell, breaking his arm.
Past (passive description) V3 + clause Background information (passive) Built in 1850, the church is beautiful.

Common Errors

1: Using a participle clause when the subjects are different (dangling participle)

‚ùå Walking to work, my phone rang.


‚úì Walking to work, I heard my phone ring.

‚úì While I was walking to work, my phone rang.

The subject of the participle clause must match the subject of the main clause. In the incorrect sentence, "my phone" appears to be walking!

2: Confusing present and past participles with -ed/-ing adjectives

‚ùå Exciting about the trip, she couldn't sleep.


‚úì Excited about the trip, she couldn't sleep.

Use the past participle (V3) when the subject experiences a feeling or state. "Exciting" describes what causes the feeling; "excited" describes who feels it.

3: Using present participle when past participle is needed

‚ùå Building in the 18th century, the palace attracts many tourists.


‚úì Built in the 18th century, the palace attracts many tourists.

The palace didn't build anything ‚Äî it was built. Use the past participle (V3) for passive meaning.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Always check: "Who or what is doing the action?" If the subject is doing it, use verb-ing. If the subject receives it or is in a state, use the past participle (V3).

üìå TIP 2: Before using a participle clause, identify the subject of your main clause. Then ask: "Is this the same person/thing doing or experiencing the participle action?" If not, use a full clause with "when," "while," or "because" instead.

üìå TIP 3: In casual conversation, full sentences often sound more natural: "Because I was tired, I left early" rather than "Being tired, I left early." Save participle clauses for writing or more formal speaking situations.
