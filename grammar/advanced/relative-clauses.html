Relative Clauses

Introduction

Relative clauses help us give more information about a noun. They are like adding extra details to your sentence. We use them to make our English more precise and to join two sentences together in a more elegant way.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Understand and use both defining and non-defining relative clauses
  • Choose and use the correct relative pronouns (who, which, that, etc.)
  • Form relative clauses with prepositions correctly
  • Create reduced relative clauses to make your English more concise

Defining Relative Clauses

Structure: We place the relative clause directly after the noun it describes, without commas, using relative pronouns (who, which, that, whose, whom, where, when)

When to use it: A defining relative clause gives essential information about a noun. This information is necessary to understand which person or thing we are talking about.

  1. The woman who lives next door is a doctor. (Specifies which woman)
  2. The book that I bought yesterday is very interesting. (Specifies which book)
  3. The city where I was born has changed a lot. (Specifies which city)

Relative Pronouns in Defining Clauses

Pronoun Used for Example
who people The man who called you is my brother.
which things, animals The car which I rented broke down.
that people or things The film that we saw was boring.
whose possession The boy whose bike was stolen is crying.
where places The house where I grew up is now a shop.
when times The day when we met was rainy.

Native speaker note: In everyday conversation, native speakers often use "that" for both people and things, especially in defining relative clauses. However, in more formal writing, they tend to use "who" for people and "which" for things.

Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Structure: We place the relative clause directly after the noun it describes, always using commas, and never using 'that'

When to use it: A non-defining relative clause gives extra information about a noun. This information is not essential - we could remove it and still understand which person or thing is being talked about.

  1. My sister, who lives in London, is visiting me next week.
  2. The book, which I bought yesterday, is on the table.
  3. Paris, where I studied for a year, is beautiful in spring.
Pronoun Used for Example
who people My teacher, who speaks five languages, is from Italy.
which things, animals My car, which I bought last year, is very reliable.
whose possession My friend, whose mother is a famous actress, is very modest.
where places Paris, where I studied for a year, is beautiful in spring.
when times 2010, when we first met, was a wonderful year.

Relative Clauses with Prepositions

Sometimes we need to use prepositions in relative clauses. There are two ways to do this:

1. Preposition at the end (informal, more common in speaking)

  1. The man who I was talking to is my cousin.
  2. The chair which she was sitting on broke.
  3. The company which I work for is international.

2. Preposition before the relative pronoun (formal, more common in writing)

  1. The man to whom I was talking is my cousin.
  2. The chair on which she was sitting broke.
  3. The company for which I work is international.

Native speaker note: In casual conversation, native speakers almost always put the preposition at the end of the clause. The formal structure with preposition + whom/which sounds very formal and is mainly used in academic or legal writing.

Reduced Relative Clauses

When to use it: We can shorten (reduce) relative clauses in certain situations, especially in writing, to make our language more concise.

Active Reduced Relative Clauses

Full relative clause: The man who is standing at the bus stop is my neighbor.
Reduced relative clause: The man standing at the bus stop is my neighbor.

Full relative clause: The children who are playing in the garden are very noisy.
Reduced relative clause: The children playing in the garden are very noisy.

Passive Reduced Relative Clauses

Full relative clause: The book which was written by Mark Twain is a classic.
Reduced relative clause: The book written by Mark Twain is a classic.

Full relative clause: The car that was damaged in the accident was repaired.
Reduced relative clause: The car damaged in the accident was repaired.

Common Errors

1: Using 'that' in non-defining clauses

My brother, that lives in Australia, is visiting next month. ‚úó

Never use 'that' in non-defining relative clauses. Use 'who' for people and 'which' for things instead.

2: Forgetting commas in non-defining clauses

My house which was built in 1980 needs a new roof. ‚úó

My house, which was built in 1980, needs a new roof. ‚úì

Always use commas around non-defining relative clauses.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Remember: defining clauses = essential information (no commas), non-defining clauses = extra information (with commas)

üìù TIP 2: Use 'who' for people, 'which' for things, and 'that' for both (but only in defining clauses)

üìù TIP 3: With prepositions, informal speech usually places them at the end, while formal writing often places them before the relative pronoun
