Relative Clauses

Introduction

Relative clauses are parts of a sentence that provide additional information about a noun. They are introduced by relative pronouns like who, which, that, whose, and whom. In advanced English, mastering the different types of relative clauses and knowing when to use them allows you to create more complex and precise sentences.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between defining and non-defining relative clauses and use them correctly.
  • Use relative clauses with prepositions in both formal and informal contexts.
  • Create reduced relative clauses to make your speech and writing more concise.

Defining Relative Clauses

Form: noun + who/which/that/whose/whom + subject/verb + rest of clause

When to use it: Use defining relative clauses to provide essential information about a noun. This information is necessary to identify exactly which person or thing you are talking about. Defining relative clauses are not separated by commas.

  1. The woman who lives next door is a doctor. (Identifies which woman we're talking about)
  2. The book that I bought yesterday is very interesting. (Identifies which book)
  3. The car whose engine failed had to be towed away. (Identifies which car)

Omitting Relative Pronouns

Form for object pronouns: noun + subject + verb + rest of clause (no relative pronoun)

Form for subject pronouns: noun + who/which/that + verb + rest of clause (pronoun required)

When to use it: You can omit the relative pronoun (who, which, that) in defining relative clauses, but only when the relative pronoun is the object of the verb in the relative clause, not when it's the subject.

When the relative pronoun is the object (can be omitted):

  1. The book (that) I bought yesterday is interesting. (Object: I bought the book)
  2. The person (who/whom/that) we met at the conference works for Google. (Object: we met the person)
  3. The movie (which/that) they recommended was excellent. (Object: they recommended the movie)

When the relative pronoun is the subject (cannot be omitted):

  1. The woman who/that lives next door is a doctor. (Subject: the woman lives next door)
  2. The car which/that broke down has been repaired. (Subject: the car broke down)
  3. The students who/that passed the exam received certificates. (Subject: the students passed the exam)

Native speaker note: In conversational English, native speakers almost always drop the relative pronoun when it's the object, especially in informal situations. This omission makes speech sound more natural and fluent. In writing, especially formal writing, the pronoun is more often retained.

Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Form: noun + , + relative pronoun + subject/verb + rest of clause + , + rest of the sentence

When to use it: Use non-defining relative clauses to add extra information about a noun that is already identified. This information is not essential for identifying the noun. Non-defining relative clauses are always separated by commas, and you cannot use 'that' as a relative pronoun.

  1. My sister, who lives in Paris, is visiting next week. (Extra information about "my sister" who is already identified)
  2. The Eiffel Tower, which was built in 1889, is the symbol of Paris. (Extra information about "the Eiffel Tower")
  3. Dr. Smith, whose research has been widely published, will give a lecture tomorrow. (Extra information about "Dr. Smith")
  4. My uncle, whom I admire greatly, is a retired lawyer. (Expressing admiration for my uncle)

Native speaker note: You might hear people use 'that' in non-defining clauses in casual speech, but it is considered incorrect in standard English and should be avoided in writing or exams.

Relative Clauses with Prepositions

Form (informal): noun + who/which/that + subject + verb + rest of clause + preposition

Form (formal): noun + preposition + whom/which + subject + verb + rest of clause

When to use it: Use these structures when the relationship between the noun and the relative clause involves a preposition (to, for, with, about, etc.). The first structure is more common in informal English, while the second is more formal.

Informal (ending with preposition):

  1. The person who I was talking to is my colleague. (To whom I was talking)
  2. The company that we work for is expanding. (For which we work)
  3. The issue which they are concerned about needs urgent attention. (About which they are concerned)

Formal (preposition before relative pronoun):

  1. The person to whom I was talking is my colleague.
  2. The company for which we work is expanding.
  3. The issue about which they are concerned needs urgent attention.

Native speaker note: Native speakers almost always use the informal structure with the preposition at the end. To make it less formal in casual speech, they use 'that' as the relative pronoun.
The formal structure with the preposition before the relative pronoun sounds very formal and is mainly used in academic writing, legal documents, or very formal speech.

Reduced Relative Clauses

Form: There are two main types of reduced relative clauses:

  • Active: noun + present participle (verb-ing)
    Used when the original clause is in the active voice.
    Example: The man talking to Sarah is my teacher.
  • Passive: noun + past participle (V3)
    Used when the original clause is in the passive voice.
    Example: The book written by Tolkien is very famous.

When to use it: Use reduced relative clauses to make your speech or writing more concise by omitting the relative pronoun and sometimes the verb "be". They can only be used with defining relative clauses, not with non-defining ones.

Original vs. Reduced Relative Clauses:

  1. Original: The woman who is standing by the window is my teacher.
    Reduced: The woman standing by the window is my teacher. (Active reduction: "who is" removed)
  2. Original: The document that was sent yesterday has arrived.
    Reduced: The document sent yesterday has arrived. (Passive reduction: "that was" removed)
  3. Original: The people who live in that house are very friendly.
    Reduced: The people living in that house are very friendly. (Active reduction: "who" removed and verb changed to "-ing" form)

Native speaker note: Reduced relative clauses are very common in both spoken and written English as they make communication more efficient. Native speakers often use them naturally without thinking about the grammar rules, especially with common verbs like "called," "named," "born," etc. For example, "The boy called John" instead of "The boy who is called John."

Summary: Relative Clauses

Type Form Function Example
Defining noun + who/which/that/whose/whom + subject/verb + rest of clause Essential information (no commas) The woman who works here is my aunt.
Object pronoun omission noun + subject + verb + rest of clause (no relative pronoun) More concise defining clause The book I bought is interesting.
Non-defining noun + , + who/which/whose/whom + subject/verb + rest of clause + , Extra information (with commas) My aunt, who works here, is retiring soon.
With preposition (informal) noun + who/which/that + subject + verb + rest of clause + preposition Relationship involving preposition The person who I spoke to was helpful.
With preposition (formal) noun + preposition + whom/which + subject + verb + rest of clause More formal expression The person to whom I spoke was helpful.
Reduced (active with be) noun + verb-ing + rest of clause More concise expression The girl standing there is my sister.
Reduced (passive) noun + past participle (V3) + rest of clause More concise expression The proposal submitted yesterday was accepted.

Common Errors

1: Using 'that' in non-defining relative clauses

My brother, that lives in London, is visiting next week.


My brother, who lives in London, is visiting next week. ‚úì

You cannot use 'that' in non-defining relative clauses. Use 'who' for people and 'which' for things instead.

2: Forgetting commas in non-defining relative clauses

Paris which is the capital of France attracts many tourists.


Paris, which is the capital of France, attracts many tourists. ‚úì

Non-defining relative clauses must be separated by commas from the rest of the sentence.

3: Using 'which' for people

The student which won the competition is from my class.


The student who/that won the competition is from my class. ‚úì

Use 'who' or 'that' for people, not 'which'. 'Which' is only for things.

4: Incorrect reduction of relative clauses

The man talked to my sister is my teacher.


The man talking to my sister is my teacher. ‚úì

When reducing active relative clauses, use the -ing form (talking), not the past tense (talked).

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: To determine whether to use a defining or non-defining relative clause, ask yourself: "Is this information necessary to identify which person/thing I'm talking about?" If yes, use a defining clause (no commas). If no, use a non-defining clause (with commas).

üìù TIP 2: How to tell subject from object: Look at what follows the relative pronoun:

  • If there's another subject (like I, you, he, she, they, we, or a name) after the relative pronoun, then the pronoun is the object and can be dropped. Example: "The book (that) I bought..." (I = subject)
  • If there's a verb directly after the relative pronoun with no intervening subject, then the pronoun is the subject and cannot be dropped. Example: "The woman who lives next door..." (lives = verb, no other subject)

üìù TIP 3: Practice reducing relative clauses to make your English more fluent and natural. Look for clauses with "who is/are" and "which is/are" as these are the easiest to reduce.
