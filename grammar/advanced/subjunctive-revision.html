Subjunctive Mood Revision

Introduction

Today we're going to review the subjunctive mood that we studied in our previous lessons. We'll practise the three main forms again: the were-subjunctive for unreal situations, the past perfect subjunctive for past regrets, and the present subjunctive for formal requests. This revision will help you feel more confident using these forms correctly.

After this revision lesson, you will be able to:

  • Review and identify the three types of subjunctive forms correctly.
  • Practise recognising key words that show which subjunctive to use.
  • Apply the correct subjunctive form confidently in different situations.

Were-Subjunctive (Hypothetical Present/Future)

Form: if/wish/as if + subject + were (for all subjects)

When to use it: Use "were" for all subjects when talking about hypothetical or unreal situations in the present or future. This form appears after if, wish, as if, as though, and suppose.

Common patterns:

  • If I/he/she/it were... (hypothetical conditionals)
  • I wish I/he/she/it were... (wishes about present)
  • He acts as if he were... (imaginary comparisons)
  1. If I were you, I would take that job.
  2. I wish the weather were better today.
  3. She speaks as if she were an expert in the field.

Native speaker note: In casual conversation, many native speakers say "was" with I, he, she, and it ("If I was you..."). For formal writing and exams, always use "were" for hypothetical situations.

Past Perfect Subjunctive (Hypothetical Past)

Form: if/wish/if only + subject + had + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use the past perfect subjunctive to talk about hypothetical or unreal situations in the past. This expresses regret or imagines how the past could have been different.

Common patterns:

  • If + subject + had + past participle (V3)... (past hypothetical conditionals)
  • I wish + subject + had + past participle (V3)... (regrets about the past)
  • If only + subject + had + past participle (V3)... (strong regrets)
  1. I wish I had studied harder last term.
  2. If only we had left earlier yesterday.
  3. If the committee had received the proposal earlier, they would have considered it.

Present Subjunctive (Formal Demands and Recommendations)

Form: that + subject + base verb (no -s, no auxiliary verbs)

When to use it: Use the present subjunctive after verbs of demand, recommendation, and suggestion, and after expressions of importance. This form is very formal and mainly used in business writing, academic English, and official contexts.

Common triggers:

  • After demand, suggest, recommend, insist, require
  • After "It is essential/vital/crucial/important that..."
  • After "It is necessary/imperative that..."
  1. The board demands that the CEO resign immediately.
  2. It is essential that all participants be present at the conference.
  3. The judge ordered that the defendant appear in court on Monday.

Native speaker note: The present subjunctive is very formal and mainly used in writing and official situations. In daily conversation, native speakers say "I suggest he's more careful" or avoid the structure entirely: "You need to be present" instead of "It is essential that you be present."

Summary: Three Types of Subjunctive

Type Form Time Reference Example
Were-subjunctive if/wish + subject + were Present/future (hypothetical) If I were rich, I would travel more.
Past perfect subjunctive if/wish + subject + had + V3 Past (unreal past) I wish I had known earlier.
Present subjunctive that + subject + base verb Present/future (formal demands) The court demands that he appear immediately.

Common Errors

1: Using "was" instead of "were" in hypothetical situations

‚úó If she was taller, she could reach the shelf.


‚úì If she were taller, she could reach the shelf.

Use "were" (not "was") for all subjects in hypothetical situations with if, wish, and as if.

2: Using present subjunctive in informal contexts

‚úó I suggest that he be more careful. (casual conversation)


‚úì I suggest that he is more careful. (casual conversation)

The present subjunctive is very formal. In everyday speech, use regular present tense or "should + base verb".

3: Confusing time references for past wishes

‚úó I wish I were rich when I was young. (mixing time references)


‚úì I wish I had been rich when I was young. (past regret)

For regrets about the past, use "had + past participle (V3)", not "were" or simple past.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Check the context: informal conversation = avoid present subjunctive; formal writing/business = use present subjunctive after demand/suggest verbs.

üìå TIP 2: Time markers help you choose: "right now/today" = were-subjunctive; "yesterday/last year" = past perfect subjunctive.

üìå TIP 3: Listen for trigger words: if/wish/as if = were or had + V3; demand/insist/essential = base verb (very formal contexts only).
