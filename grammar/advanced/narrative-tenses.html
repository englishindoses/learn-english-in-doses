Narrative Tenses

Introduction

Narrative tenses are past tenses used to tell stories, describe past events, and show the relationship between different actions in the past. Using these tenses correctly helps your listener or reader understand when events happened in relation to each other.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use past simple, past continuous, and past perfect correctly in storytelling.
  • Understand the sequence of events in a narrative by recognizing different past tenses.
  • Create more sophisticated narratives by combining different past tenses.

Past Simple

Form: Subject + past form of the verb (V2)

When to use it: Use the past simple for completed actions in the past. It's the most common narrative tense and forms the backbone of most stories.

  1. I arrived at the office at 9 am yesterday. (A specific completed action)
  2. She visited her grandmother every Sunday. (Regular past habit)
  3. We took three flights to reach our destination. (Sequence of completed actions)

Native speaker note: Native speakers often use the past simple for almost all past events, even when other past tenses might be more precise. It's the default tense for telling stories.

Past Continuous

Form: Subject + was/were + verb-ing

When to use it: Use the past continuous for actions that were in progress at a specific moment in the past or actions that were interrupted by another action (usually in past simple).

  1. I was working when my boss called. (Action in progress interrupted by another action)
  2. At 3 pm yesterday, she was studying for her exam. (Action in progress at a specific time)
  3. While we were travelling through Spain, we visited many beautiful cities. (Background action during which other actions occurred)

Native speaker note: The past continuous is often used to set the scene in stories: "It was raining heavily, and the wind was blowing the trees." This creates a vivid backdrop for the main events.

Past Perfect

Form: Subject + had + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use the past perfect for actions that happened before another action in the past. It shows the order of events clearly when you're not telling a story in chronological order.

  1. By the time I arrived, the meeting had already started. (The meeting started before I arrived)
  2. She was nervous because she had never flown before. (The lack of flying experience happened before the nervousness)
  3. When we reached the cinema, the film had finished. (The film finished before we reached the cinema)

Native speaker note: While the past perfect is important for clarity, native speakers sometimes replace it with past simple when the order of events is obvious from context or when time expressions like "already," "just," or "before" make the sequence clear.

Combining Narrative Tenses

When to combine tenses: To create a rich, clear narrative, you'll often need to use all three tenses together to show the relationship between events.

  1. I was reading (past continuous) a book when the phone rang (past simple). I had forgotten (past perfect) to turn off the sound.
  2. When Sarah arrived (past simple) at the party, John was dancing (past continuous) with someone else. She had hoped (past perfect) he would wait for her.
  3. The police discovered (past simple) that the thief had entered (past perfect) through the back door. An alarm was ringing (past continuous) when they arrived.

Native speaker note: When telling stories, native speakers often switch between tenses naturally to create drama or emphasize certain events. Past continuous is used for setting the scene, past simple for main events, and past perfect for explaining backstory.

Summary: Narrative Tenses

Tense Form Use Example
Past Simple Subject + past form of the verb (V2) Completed actions in the past She went to Paris last year.
Past Continuous Subject + was/were + verb-ing Actions in progress in the past I was sleeping when the phone rang.
Past Perfect Subject + had + perfect form of the verb (V3) Actions completed before another past action They had finished dinner when I arrived.

Common Errors

1: Confusing when to use past perfect vs. past simple

When I arrived at the station, the train left.


When I arrived at the station, the train had left. ‚úì

If the train left before your arrival, use past perfect to show this sequence clearly. The incorrect version suggests both actions happened at the same time.

2: Incorrect formation of past continuous

She was cook dinner when I called.


She was cooking dinner when I called. ‚úì

Past continuous requires the -ing form of the verb. Don't forget to add -ing after was/were.

3: Using past perfect when it's not necessary

I had arrived at work at 9 am, then I had started my computer, and I had checked my emails.


I arrived at work at 9 am, then I started my computer, and I checked my emails. ‚úì

When telling a story in chronological order, use past simple. Past perfect is only needed to show that an event happened before another past event.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Visualize a timeline to help decide which tense to use. Place all events on the timeline, then use past perfect for events that came before your main narrative point, past continuous for actions in progress, and past simple for completed actions.

üìù TIP 2: Use time markers to help clarify the sequence of events: "already," "just," "yet," "before," "after," "by the time," and "when" can help make the relationship between events clearer.

üìù TIP 3: Practice storytelling by rewriting simple news articles using all three narrative tenses. Start with the main event in past simple, add background in past continuous, and earlier events in past perfect.
