That-Clauses and If/Whether Clauses

Introduction

That-clauses and if/whether clauses are groups of words that work like nouns in sentences. They help you express opinions, report information, and talk about uncertainty. These clauses make your English sound more natural and formal.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use that-clauses to express facts, opinions, and reported speech in subject and object positions.
  • Form if/whether clauses to express uncertainty and indirect yes/no questions in different positions.
  • Use complement clauses after linking verbs to explain and describe subjects.

That-Clauses

Form: that + subject + verb

When to use it: Use that-clauses to express facts, opinions, beliefs, or to report what someone said.

Key rules:

  • Subject clause: Comes at the beginning, before the main verb - "that" cannot be dropped
  • Object clause: Comes after action verbs (think, say, know, believe) - "that" can be dropped

Subject clauses (cannot drop "that"):

  1. That she works weekends is surprising. (something is surprising)
  2. That he speaks five languages impressed the interviewer. (something impressed the interviewer)
  3. That the train was delayed caused problems. (something caused problems)

Object clauses ("that" is optional):

  1. I think (that) the price is too high. (I think something)
  2. He said (that) the meeting was cancelled. (he said something)
  3. We believe (that) everyone deserves a chance. (we believe something)

Native speaker note: That-clauses as subjects ("That she works weekends is surprising") are very formal and rare in everyday English. Native speakers typically use "it" instead: "It's surprising that she works weekends."

If/Whether Clauses

Form: if/whether + subject + verb

When to use it: Use if/whether clauses to express uncertainty, doubt, or to report yes/no questions.

Key rules:

  • Subject clause: Comes at the beginning, before the main verb - must use "whether" (not "if")
  • Object clause: Comes after verbs (ask, know, wonder) - both "if" and "whether" work

Subject clauses (use "whether", not "if"):

  1. Whether the shop is open depends on the day. (something depends on the day)
  2. Whether we should cancel the trip is unclear. (something is unclear)
  3. Whether she accepts the job remains to be seen. (something remains to be seen)

Object clauses (both "if" and "whether" work):

  1. I don't know if the meeting is cancelled. (I don't know something)
  2. She asked whether I could drive her home. (she asked something)
  3. We're deciding if we should move house. (we're deciding something)

Remember: Use "whether" (not "if") when the clause comes before the main verb (subject position). Both "if" and "whether" work when the clause comes after verbs (object position), but "whether" is more formal.

Complement Clauses

Form: linking verb + that/whether + subject + verb

When to use it: Use complement clauses after linking verbs (be, seem, appear) to explain or describe the subject. These clauses complete the meaning of the sentence.

Key rules:

  • Complement clause: Comes after linking verbs (be, seem, appear) - "that" is preferred in formal writing but can be dropped in informal speech, prefer "whether" over "if"
  1. The problem is that we need more staff. (formal - the problem = this)
  2. The question is whether we have enough money. (uncertainty - the question = this)
  3. The truth is (that) he was lying. (informal speech - the truth = this)

Remember: After linking verbs (be, seem, appear), "that" is preferred in formal writing and exams like IELTS, but can be dropped in informal speech. "Whether" is preferred over "if" for uncertainty.

Summary: That-Clauses and If/Whether Clauses

Position That-clauses If/Whether clauses Key Rules
Subject (before main verb) That he quit was shocking. Whether it rains doesn't matter. Keep "that"; use "whether" not "if"
Object (after action verbs) I think (that) she's right. I wonder if/whether she's coming. "That" optional; "if"/"whether" both work
Complement (after linking verbs) The truth is (that) we failed. The question is whether we should go. Prefer "that" (formal); prefer "whether" over "if"

Common Errors

1: Dropping "that" when the clause is the subject

She speaks three languages is impressive.


That she speaks three languages is impressive. ‚úì

When the that-clause is the subject, you cannot remove "that".

2: Using "if" when the clause is the subject

If we should cancel the trip is unclear.


Whether we should cancel the trip is unclear. ‚úì

When the if/whether clause is the subject, you must use "whether", not "if".

3: Using "if" instead of "whether" in complement clauses

The question is if we have enough budget.


The question is whether we have enough budget. ‚úì

In complement clauses after linking verbs, prefer "whether" over "if" when expressing uncertainty.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Use position to identify clause type: before the main verb = subject clause, after action verbs = object clause, after linking verbs = complement clause.

üìù TIP 2: Remember the "that" rule: always keep it in subject and complement positions, but you can drop it in object positions after verbs like think, say, believe.

üìù TIP 3: For if/whether choice: use "whether" when the clause is the subject or after linking verbs. Both work in object positions, but "whether" sounds more formal.
