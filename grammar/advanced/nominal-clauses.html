Nominal Clauses

Introduction

Nominal clauses (also called noun clauses) are dependent clauses that function as nouns in a sentence. They can act as subjects, objects, or complements, letting you report speech and thought, embed questions, and express complex ideas more naturally and professionally.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use that-, wh-, and if/whether clauses correctly in different sentence positions.
  • Choose appropriate structures for formal vs. informal contexts and know when that can/can’t be omitted.
  • Transform direct statements and questions into embedded (reported) nominal clauses.

That-Clauses

Form: that + subject + verb (statement word order)

When to use: Report facts, opinions, feelings, and beliefs. Common after verbs of thinking/speaking/feeling and after adjectives expressing certainty or emotion.

As object of a verb:

  1. I believe that the meeting will be productive.
  2. She mentioned that she had already finished the report.
  3. They discovered that the data was incorrect.

As subject (very formal/academic - not common):

  1. That he arrived late surprised everyone.
  2. That the project succeeded was due to teamwork.

After adjectives:

  1. I’m certain that we made the right decision.
  2. It’s obvious that she has worked hard.
  3. We’re pleased that you could join us.

Native speaker note: In object position, many speakers drop that (“I think (that) it’s a good idea”). Do not drop it when the clause is the subject or after certain nouns (e.g., the fact that…).

Omitting that

In many cases, the word that can be left out in everyday English — especially when the clause is the object of a verb. However, there are important cases where it must stay.

1) When that is optional

  • Common in conversation after verbs of thinking, saying, and feeling:
    ✔ I think (that) we should leave now.
    ✔ She said (that) she was tired.
  • More likely to keep that in careful or formal writing for clarity, especially in longer sentences:
    ✔ The manager emphasised that the new policy applies to everyone.

2) When that is required

  • When the clause is the subject:
    ✘ The project succeeded was due to teamwork.
    ✔ That the project succeeded was due to teamwork.
    ✔ It was due to teamwork that the project succeeded.
  • After certain nouns such as fact, idea, belief, possibility:
    ✘ The fact she won surprised everyone.
    ✔ The fact that she won surprised everyone.
  • To avoid confusion in complex sentences:
    ✔ He said that after the meeting, he’d send the report. (Without that, “after the meeting” might seem to modify “said” instead of the whole clause.)

3) Style and clarity tips

  • In informal speech, dropping that is normal and natural when it’s clear without it.
  • In formal writing (including professional emails), keeping that can make the sentence easier to read and reduce misinterpretation.
  • For learners, it’s safer to keep that until you’re very confident about when it can be omitted.

Wh-Clauses

Form: wh-word + subject + verb (statement word order, not question word order)

When to use: Embed information questions (what, where, when, why, who, how, which, whose) to express unknown or detailed information.

As object of a verb:

  1. I don’t know where she lives. (NOT: where does she live)
  2. Can you tell me what time the meeting starts?
  3. She explained why the project had failed.

As subject:

  1. What happened yesterday remains a mystery.
  2. How they solved the problem was impressive.

After prepositions:

  1. We’re discussing what we should do next.
  2. It depends on how much time we have.
  3. I’m interested in where you learned that skill.

Native speaker note: Think of wh-clauses as embedded statements. Say “I don’t know where he is,” not “I don’t know where is he.”

If/Whether Clauses

Form: if/whether + subject + verb (statement word order)

When to use: Embed yes/no questions or present alternatives. Whether is more formal and is required in certain positions.

Expressing uncertainty:

  1. I wonder if/whether she received my email.
  2. Do you know if/whether the shop is open today?
  3. They asked whether we had finished the project.

With “or not” (alternatives):

  1. I’m not sure whether he’s coming or not.
  2. The question is whether we should proceed or wait.

Cases where only whether is used:

  1. After prepositions: We’re thinking about whether to hire more staff.
  2. Before infinitives: I don’t know whether to laugh or cry.
  3. As subject: Whether he agrees doesn’t matter to me.

Native speaker note: In everyday speech, if is much more common than whether except in the specific cases above.

Where Nominal Clauses Fit in a Sentence

Nominal clauses can appear in three main positions:

  • Subject: What she said was true. / That he left early surprised us.
  • Object: He knows that I’m busy. I wonder where she is.
  • Complement (after linking verbs): The problem is that we’re late. The question is whether we should continue.

Style tip: Subject clauses can feel “top-heavy.” To make them lighter, use a placeholder it: “It surprised us that he left early.”

Other Noun-Like Structures (Not Full Finite Clauses)

These aren’t full finite clauses, but they also function as nouns. Keep them conceptually separate so learners don’t confuse them with the three core nominal clause types above.

1) Nominal -ing structures (gerund phrases)

Form: V-ing (+ objects/modifiers)

  1. Working late every night affects your health. (subject)
  2. She enjoys reading novels. (object)
  3. His hobby is collecting stamps. (complement)

2) Nominal infinitive structures

Form: to + base verb

  1. To arrive on time is important. (subject)
  2. She wants to study abroad. (object)
  3. His goal is to become a doctor. (complement)

Teaching tip: Present gerunds/infinitives as noun phrases that behave like nouns, while reserving “nominal clauses” for the that/wh/if-whether patterns above.

Summary: Nominal Clauses

Type Form Function Example
That-clause that + subject + verb Facts, opinions, beliefs I think (that) she’s right.
Wh-clause wh-word + subject + verb Embeds information questions I know where he works.
If/Whether-clause if/whether + subject + verb Embeds yes/no questions I wonder whether they’ll come.
Subject position Clause + verb Acts as sentence subject What she said was true.
Object position Verb + clause Acts as verb object He knows that I’m busy.
Complement position Be + clause Completes the meaning The problem is that we’re late.

Common Errors

1: Using question word order in embedded clauses

Can you tell me where is the station?


Can you tell me where the station is? ✓

Nominal clauses use statement word order (subject + verb), even when they start with a question word.

2: Using “if” where only “whether” is correct

I’m thinking about if to apply for the job.


I’m thinking about whether to apply for the job. ✓

Use whether after prepositions and before infinitives; if is not used in these positions.

3: Omitting “that” when it cannot be omitted

The fact she won surprised everyone.


The fact that she won surprised everyone. ✓

Keep that after certain nouns (e.g., the fact that) and when the clause is the subject.

Useful Tips

📝 TIP 1: Treat wh-clauses as embedded statements. Convert the original question to a statement to check word order: “Where does she live?” → “She lives in London” → I know where she lives.

📝 TIP 2: Choosing if vs. whether: in casual speech, if is fine for most yes/no embeddings, but use whether before infinitives, after prepositions, and often with “or not” for explicit alternatives.

📝 TIP 3: “Is it a nominal clause?” Try replacing the whole part with it or something. If the sentence still works and the structure is finite (has its own subject + verb), you’ve got a nominal clause.
