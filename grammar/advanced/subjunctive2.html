Present Subjunctive and Formal Expressions

Introduction

The present subjunctive is a special verb form used in formal English to express demands, recommendations, and necessity. This form is particularly important in business writing, academic English, and formal situations. While less common in everyday conversation, understanding the present subjunctive will help you sound more professional and polished in formal contexts.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the present subjunctive correctly after verbs of demand, recommendation, and suggestion.
  • Form subjunctive structures after expressions of importance and necessity.
  • Recognise when formal subjunctive forms are appropriate.

Present Subjunctive After Certain Verbs

Form: that + subject + base verb (no -s, no auxiliary verbs)

When to use it: Use the present subjunctive after verbs that express demands, requests, recommendations, or suggestions. This form is very common in formal English, especially in business and academic writing.

Common verbs that trigger the subjunctive:

  • demand, request, require, insist
  • suggest, recommend, propose
  • ask, advise, urge
  1. The board of directors insists that the CEO resign immediately. (not "resigns")
  2. The committee recommends that the university implement new security measures. (not "implements")
  3. The judge demands that the defendant appear in court next Monday. (not "appears")

Using These Verbs in Past Tense

When the main verb is in past tense (demanded, suggested, recommended, insisted), the subjunctive form stays the same - still use the base verb.

  1. The manager insisted that the employee submit a written apology. (not "submitted")
  2. The doctor recommended that the patient avoid strenuous exercise. (not "avoided")
  3. The principal demanded that the teacher attend the disciplinary hearing. (not "attended")

Native speaker note: The present subjunctive is very formal and mainly used in writing and formal situations. In daily conversation, many people add -s to the verb in both present and past: "He insists that she writes the report" or "He insisted that she writes the report."

Subjunctive After Expressions of Importance

Form: It is + adjective/noun + that + subject + base verb

When to use it: Use the subjunctive after certain expressions that show importance, necessity, or urgency. These are very formal and common in business and academic English.

Common expressions:

  • It is essential/vital/crucial/important that...
  • It is necessary/imperative that...
  • It is recommended/suggested that...
  1. It is essential that all shareholders be notified of the merger immediately. (not "are")
  2. It is vital that the research proposal include detailed financial projections. (not "includes")
  3. It is imperative that the witness testify under oath during the proceedings. (not "testifies")

Alternative: In British English, you can also use "should + base verb": "It is essential that all shareholders should be notified immediately." Both forms are correct, but the subjunctive (without "should") is more common in American English.

Using These Expressions in Past Tense

When the expression is in past tense (it was essential, it was vital, it was crucial), the subjunctive form stays the same - still use the base verb.

  1. It was imperative that the diplomat remain neutral during the negotiations. (not "remained")
  2. It was essential that the laboratory maintain strict safety protocols. (not "maintained")
  3. It was crucial that all evidence be preserved for the investigation. (not "was")

Native speaker note: In everyday speech, native speakers often restructure this and say "It was essential for the laboratory to maintain strict safety protocols" instead of using the subjunctive form.

Summary: Present Subjunctive Forms

Context Form Formality Example
After demand/suggest verbs (present) that + subject + base verb Very formal The ministry proposes that the legislation take effect immediately.
After demand/suggest verbs (past) that + subject + base verb Very formal The judge ordered that the witness appear in court on Monday.
After importance expressions (present) It is essential that + subject + base verb Very formal It is crucial that the contract be signed before the deadline.
After importance expressions (past) It was essential that + subject + base verb Very formal It was necessary that the team complete the project before the merger.

Common Errors

1: Adding -s to the verb in present subjunctive

‚ùå The board demands that the executive director submits his resignation. (formal writing)


‚úì The board demands that the executive director submit his resignation.

In formal subjunctive, use the base form of the verb (no -s) for all subjects. Note: In informal speech, many native speakers do add -s, but this is incorrect in formal business or academic writing.

2: Using "is" or "are" instead of "be" after expressions of importance

‚ùå It is essential that the applicant is present for the interview. (formal writing)


‚úì It is essential that the applicant be present for the interview.

After expressions like "it is essential/vital/important," use the base form "be" (not "is" or "are") in formal writing. In informal speech, using "is/are" is common but not appropriate for formal contexts.

3: Using past tense in the "that" clause when the main verb is past

‚ùå The manager suggested that the team worked overtime. (formal writing)


‚úì The manager suggested that the team work overtime.

Even when the main verb is past (suggested), the subjunctive stays as the base form (work, not worked). In informal speech, using past tense is common but not appropriate in formal writing.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Remember trigger verbs: demand, suggest, recommend, insist, request, require. After these verbs + "that," always use the base form (subjunctive).

üìå TIP 2: The present subjunctive has NO auxiliary verbs (no do/does, no is/are). Just use the base form: "The council proposes that the law change" (not "does change").

üìå TIP 3: The present subjunctive is very formal. In informal speech, use regular present tense: "I suggest he leaves" instead of "I suggest he leave."
