Present Subjunctive and Formal Expressions

Introduction

The present subjunctive is a special verb form used in formal English to express demands, recommendations, and necessity. This form is particularly important in business writing, academic English, and formal situations. While less common in everyday conversation, understanding the present subjunctive will help you sound more professional and polished in formal contexts.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the present subjunctive correctly after verbs of demand, recommendation, and suggestion.
  • Form subjunctive structures after expressions of importance and necessity.
  • Recognise when formal subjunctive forms are appropriate and when informal alternatives are better.

Present Subjunctive After Certain Verbs

Form: that + subject + base verb (no -s, no auxiliary verbs)

When to use it: Use the present subjunctive after verbs that express demands, requests, recommendations, or suggestions. This form is very common in formal English, especially in business and academic writing.

Common verbs that trigger the subjunctive:

  • demand, request, require, insist
  • suggest, recommend, propose
  • ask, advise, urge
  1. The manager insists that he arrive on time. (not "arrives")
  2. I suggest that she take the earlier train. (not "takes")
  3. The doctor recommends that my father stop smoking. (not "stops")

Native speaker note: The present subjunctive is very formal and mainly used in writing and formal situations. In daily conversation, most people would say "I suggest she takes the earlier train" using normal present tense instead.

Subjunctive After Expressions of Importance

Form: It is + adjective/noun + that + subject + base verb

When to use it: Use the subjunctive after certain expressions that show importance, necessity, or urgency. These are very formal and common in business and academic English.

Common expressions:

  • It is essential/vital/crucial/important that...
  • It is necessary/imperative that...
  • It is recommended/suggested that...
  1. It is essential that he be informed immediately. (not "is")
  2. It is vital that the company reduce costs this year. (not "reduces")
  3. It is important that everyone attend the training session. (not "attends")

Alternative: In British English, you can use "should + base verb" instead: "It is essential that he should be informed immediately." Both forms are correct, but the subjunctive (without "should") is more common in American English and formal writing.

Summary: Present Subjunctive Forms

Context Form Formality Example
After demand/suggest verbs that + subject + base verb Very formal I suggest that he leave early.
After importance expressions It is essential that + subject + base verb Very formal It is vital that she arrive on time.

Common Errors

1: Adding -s to the verb in present subjunctive

The manager insists that he arrives on time.


The manager insists that he arrive on time. ‚úì

In the present subjunctive, use the base form of the verb (no -s) for all subjects.

2: Using "is" instead of "be" after expressions of importance

It is essential that everyone is present.


It is essential that everyone be present. ‚úì

After expressions like "it is essential/vital/important," use the base form "be" (not "is").

3: Adding auxiliary verbs to the subjunctive

I recommend that she should take the job.


I recommend that she take the job. ‚úì

The present subjunctive does not use auxiliary verbs like "should" or "does". Just use the base form.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Remember trigger verbs: demand, suggest, recommend, insist, request, require. After these verbs + "that," always use the base form (subjunctive).

üìù TIP 2: The present subjunctive has NO auxiliary verbs (no do/does, no is/are). Just use the base form: "I suggest that he leave" (not "I suggest that he should leave" or "does leave").

üìù TIP 3: The present subjunctive is very formal. In informal speech, use regular present tense: "I suggest he leaves" instead of "I suggest he leave."
