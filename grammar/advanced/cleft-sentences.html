It-Cleft Sentences (It is/was... that/who)

Introduction

It-cleft sentences are special structures that help us emphasise specific information in a sentence. We split (or "cleave") a sentence into two parts using "it is/was" to focus attention on the most important element. This structure is very useful in both formal writing and everyday conversation when you want to highlight particular information or correct a misunderstanding.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use "it is/was... that/who" to emphasise different parts of a sentence.
  • Choose the correct word (that/who) based on what you are emphasising.
  • Identify which element of a sentence is being emphasised in an it-cleft structure.

Basic It-Cleft Sentences: It is/was... that/who

Form: it + is/was + emphasised element + that/who + rest of sentence

When to use it: Use it-cleft sentences to emphasise a specific part of the sentence. This structure draws attention to the information that comes after "it is/was" and before "that/who".

Key points:

  • Use "who" when emphasising a person (subject or object)
  • Use "that" for things, places, times, or reasons
  • Use "is" for present situations and general truths
  • Use "was" for past situations
  1. It was Sarah who solved the problem.
    (emphasising the person - not John or anyone else)
  2. It is the price that worries me most.
    (emphasising what worries you - not the quality or the design)
  3. It was in 2019 that we moved to London.
    (emphasising when - not 2018 or 2020)

Comparing Normal Sentences with It-Cleft Sentences

Look at how the same sentence can emphasise different information depending on which element comes after "it is/was":

Normal sentence:
I met your brother at the conference last month.


It-cleft (emphasising who):
It was your brother that I met at the conference last month.
(not your sister or someone else)


It-cleft (emphasising where):
It was at the conference that I met your brother last month.
(not at the office or another place)


It-cleft (emphasising when):
It was last month that I met your brother at the conference.
(not last week or another time)

Emphasising Different Elements

You can use it-cleft sentences to emphasise different parts of a sentence. The word you use after the emphasised element depends on what type of information you are emphasising.

Emphasising People

Form: it + is/was + person + who + verb + rest of sentence

When to use it: Use "who" after the person's name or description. This is useful when correcting someone or making clear who was responsible for an action.

  1. It was the manager who approved your holiday request.
    (emphasising who approved it - not the director or someone else)
  2. It is my sister who needs help with the project.
    (emphasising who needs help - not me or anyone else)
  3. It was a colleague who told me about the job vacancy.
    (emphasising the source of information - not a friend or family member)

Native speaker note: In informal speech, native speakers sometimes use "that" instead of "who" even for people: "It was Tom that called." However, "who" is more appropriate in formal contexts and is always correct for people.

Emphasising Objects (Things)

Form: it + is/was + object/thing + that + subject + verb

When to use it: Use "that" after objects or things. This emphasises what was affected by the action or what something is about.

  1. It was this laptop that I bought yesterday, not the other one.
    (emphasising which specific object)
  2. It is the cost that concerns me most.
    (emphasising what concerns you - not the size or quality)
  3. It was your email that reminded me about the deadline.
    (emphasising what caused the reminder - not a phone call or message)

Emphasising Time

Form: it + is/was + time expression + that + subject + verb

When to use it: Use "that" after time expressions. This emphasises when something happened or happens.

  1. It was last Tuesday that we met the client.
    (emphasising when - not Monday or another day)
  2. It is in the morning that I work best.
    (emphasising when you work best - not in the afternoon or evening)
  3. It was during the summer holidays that we renovated the house.
    (emphasising when the renovation happened - not during term time)

Emphasising Place

Form: it + is/was + place expression + that + subject + verb

When to use it: Use "that" after place expressions. This emphasises where something happened or happens.

  1. It was in the drawer that I found my keys.
    (emphasising where - not in my bag or on the table)
  2. It is at the gym that I feel most relaxed.
    (emphasising where you feel relaxed - not at home or work)
  3. It was in Paris that they got engaged.
    (emphasising where - not in London or another city)

Emphasising Reasons

Form: it + is/was + reason + that + subject + verb

When to use it: Use "that" after reasons. This emphasises why something happened or happens.

  1. It is because of the traffic that I'm late.
    (emphasising the reason - not because you overslept or forgot)
  2. It was because of the cost that we cancelled the trip.
    (emphasising why - not because of time or other reasons)
  3. It is for health reasons that she stopped eating sugar.
    (emphasising why - not for weight loss or taste preferences)

Choosing the Correct Tense: is vs was

When to use "it is": Use "it is" for present situations, general truths, and habits. The main verb in the sentence can be in any present tense.

  1. It is my brother who lives in Australia. (present simple - he lives there now)
  2. It is the noise that is bothering me. (present continuous - it's bothering me now)
  3. It is the manager who has approved the budget. (present perfect - the approval happened recently and is relevant now)

When to use "it was": Use "it was" for past situations and completed actions. The main verb in the sentence is usually in a past tense.

  1. It was Sarah who called you yesterday. (past simple - the call happened yesterday)
  2. It was in 2019 that we moved to London. (past simple - the move happened in 2019)
  3. It was the manager who had approved the request before the meeting. (past perfect - the approval happened before another past event)

Important rule: Match "it is/was" to the time reference in your sentence, not to the tense of the main verb. If you're talking about the past (yesterday, last week, in 2019), use "it was". If you're talking about the present (now, these days, recently), use "it is".

Remember: The tense of "it is/was" controls the time frame. The main verb can then be in any tense that makes sense within that time frame.

Summary: It-Cleft Sentence Structures

What to Emphasise Form Example
Who did the action (subject) it is/was + person + who + verb It was my brother who borrowed your car.
What was affected (object) it is/was + thing + that + subject + verb It was the red car that they damaged.
When something happened it is/was + time + that + subject + verb It was yesterday that I saw her.
Where something happened it is/was + place + that + subject + verb It was at the station that we met.
Why something happened it is/was + reason + that + subject + verb It was because of the rain that the match was cancelled.

Common Errors

1: Using "it is" or "it was" incorrectly with verb tenses

‚ùå It is Sarah who made the cake yesterday.


‚úì It was Sarah who made the cake yesterday.

Use "it was" for past actions and "it is" for present actions or general truths. The tense of "is/was" should match the time reference, not the main verb tense.

2: Forgetting "that" or "who" in the it-cleft structure

‚ùå It was in London I met my husband.


‚úì It was in London that I met my husband.

Always include "that" (for things, places, times) or "who" (for people) after the emphasised element. This word connects the two parts of the cleft sentence and is essential to the structure.

3: Using "where" or "when" instead of "that" in it-cleft sentences

‚ùå It was at the caf√© where I left my wallet.


‚úì It was at the caf√© that I left my wallet.

In it-cleft sentences, always use "that" after places and times, not "where" or "when". Save "where" and "when" for relative clauses in different sentence structures.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Use it-cleft sentences to correct misunderstandings. If someone says "Did John call you?", you can answer: "No, it was Sarah who called me" to emphasise the correct person.

üìå TIP 2: Remember: use "who" for people in formal contexts, but "that" is acceptable in informal speech. For things, places, times, and reasons, always use "that".

üìå TIP 3: Think about the question you want to answer: "Who did it?" ‚Üí emphasise the person. "When did it happen?" ‚Üí emphasise the time. "Where did it happen?" ‚Üí emphasise the place.
