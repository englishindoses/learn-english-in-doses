Modal Progressive Forms

Introduction

Modal progressive forms combine modal verbs (must, should, might, etc.) with the continuous aspect to talk about ongoing actions. These forms add nuance to your English and are commonly used by native speakers in various situations.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form and use modal verbs with the progressive aspect (continuous form)
  • Understand the different meanings these combinations express
  • Use modal progressive forms to talk about ongoing actions, predictions, and deductions

Basic Structure of Modal Progressive Forms

The basic pattern: Modal verbs can be combined with the continuous aspect to talk about ongoing actions.

Structure: subject + modal verb + be + verb-ing

  1. She must be working late tonight. (I'm assuming/deducing this is happening now)
  2. He might be sleeping right now. (It's possible this action is in progress)
  3. They should be arriving at the airport soon. (I expect this will happen)

Native speaker note: Native speakers often use these forms when they're making guesses or assumptions about what is happening. The combination of modal + continuous feels less definite than simple modal forms.

Modal Progressive Forms for Deduction

Making deductions about current situations: We often use modal progressive forms to make guesses about what is happening now.

Common modals for deduction: must, might, could, may, can't

  1. He's not answering his phone. He must be having his meeting now. (I'm fairly certain)
  2. Look at those clouds. It might be going to rain soon. (It's possible)
  3. She can't be sleeping already - it's only 7 pm! (I think it's impossible)

Native speaker note: The level of certainty changes with different modals. "Must be doing" shows strong certainty, while "might/could/may be doing" shows possibility. "Can't be doing" shows certainty that something is NOT happening.

Modal Progressive Forms for Criticising or Expressing Expectations

Suggesting what people should or shouldn't be doing: We use should/shouldn't + be + -ing to talk about what we expect to be happening now or around now.

Structure: subject + should/ought to + be + verb-ing

  1. You should be studying for your exam instead of watching TV. (I expect this action)
  2. They should be preparing for their presentation right now. (This is the expected action)
  3. He shouldn't be working so late every night. (This ongoing action is not good)

Native speaker note: "Should be doing" often carries a sense of criticism or expectation. It suggests what the speaker thinks is the right or appropriate ongoing action in this situation.

Modal Progressive Forms for Future Arrangements

Talking about future expectations and arrangements: Will, should and ought to can be used with the progressive form to talk about actions expected to be in progress at a future time.

Structure: subject + will/should/ought to + be + verb-ing

  1. This time next week I will be flying to London. (Future action in progress)
  2. They should be arriving at the hotel around 6 pm. (Expected future arrangement)
  3. The company will be launching the new product next month. (Planned future action)

Native speaker note: "Will be doing" focuses on the action being in progress at a specific future time, while "should be doing" adds an element of expectation to the future action.

Summary of Modal Progressive Forms

Modal Progressive Form Function Level of Certainty Example
Must be doing Making deductions about present Strong certainty She must be working late.
Might/may/could be doing Expressing possibility Uncertainty He might be sleeping.
Should/ought to be doing Expressing expectation or criticism Expected action You should be studying.
Will be doing Future action in progress Certainty about future I will be waiting for you.
Can't be doing Negative deduction about present Certainty that something isn't happening She can't be sleeping yet.

Common Errors

1: Using the wrong form of 'be'

He must working late today.

He must be working late today. ‚úì

Always include 'be' between the modal verb and the -ing form.

2: Using the past form with modal verbs

They should were working on the project.

They should be working on the project. ‚úì

Modal verbs are always followed by the base form of 'be', not 'was' or 'were'.

3: Using the infinitive instead of the -ing form

She might be work in the office.

She might be working in the office. ‚úì

After 'be' in this structure, always use the -ing form of the main verb.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Modal progressive forms are often used to make guesses about current situations. If you're speculating about what might be happening right now, these forms are perfect.

üìù TIP 2: To make these forms negative, simply add "not" after the modal verb: "She might not be feeling well" or "He can't be sleeping yet."

üìù TIP 3: When using "should be doing," be aware that it can sound like criticism. It implies that you're not doing what is expected. This form is common in advice and suggestions.

Key Points to Remember

  • Basic Structure: modal verb + be + verb-ing
  • For Deduction: must be doing (strong certainty), might/may/could be doing (possibility), can't be doing (negative certainty)
  • For Expectations: should be doing (expectation or mild criticism)
  • For Future: will be doing (future action in progress at a specific time)
  • Always use the base form of 'be' after modal verbs, not 'was/were'
  • Always use the -ing form of the main verb after 'be'
