Advanced Conditional Expressions

Introduction

Conditional expressions like "unless," "provided that," and "as long as" help us communicate conditions in more precise and varied ways than basic if-clauses. These expressions allow you to express negative conditions, requirements, and ongoing circumstances that affect outcomes.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use "unless" correctly to express negative conditions.
  • Apply "provided that" and "providing that" to express requirements and stipulations.
  • Use "as long as" and "so long as" to describe continuing conditions.
  • Choose the most appropriate conditional expression for different situations.

Unless

Form: Unless + present simple/continuous, main clause (will/modal + verb)

When to use it: "Unless" means "if...not" or "except if." We use it to express negative conditions‚Äîsituations where something will happen only if a particular condition is not met. It presents the only circumstance that would prevent the main clause from happening.

  1. Unless we finish this report by 5 pm, the client will be very unhappy. (= If we don't finish the report by 5 pm, the client will be very unhappy.)
  2. Unless it rains tomorrow, I'll go for a long walk in the park. (= If it doesn't rain tomorrow, I'll go for a long walk in the park.)
  3. Unless you book tickets in advance, you won't get seats on that flight. (= If you don't book tickets in advance, you won't get seats.)

Native speaker note: Native speakers often use "unless" when they want to mention an exception that would change a planned course of action. For example, in a work setting: "I'll be at the meeting at 2pm unless the client calls with those urgent changes." In casual conversation, you might hear: "We're going to the beach tomorrow, unless it rains, in which case we'll go to the cinema instead." Notice how native speakers sometimes follow "unless" with "in which case" to clarify the alternative plan.

Provided that / Providing that

Form: Provided (that) / Providing (that) + present simple/continuous, main clause (will/modal + verb)

When to use it: "Provided that" or "providing that" (both forms are correct) express a specific requirement or condition that must be fulfilled for the main clause to happen. These expressions emphasize the importance of the condition being met. The word "that" is optional.

  1. You can use my car provided (that) you return it with a full tank of petrol. (The condition of returning the car with a full tank must be fulfilled.)
  2. We'll finish the project on time providing (that) everyone works efficiently. (The project will be completed on time only if the condition of efficient work is met.)
  3. The hotel will offer a discount provided (that) you stay for at least three nights. (The discount depends on meeting the minimum stay requirement.)

Native speaker note: "Provided that" sounds somewhat formal and is more common in written English and business contexts. You'll often hear it in contract discussions: "We'll deliver by next Friday, provided that we receive the materials on time." In everyday conversation, many native speakers would simply use "if" instead: "I can help you move house if you have all your stuff packed." When you do hear "provided" in speech, it's usually without "that": "You can borrow my car, provided you fill it up before returning it."

As long as / So long as

Form: As long as / So long as + present simple/continuous, main clause (will/modal + verb)

When to use it: "As long as" and "so long as" (both mean the same thing) express a continuing condition that must remain true for the main clause to be valid. They suggest that the outcome depends on a condition being maintained over time or consistently applied.

  1. You can borrow my laptop as long as you don't download any software. (You can borrow it on the continuing condition that no software is downloaded.)
  2. I'll help you move house so long as we finish before dinner time. (My help is conditional on finishing by dinner time.)
  3. The tour guide will answer questions as long as they're relevant to the tour. (The willingness to answer questions has the ongoing condition of relevance.)

Native speaker note: "As long as" is very common in everyday speech, especially when making arrangements or setting boundaries. You might hear a parent say to a child: "You can go to the party as long as you're back by 11." Or between friends: "I'm happy to share my Netflix account as long as you don't change my password." The tone often carries a slight warning or limitation. "So long as" means the same thing but sounds slightly more formal: "The company will support your project so long as it stays within budget."

Key Differences: Choosing the Right Expression

Key differences: Choosing between these expressions depends on what aspect of the condition you want to emphasize:

  • Unless: Emphasizes a negative condition or exception
  • Provided that: Emphasizes a strict requirement that must be met
  • As long as: Emphasizes a continuing condition or boundary

Compare these pairs:

  1. Unless I hear otherwise, I'll send the report tomorrow. (Emphasizes that sending the report is the default plan, with only one thing that might prevent it.)
    Provided that I get your feedback, I'll send the report tomorrow. (Emphasizes that getting feedback is a necessary requirement.)
  2. Unless it's too expensive, we'll stay at the Grand Hotel. (Emphasizes that high cost is the only potential barrier.)
    As long as it's reasonably priced, we'll stay at the Grand Hotel. (Emphasizes the ongoing condition of reasonable pricing.)
  3. Provided that you follow the safety guidelines, you can use the equipment. (Emphasizes that following guidelines is a strict requirement.)
    As long as you follow the safety guidelines, you can use the equipment. (Similar meaning, but emphasizes the continuing nature of following guidelines.)

Native speaker note: In many cases, native speakers use these expressions somewhat interchangeably, especially "as long as" and "provided that," with the choice often being a matter of personal preference or slight difference in emphasis rather than strict grammatical distinction.

Summary: Conditional Expressions

Expression Meaning Emphasis Example
Unless If not, except if Negative condition or exception Unless it rains, we'll have the party outside.
Provided that / Providing that On the condition that Strict requirement You'll pass the exam provided that you study hard.
As long as / So long as On the continuing condition that Ongoing condition or boundary You can watch TV as long as you finish your homework first.

Common Errors

1: Using the wrong tense with "unless"

Unless we will finish this by tomorrow, the project will fail.


Unless we finish this by tomorrow, the project will fail. ‚úì

With "unless" (and most conditional expressions), we use the present simple in the conditional clause (even when talking about the future), not "will." Remember to use present simple for the condition and will/would for the result.

2: Using double negatives with "unless"

Unless you don't hurry, we'll miss the train.


Unless you hurry, we'll miss the train. ‚úì

"Unless" already implies a negative condition (it means "if not"), so adding another negative creates a confusing double negative. Keep the verb in the clause after "unless" in its positive form.

3: Confusing "provided" as a past tense verb

The company provided that you work overtime, you'll get a bonus.


Provided that you work overtime, you'll get a bonus. ‚úì

In this context, "provided that" is a fixed phrase functioning as a conditional conjunction. It's not the past tense of the verb "provide." There should be no subject before "provided that" when used as a conditional expression.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: When using "unless," try mentally replacing it with "if...not" to check if your sentence still makes sense. This can help you avoid double negatives and ensure you're using it correctly.

üìù TIP 2: For formal writing such as business emails or academic essays, "provided that" often sounds more appropriate than "as long as," which has a more conversational tone.

üìù TIP 3: These conditional expressions can appear at the beginning or in the middle of sentences. When they appear at the beginning, remember to use a comma after the conditional clause: "As long as you study hard, you'll pass the exam."
