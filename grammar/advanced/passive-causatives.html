Complex Passive Causatives (have/get something done)

Introduction

Causative structures in English express when someone causes something to happen, typically by arranging for another person to do it. Passive causatives specifically focus on the result rather than who performed the action. These structures are essential for talking about services, experiences, and situations where you arrange for things to be done but don't do them yourself.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use "have something done" and "get something done" structures correctly
  • Understand the subtle differences between the various causative forms
  • Apply these structures to talk about services, experiences, and arrangements

Have Something Done

Form: subject + have + object + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use it: Use this structure when:

  • Someone arranges for a service to be done
  • The subject doesn't perform the action themselves
  • Discussing professional or paid services
  1. We had our house painted last month. (We arranged for painters to do this)
  2. I'm having my car serviced tomorrow. (I've scheduled this service)
  3. The company had its website redesigned by a professional. (They hired someone)

Native speaker note: This structure is neutral in tone and commonly used for standard services or arrangements. The emphasis is on the arrangement and result rather than who performed the action.

Get Something Done

Form: subject + get + object + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use it: Use this structure when:

  • Someone arranges for something with some effort or difficulty
  • Emphasising the achievement of getting something accomplished
  • In more informal or conversational contexts
  1. I finally got my visa approved after months of paperwork. (Emphasises effort/achievement)
  2. She got her article published in a major magazine. (Suggests accomplishment)
  3. We got our application processed quickly because we knew someone in the office. (Implies advantage)

Native speaker note: "Get something done" often implies more effort, difficulty, or personal involvement than "have something done." It's also more informal and common in spoken English.

Negative Experiences with Causatives

Form: subject + have/get + object + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use this structure when:

  • Something negative happens to the subject's possessions or themselves
  • The subject didn't want or arrange for it to happen
  • Describing unfortunate events or victimisation
  1. She had her purse stolen on the train. (She didn't arrange this; it happened to her)
  2. They got their window broken during the storm. (Unwanted experience)
  3. He had his identity stolen when he used that unsecured website. (Negative consequence)

Native speaker note: For negative experiences, the context makes it clear that the subject was a victim rather than someone who arranged for the action. The structure is the same as for arranged services, but the meaning is different.

Summary: Passive Causative Structures

Causative Type Structure Usage Example
Have causative have + object + past participle Arranged services, neutral tone I had my hair cut yesterday.
Get causative get + object + past participle Achievement with effort, informal She got her car repaired yesterday.
Negative experience with have have + object + past participle Victim of negative event I had my wallet stolen.
Negative experience with get get + object + past participle Victim of negative event (informal) We got our car scratched.
Present continuous causative am/is/are having + object + past participle Service in progress or planned I'm having my teeth cleaned today.
Past causative had + object + past participle Completed arranged service They had their house painted last month.
Future causative will have + object + past participle Future arranged service I'll have my car serviced next week.

Common Errors

1: Confusing have/get something done with passive voice

I had cut my hair yesterday.


I had my hair cut yesterday. ‚úì

Remember that in causative structures, the object (hair) comes between "have/get" and the past participle.

2: Using the wrong form of the main verb

She got her house painting last week.


She got her house painted last week. ‚úì

Use the past participle (V3) after the object, not the -ing form.

3: Using causative for things you do yourself

I had written my essay yesterday.


I wrote my essay yesterday. ‚úì

Only use causative structures when someone else performs the action. For actions you do yourself, use normal active voice.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: To decide whether to use "have" or "get" in causative structures, consider the formality of the situation and whether you want to emphasise effort or achievement (use "get") or simply state a fact (use "have").

üìù TIP 2: For negative experiences, the same structure is used, but the context makes it clear that you didn't arrange for it to happen. If in doubt, add information that clarifies it was unwanted: "I had my wallet stolen while I was on holiday."

üìù TIP 3: The causative structure is particularly useful when talking about services: haircuts, repairs, cleaning, etc. It's a very common way to describe such situations in English, so it's worth practising examples related to everyday services you might use.
