Complex Prepositional Phrases

Introduction

Simple prepositions like in, on, at, and for are among the first words English learners study. However, English also has many multi-word prepositions ‚Äî sometimes called complex prepositions ‚Äî that are essential for advanced writing and formal communication. These phrases work just like simple prepositions but allow you to express more precise relationships between ideas. You will find them everywhere in academic texts, business emails, and news reports.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use two-word and three-word complex prepositions correctly in sentences.
  • Choose between complex prepositions and their simpler alternatives depending on formality.
  • Use complex prepositional phrases to add detail about reason, contrast, addition, and exception.

Two-Word Complex Prepositions

Form: two-word preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Two-word complex prepositions are made up of an adverb or adjective followed by a simple preposition. They express more specific meanings than simple prepositions alone. These are common in both written and spoken English.

Key rules:

  • According to ‚Äî used to report what someone else has said or written.
  • Due to ‚Äî used to give a reason or cause (similar to because of).
  • Apart from ‚Äî used to express an exception or addition (similar to except for or in addition to).
  • Ahead of ‚Äî used to express being in front of something in time or position.
  • Along with ‚Äî used to add someone or something to a group.
  1. According to the manager, the deadline has been moved to Friday.
    (= reports what the manager said.)
  2. The flight was cancelled due to bad weather.
    (= gives the reason for the cancellation.)
  3. Apart from the hotel, everything about the trip was excellent.
    (= the hotel was the exception.)

Native speaker note: Due to and because of are often used interchangeably in everyday English. Strictly speaking, due to follows the verb be ("The delay was due to traffic"), while because of follows action verbs ("We were late because of traffic"). In practice, most native speakers do not make this distinction.

Three-Word Complex Prepositions

Form: preposition + noun + preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Three-word complex prepositions follow the pattern: simple preposition + noun + simple preposition. They are very common in formal and academic English. Learning these phrases will make your writing more precise and professional.

Key rules:

  • In spite of ‚Äî used to show contrast (similar to despite or although).
  • In addition to ‚Äî used to add extra information (similar to as well as).
  • In terms of ‚Äî used to specify which aspect you are talking about.
  • On behalf of ‚Äî used to say you are acting or speaking for someone else.
  • By means of ‚Äî used to describe the method or instrument used (formal).
  • In favour of ‚Äî used to show support for something.
  1. In spite of the rain, we enjoyed the walking tour.
    (= the rain did not stop the enjoyment.)
  2. In addition to her salary, she receives a travel allowance.
    (= extra information about her income.)
  3. The company voted in favour of the new working policy.
    (= the company supported it.)

Complex Prepositions for Reason and Cause

Form: complex preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Several complex prepositions are used specifically to explain why something happened. These are especially useful in formal writing, reports, and academic essays.

Key rules:

  • As a result of ‚Äî the situation that caused something.
  • On account of ‚Äî a formal way to say because of.
  • In view of ‚Äî considering a situation (often used before decisions or recommendations).
  • These prepositions can be followed by a noun, a noun phrase, or a verb-ing form.
  1. As a result of the new regulations, several flights were delayed.
    (= the regulations caused the delays.)
  2. The event was postponed on account of safety concerns.
    (= the concerns were the reason.)
  3. In view of the recent changes, the team decided to update the plan.
    (= considering the changes.)

Complex Prepositions for Contrast and Exception

Form: complex preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: These complex prepositions are used to show contrast (something unexpected) or to express exceptions (something that is not included).

Key rules:

  • In spite of / despite ‚Äî something happened even though there was a reason for it not to. (In spite of is followed by a noun or verb-ing; despite works the same way.)
  • Regardless of ‚Äî no matter what the situation is.
  • With the exception of ‚Äî everything except one thing.
  1. In spite of being tired, she finished the entire report.
    (= she was tired, but she still finished it.)
  2. The museum is open every day, regardless of public holidays.
    (= public holidays do not change this.)
  3. With the exception of the final chapter, the book was very enjoyable.
    (= every part except the final chapter.)

Native speaker note: In spite of and despite mean exactly the same thing. Despite is slightly more common in modern English because it is shorter. Never write "despite of" ‚Äî this is always incorrect.

Key Differences

It can be difficult to choose between a simple preposition and a complex one. Here is a simple guide:

  • Use complex prepositions when you want to sound more precise, formal, or professional.
  • Use simple alternatives in casual conversation or informal writing.
  • Both forms are grammatically correct ‚Äî the difference is mainly one of tone and register.
  • The project was delayed because of budget problems. (simple ‚Äî works in any context.)
  • The project was delayed as a result of budget problems. (complex ‚Äî sounds more formal and precise.)
  • Besides the cost, everything was fine. (simple ‚Äî casual.)
  • In addition to the cost, there were logistical issues. (complex ‚Äî formal and detailed.)

Summary: Complex Prepositional Phrases

Complex Preposition Meaning Formality Example
According to Reports information from a source Neutral According to the news, prices are rising.
Due to Gives a reason or cause Neutral to formal The delay was due to traffic.
Apart from Exception or addition Neutral Apart from the food, the trip was great.
In spite of / despite Contrast (something unexpected) Neutral to formal In spite of the rain, we went out.
In addition to Adds extra information Formal In addition to English, she speaks French.
As a result of Cause leading to an outcome Formal As a result of the error, the project was delayed.
On behalf of Acting or speaking for someone Formal He signed on behalf of the director.
Regardless of No matter what Formal The policy applies regardless of age.
In terms of Specifying an aspect Neutral to formal In terms of quality, this is the best option.

Common Errors

1: Writing "despite of" instead of "despite" or "in spite of"

‚ùå Despite of the bad reviews, we watched the film.


‚úì Despite the bad reviews, we watched the film.

Despite is never followed by of. If you want to use of, write in spite of. Both mean the same thing.

2: Using "according to me" to give a personal opinion

‚ùå According to me, the hotel was too expensive.


‚úì In my opinion, the hotel was too expensive.

According to is used to report what someone else (a source, a report, a person) has said. For your own opinion, use in my opinion or I think.

3: Confusing "in addition to" with "in addition" (without to)

‚ùå In addition to, the company offers free parking.


‚úì In addition, the company offers free parking.

‚úì In addition to a good salary, the company offers free parking.

In addition to is a preposition and must be followed by a noun or noun phrase. In addition (without to) is a connecting adverb and is followed by a comma and a new clause.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Learn complex prepositions in groups based on their function: reason (due to, as a result of, on account of), contrast (in spite of, regardless of), addition (in addition to, along with). This makes it easier to choose the right one when you are writing.

üìå TIP 2: When writing formal emails or reports, try replacing simple prepositions with their complex equivalents. For example, swap because of for as a result of, or except for for with the exception of. This small change can make your writing sound much more professional.

üìå TIP 3: Remember that complex prepositions are followed by nouns, noun phrases, or verb-ing forms ‚Äî never by a full clause with a subject and verb. If you need a full clause, use the conjunction equivalent instead (e.g. use although instead of in spite of).
