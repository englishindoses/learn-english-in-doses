Complex Prepositional Phrases

Introduction

Simple prepositions like in, on, at, and for are among the first words English learners study. However, English also has many multi-word prepositions — sometimes called complex prepositions — that are essential for advanced writing and formal communication. These phrases work just like simple prepositions but allow you to express more precise relationships between ideas. You will find them everywhere in academic texts, business emails, and news reports.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use two-word, three-word, and multi-word complex prepositions correctly in sentences.
  • Choose between complex prepositions and their simpler alternatives depending on formality.
  • Use complex prepositional phrases to add detail about reason, contrast, addition, and exception.

Two-Word Complex Prepositions

Form: two-word preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Two-word complex prepositions are made up of an adverb or adjective followed by a simple preposition. They express more specific meanings than simple prepositions alone. These are common in both written and spoken English.

Key prepositions:

  • According to — used to report what someone else has said or written.
  • Due to — used to give a reason or cause (similar to because of).
  • Apart from — used to express an exception or addition (similar to except for or in addition to).
  1. According to the manager, the deadline has been moved to Friday.
    (= reports what the manager said.)
  2. The flight was cancelled due to bad weather.
    (= gives the reason for the cancellation.)
  3. Apart from the hotel, everything about the trip was excellent.
    (= the hotel was the exception.)

Usage note: Due to and because of have the same meaning, but they are used differently in a sentence. Use due to after the verb be ("The delay was due to traffic."). Use because of after action verbs ("We were late because of traffic."). In formal writing, it is best to follow this rule.

Three-Word Complex Prepositions

Form: preposition + noun + preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Three-word complex prepositions follow the pattern: simple preposition + noun + simple preposition. They are very common in formal and academic English. Learning these phrases will make your writing more precise and professional.

Key prepositions:

  • In spite of — used to show contrast (similar to despite or although).
  • In addition to — used to add extra information (similar to as well as).
  • On behalf of — used to say you are acting or speaking for someone else.
  1. In spite of the rain, we enjoyed the walking tour.
    (= the rain did not stop the enjoyment.)
  2. In addition to her salary, she receives a travel allowance.
    (= extra information about her income.)
  3. She spoke on behalf of the entire team.
    (= she represented the team.)

Usage note: In spite of and despite mean exactly the same thing. Despite is slightly more common in modern English because it is shorter. Never write "despite of" — this is always incorrect.

Multi-Word Complex Prepositions

Form: multi-word preposition (4+ words) + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Some complex prepositions are made up of four or more words. These are the most formal and are mainly found in written English — especially in reports, contracts, official documents, and academic essays.

Key prepositions:

  • As a result of — used to explain the cause of something (similar to because of).
  • With the exception of — used to say that everything is included except one thing (similar to except for).
  • In the event of — used to talk about what to do if something happens (similar to if there is).
  1. As a result of the new regulations, several flights were delayed.
    (= the regulations caused the delays.)
  2. With the exception of the final chapter, the book was very enjoyable.
    (= every part except the final chapter.)
  3. In the event of an emergency, please call this number.
    (= if an emergency happens.)

Formality note: Complex prepositions are generally more formal and precise than their simpler alternatives. Use them in professional writing such as emails, reports, and essays. In casual conversation, it is usually more natural to use the simpler form. Both are grammatically correct — the difference is mainly one of tone and register.

Summary: Complex Prepositional Phrases

Complex Preposition Meaning Formality Example
According to Reports information from a source. Neutral According to the news, prices are rising.
Due to Gives a reason or cause. Neutral to formal The delay was due to traffic.
Apart from Exception or addition. Neutral Apart from the food, the trip was great.
In spite of Contrast (something unexpected). Neutral to formal In spite of the rain, we went out.
In addition to Adds extra information. Formal In addition to English, she speaks French.
On behalf of Acting or speaking for someone. Formal He signed on behalf of the director.
As a result of Cause leading to an outcome. Formal As a result of the error, the project was delayed.
With the exception of Everything except one thing. Formal With the exception of Tom, everyone passed.
In the event of If something happens. Formal In the event of rain, the event will move indoors.

Common Errors

1: Writing "despite of" instead of "despite" or "in spite of"

❌ Despite of the bad reviews, we watched the film.


✓ Despite the bad reviews, we watched the film.

Despite is never followed by of. If you want to use of, write in spite of. Both mean the same thing.

2: Using "according to me" to give a personal opinion

❌ According to me, the hotel was too expensive.


✓ In my opinion, the hotel was too expensive.

According to is used to report what someone else (a source, a report, a person) has said. For your own opinion, use in my opinion or I think.

3: Confusing "in addition to" with "in addition" (without to)

❌ In addition to, the company offers free parking.


✓ In addition, the company offers free parking.

✓ In addition to a good salary, the company offers free parking.

In addition to is a preposition and must be followed by a noun or noun phrase. In addition (without to) is a connecting adverb and is followed by a comma and a new clause.

Useful Tips

📌 TIP 1: Learn complex prepositions in groups based on their function: reason (due to, as a result of), contrast (in spite of), addition (in addition to, apart from), exception (with the exception of). This makes it easier to choose the right one when you are writing.

📌 TIP 2: When writing formal emails or reports, try replacing simple prepositions with their complex equivalents. For example, swap because of for as a result of, or except for for with the exception of. This small change can make your writing sound much more professional.

📌 TIP 3: Remember that complex prepositions are followed by nouns, noun phrases, or verb-ing forms — never by a full clause with a subject and verb. If you need a full clause, use the conjunction equivalent instead (e.g. use although instead of in spite of).
