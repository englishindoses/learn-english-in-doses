Perfect Infinitives and Perfect Gerunds

Introduction

Perfect infinitives and perfect gerunds allow you to express complex time relationships in a concise way. These advanced verb forms add time depth to your sentences by showing actions that occurred before the main verb. Mastering these forms will significantly enhance the sophistication and precision of your English.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form and use perfect infinitives (to have done) correctly.
  • Use perfect gerunds (having done) in appropriate contexts.
  • Express completed actions before a main verb.
  • Understand and use common verb patterns with perfect infinitives and gerunds.

Perfect Infinitives: Form and Basic Uses

Form: to + have + past participle (to have done, to have seen, to have gone, etc.)

When to use it: Use the perfect infinitive to express an action that happened before the main verb in the sentence. It creates a clear time sequence, showing that one action preceded another.

  1. "She claims to have met the president last year." (The meeting happened before her claim)
  2. "I'm happy to have finished the project on time." (The finishing happened before the feeling of happiness)
  3. "They appear to have solved the problem." (The solving seems to have happened already)

Native speaker note: Native speakers often use perfect infinitives in formal contexts or in writing more than in casual conversation. In conversational English, they might use a that-clause instead: "She claims that she met the president last year."

Perfect Infinitives with Modal Verbs

Form: modal verb + have + past participle (should have done, might have seen, etc.)

When to use it: Perfect infinitives with modal verbs express hypothetical past situations, missed opportunities, past possibility, probability, or obligation.

  1. "You should have called me earlier." (Past obligation or advice that wasn't followed)
  2. "They might have missed the train." (Past possibility)
  3. "He must have forgotten our appointment." (Strong past probability)
  4. "I could have helped you if you had asked." (Past possibility that didn't happen)

Native speaker note: The tone and emphasis of modal perfect infinitives can vary greatly. "You should have called" often conveys criticism, while "could have helped" can express regret or missed opportunity. Native speakers frequently use these forms to express judgments about past actions.

Perfect Gerunds: Form and Basic Uses

Form: having + past participle (having done, having seen, having gone, etc.)

When to use it: Use the perfect gerund to talk about an action that happened before another action, especially after prepositions or certain verbs that are followed by gerunds.

  1. "She denied having taken the money." (The taking happened before the denial)
  2. "Having finished my work, I went home." (The finishing happened before going home)
  3. "He regrets having told her the secret." (The telling happened before the regret)

Native speaker note: Perfect gerunds are less common in conversational English. Instead of "She denied having taken the money," a native speaker might say "She denied that she took the money." However, perfect gerunds are common in formal writing and academic contexts.

Verbs Commonly Used with Perfect Infinitives

Form: specific verb + to have + past participle

When to use it: Certain verbs frequently take perfect infinitives to show that the infinitive action happened before the main verb. These include verbs of claiming, reporting, perception, and feeling.

  1. Claim/Report: "He claims to have worked with many celebrities." (The working happened before the claim)
  2. Seem/Appear: "They seem to have left already." (The leaving appears to have happened in the past)
  3. Pretend: "She pretended to have forgotten about the incident." (She acted as if she had forgotten)
  4. Be + adjective: "I'm pleased to have met you." (The meeting happened before the feeling of pleasure)

Native speaker note: With reporting verbs like "claim," "say," or "report," native speakers often prefer a that-clause in conversation: "He says that he worked with celebrities" rather than "He claims to have worked with celebrities," which sounds more formal.

Verbs Commonly Used with Perfect Gerunds

Form: specific verb + having + past participle

When to use it: Certain verbs take gerunds, and when you need to express that the gerund action happened before the main verb, you use a perfect gerund. Common verbs include those expressing feelings, denial, or admission.

  1. Deny/Admit: "He denied having seen the document." (The seeing allegedly happened before the denial)
  2. Regret/Remember: "I regret having told her the truth." (The telling happened before the regret)
  3. Appreciate/Thank: "They appreciated having been informed in advance." (The informing happened before the appreciation)

Native speaker note: Expressions of regret frequently use perfect gerunds: "I regret having said that" is common in both spoken and written English, though "I regret that I said that" is also frequently used in casual conversation.

Perfect Infinitives and Gerunds with Prepositions

Form:
Preposition + having + past participle
for/except/instead of + to have + past participle

When to use it: After prepositions, we typically use gerunds, including perfect gerunds. In a few cases, especially with 'for', 'except', and 'instead of', we may see perfect infinitives.

  1. "They congratulated him for having won the competition." (Preposition + perfect gerund)
  2. "Despite having studied all night, I failed the exam." (Preposition + perfect gerund)
  3. "After having lived abroad for ten years, she returned home." (Preposition + perfect gerund)
  4. "She had no choice except to have trusted him." (Exception with perfect infinitive)

Native speaker note: With time prepositions like "after" or "before," native speakers often drop the word "having" in casual speech: "After living abroad" instead of "After having lived abroad." In formal writing, however, the perfect form is more common.

Summary: Perfect Infinitives and Perfect Gerunds

Common Errors

1: Forgetting 'have' in perfect infinitives

She claims to met the president.


She claims to have met the president. ‚úì

A perfect infinitive must include 'have' between 'to' and the past participle to indicate that the action happened before the main verb.

2: Incorrect form with modals

You should to have called me.


You should have called me. ‚úì

With modal verbs, we don't use 'to'. The correct form is: modal + have + past participle.

3: Incorrect form of perfect gerund

He denied have taken the money.


He denied having taken the money. ‚úì

The perfect gerund form is 'having' + past participle, not 'have' + past participle.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Think of perfect infinitives and gerunds as "time machines" that take you back before the main verb. They show that one action happened before another.

üìù TIP 2: Remember the verb patterns: some verbs are typically followed by infinitives (claim, appear, seem), while others are followed by gerunds (deny, admit, regret).

üìù TIP 3: To decide whether to use a perfect form, ask yourself: "Did this action happen before the action in the main clause?" If yes, the perfect form is usually needed.

üìù TIP 4: With modal verbs, perfect infinitives usually express hypothetical past situations or judgments about past actions: "should have done," "could have gone," "must have been."
