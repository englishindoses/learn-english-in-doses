Dependent Prepositions — Part 1

Introduction

In English, many verbs are always followed by a specific preposition (for example, depend on or apologise for). There are no clear rules — you must learn each verb together with its preposition as a fixed pair.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the correct dependent preposition after common English verbs.
  • Recognise and avoid common preposition errors with verbs.
  • Form sentences using verb + preposition combinations followed by nouns and verb-ing forms.

Dependent prepositions vs phrasal verbs: These are not the same. With a dependent preposition, the verb keeps its normal meaning (depend on, apologise for). With a phrasal verb, the verb + particle creates a new meaning (give up = stop trying, run into = meet by chance).

Verbs Followed by "for"

Form: verb + for + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example verbs: apply for, wait for, pay for, apologise for, blame (someone) for, search for.

  1. She paid for lunch.
  2. We applied for the new position.
  3. He apologised for arriving late.

Verbs Followed by "to"

Form: verb + to + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example verbs: belong to, respond to, refer to, object to, look forward to, contribute to.

  1. This bag belongs to Sarah.
  2. She responded to my email.
  3. We look forward to meeting you next week.

Look forward to — a special case: Look forward is a phrasal verb (meaning "to feel excited about the future"). It is followed by the dependent preposition to. Because to here is a preposition — not the infinitive marker — the verb that follows must be in the verb-ing form.

✓ I look forward to hearing from you.

❌ I look forward to hear from you.

Verbs Followed by "about"

Form: verb + about + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example verbs: think about, talk about, worry about, complain about, dream about, forget about.

  1. We talked about politics.
  2. He worries about his health.
  3. She often dreams about travelling the world.

Verbs Followed by "on"

Form: verb + on + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example verbs: depend on, rely on, concentrate on, insist on, spend (time/money) on, congratulate (someone) on.

  1. We rely on technology every day.
  2. The plan depends on the weather.
  3. She insisted on paying the bill.

Verbs Followed by "in"

Form: verb + in + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example verbs: believe in, succeed in, participate in, result in, specialise in, invest in.

  1. He believes in love.
  2. The company invests in new technology.
  3. She succeeded in passing the exam.

Common Errors

1: Using the wrong preposition after depend

❌ The result depends of the final interview.


✓ The result depends on the final interview.

Depend is always followed by on, never of. This is a very common error, especially for speakers of Romance languages where the equivalent verb uses "of".

2: Using the infinitive instead of verb-ing after look forward to

❌ I look forward to hear from you soon.


✓ I look forward to hearing from you soon.

In the phrase look forward to, the word to is a preposition, not part of the infinitive. Prepositions are always followed by a noun or verb-ing form.

3: Leaving out the preposition entirely

❌ She apologised being late.


✓ She apologised for being late.

Many verbs require a dependent preposition before the object. Without it, the sentence is grammatically incomplete.

Memory Aids

📌 For — think of waiting, searching, asking, and paying (wait for, search for, ask for, pay for).

📌 To — think of speaking, responding, and relating (talk to, respond to, refer to, belong to).

📌 About — think of thinking, speaking, or feeling about a topic (think about, talk about, worry about).

📌 On — think of depending, focusing, or deciding (depend on, focus on, decide on).

📌 In — think of participation, belief, interest, or success (participate in, believe in, succeed in).
