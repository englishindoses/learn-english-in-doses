Passive Infinitives and Gerunds

Introduction

In English, not only can main verbs be passive, but infinitives and gerunds can also take passive forms. Passive infinitives and gerunds allow us to express complex ideas while maintaining the focus on the receiver of an action, especially in formal or academic contexts. These advanced structures are essential for sophisticated expression in English.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form and use passive infinitives correctly (to be + past participle)
  • Form and use passive gerunds properly (being + past participle)
  • Identify contexts where passive infinitives and gerunds are appropriate

Passive Infinitives

Form: to be + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use passive infinitives when:

  • The infinitive verb needs to be in passive voice
  • The focus is on the receiver of the action
  • Following verbs, adjectives, or nouns that require an infinitive
  1. The new employees expect to be trained properly during the first week. (After expect)
  2. The documents need to be signed by tomorrow morning. (After need)
  3. It's important to be vaccinated before travelling to certain countries. (After adjective)

Native speaker note: Passive infinitives often follow these verbs: want, need, expect, hope, would like, deserve, arrange. They also commonly follow "it + adjective" structures like "it's important/necessary/vital to be...".

Passive Gerunds

Form: being + past participle (V3)

When to use it: Use passive gerunds when:

  • The gerund verb needs to be in passive voice
  • The action is happening to the subject
  • Following prepositions or verbs that require a gerund
  1. She hates being photographed at family gatherings. (After the verb "hate")
  2. The suspect denied being seen near the crime scene. (After the verb "deny")
  3. We worry about being judged by others too often. (After the preposition "about")

Native speaker note: Passive gerunds often follow verbs of emotion (like, dislike, hate, love), reporting verbs (deny, admit, mention), and prepositions (about, of, on).

Special Structures with Passive Infinitives

Form: Various special constructions

When to use it: These special structures have specific meanings and uses.

Worth + being + past participle (V3) OR worth + gerund (with passive meaning):

  1. This book is worth being read. / This book is worth reading. (Both have passive meaning)
  2. The issue is worth being discussed. / The issue is worth discussing.

Need + passive infinitive OR need + gerund (with passive meaning):

  1. These shirts need to be ironed. / These shirts need ironing.
  2. The car needs to be washed. / The car needs washing.

Deserve + passive infinitive:

  1. She deserves to be promoted for her hard work.
  2. This achievement deserves to be celebrated.

Native speaker note: With structures like "need + -ing" (e.g., "The car needs washing"), native speakers often use the -ing form rather than "to be + past participle" in casual speech. Both forms are correct, but the -ing form is more concise.

Summary: Passive Infinitives and Gerunds

Structure Form Common Contexts Example
Passive Infinitive to be + past participle (V3) After verbs like want, need, expect The house needs to be painted.
Passive Gerund being + past participle (V3) After emotion verbs, prepositions She enjoys being admired.
Perfect Passive Infinitive to have been + past participle (V3) Referring to past events She seems to have been promoted last month.
Perfect Passive Gerund having been + past participle (V3) Referring to past experiences Having been rejected twice, he was nervous.
Worth + gerund worth + gerund Indicating value or merit The film is worth seeing.
Need + gerund need + gerund Indicating necessity Your hair needs cutting.
Deserve + passive infinitive deserve + to be + past participle Indicating merit He deserves to be recognized.

Common Errors

1: Forgetting "to be" in passive infinitives

The form needs signed by tomorrow.


The form needs to be signed by tomorrow. ‚úì

Remember to include the full form "to be" before the past participle in passive infinitives.

2: Incorrect form with -ing passives

I enjoy been invited to parties.


I enjoy being invited to parties. ‚úì

Use "being" (not "been") before the past participle when forming passive gerunds.

3: Using active when passive is needed (or vice versa)

I deserve promoting to senior position.


I deserve to be promoted to senior position. ‚úì

Certain verbs like "deserve" need a passive infinitive when the subject receives the action.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Remember the basic rules: passive infinitives use "to be + past participle" and passive gerunds use "being + past participle".

üìù TIP 2: With verbs like "need" and "require", you have a choice between "needs to be done" and "needs doing" - both have passive meaning, but the second form is more informal and common in everyday speech.

üìù TIP 3: When deciding between active and passive forms, ask yourself: "Who or what is performing the action?" If the receiver of the action is more important, use passive.

A. The floor needs to be cleaned.
B. I remember seeing her at the party.

D. The room is being painted.

3. Complete the sentence: "The windows need ________ before the guests arrive."

A. clean
B. cleaned
C. to be cleaned
D. cleaning

4. Which sentence is INCORRECT?

A. The car needs washing.
B. The car needs to be washed.
C. The car needs washed.

5. Which sentence contains a "worth + gerund" structure with passive meaning?

A. The book is worth reading.

D. watching
