Nominal Clauses Review

Introduction

Nominal clauses are groups of words that work like nouns in sentences. They help you express facts, opinions, uncertainty, and unknown information. This review covers the most important types you need for natural, advanced English communication.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use that-clauses to express facts and opinions, including when to drop "that".
  • Use if/whether clauses to express uncertainty and doubt.
  • Use wh-clauses to ask about unknown information, including embedded questions.

That-Clauses

Form: that + subject + verb

Meaning: That-clauses express facts, opinions, beliefs, and reports. You can often drop "that" in informal speech.

  1. The manager thinks (that) the project will succeed.
  2. Everyone knows (that) London is expensive.
  3. The news reported (that) the meeting was cancelled.

Reduction note: You can drop "that" in informal conversation, but keep it in formal writing and professional situations.

If/Whether Clauses

Form: if/whether + subject + verb

Meaning: If/whether clauses express uncertainty, doubt, and indirect yes/no questions.

  1. I don't know if the shop is open today.
  2. She asked whether I could help her.
  3. The question is whether we have enough budget.

Formal note: Use "whether" (not "if") in formal writing and after linking verbs like "is".

Practice: Fill in the gaps

1. We're not sure the train is on time.

Summary: Nominal Clauses

Clause Type Form Meaning Example
That-clause that + subject + verb Facts, opinions, reports I think (that) it's expensive.
If/Whether clause if/whether + subject + verb Uncertainty, doubt I don't know if/whether it's open.
Wh-clause wh-word + subject + verb Unknown information I know what time it is.
Embedded question wh-word + to + verb Instructions, advice I know what to do.

Common Errors

1: Using question word order in wh-clauses
I don't know what time is the meeting.
I don't know what time the meeting is. ‚úì
Use normal word order (subject + verb) in wh-clauses, not question order.
2: Missing "to" in embedded questions
She knows how use the machine.
She knows how to use the machine. ‚úì
Always include "to" in embedded questions with wh + to + verb.
3: Using wrong clause type for the meaning
I don't know to do what.
I don't know what to do. ‚úì
Use wh + to + verb for embedded questions, not infinitive + wh-word.

Useful Tips

üìù Tip 1: Choose based on what you want to express

That-clauses = facts and opinions; If/whether = uncertainty; Wh-clauses = unknown information; Infinitives = goals and plans.

üìù Tip 2: Use "whether" in formal situations

In business emails, academic writing, and professional contexts, prefer "whether" over "if" for a more formal tone.

üìù Tip 3: Remember normal word order in wh-clauses

Always use subject + verb order in wh-clauses, even though they contain question words. Think: "I know what she wants" not "what does she want".
