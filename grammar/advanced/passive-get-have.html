Advanced Passive Constructions with Get and Have

Introduction

Apart from the standard passive voice formed with "be + past participle (V3)", English offers alternative passive constructions using "get" and "have". These constructions are common in everyday speech and add different nuances to passive meanings. Understanding these alternative forms will enhance your ability to express subtle differences in meaning and register.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form passive sentences using "get" instead of "be".
  • Use "have something done" structures correctly.
  • Choose appropriately between "be", "get", and "have" passive constructions.

Get-Passive

Form: subject + get + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use it: Use get-passive when:

  • Emphasising a change of state or unexpected event
  • Speaking informally or conversationally
  • Focusing on the receiver of the action (especially with benefit or suffering)
  1. My phone got stolen at the concert last night. (negative, unexpected event)
  2. She got promoted after only six months with the company. (positive change)
  3. The children got caught playing in the neighbour's garden. (unexpected consequence)

Native speaker note: The get-passive is much more common in spoken English than in formal writing. It often carries an emotional tone and suggests something unexpected happened.

Have-Passive (Causative)

Form: subject + have + object + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use it: Use have-passive when:

  • Someone arranges for another person to do something
  • The subject does not perform the action themselves
  • Describing services that are paid for or requested
  1. We had our house painted last month. (We arranged for someone to paint it)
  2. I'm having my hair cut tomorrow. (I've booked an appointment)
  3. The company had its website redesigned by a professional. (They paid for this service)

Native speaker note: This structure doesn't necessarily indicate who performed the action, but implies the subject arranged for it to happen.

Key Differences Between Be, Get, and Have Passives

Form: Different structures with different meanings and nuances

When to use each: Your choice depends on the context, formality, and focus.

Compare these pairs:

  1. My car was repaired yesterday. (Simple fact, neutral)
    My car got repaired yesterday. (Implies satisfaction, informal)
    I had my car repaired yesterday. (I arranged and paid for the service)
  2. The fence was broken during the storm. (Just stating what happened)
    The fence got broken during the storm. (Emphasises the change, slight surprise)
    I had the fence painted before their visit. (Deliberately arranged for this to happen)
  3. Her story was published in a magazine. (Formal statement)
    Her story got published in a magazine. (Implies achievement, informal)
    She had her story published in a magazine. (She took action to make it happen)

Native speaker note: Native speakers often choose between these forms based on subtle nuances they want to express. The choice between "be/get/have" can significantly change the implications of a sentence.

Summary: Be, Get, and Have Passives

Passive Type Structure Usage Example
Be-passive is/was + V3 Formal, factual, neutral The house was built in 1950.
Get-passive get/got + V3 Informal, unexpected events, emotional He got fired last week.
Have-passive have/had + object + V3 Arranged services, delegation I'm having my car serviced tomorrow.
Having something done having + object + V3 Currently arranging a service I'm currently having my house painted.
Had something done had + object + V3 Arranged a service in the past We had our roof repaired last month.
Will have something done will have + object + V3 Arranging a future service I'll have my suit cleaned next week.
Negative event (get) get + V3 Unfortunate happenings My bicycle got stolen.

Common Errors

1: Confusing have-passive with simple past passive

I had my bike stolen yesterday.


My bike was stolen yesterday. ‚úì

When something negative happens that you didn't arrange, use "be" or "get" passive. "I had my bike stolen" suggests you arranged for someone to steal it!

2: Forgetting the object in have-passive constructions

I had repaired last week.


I had my car repaired last week. ‚úì

In have-passive constructions, you need to include what was affected (the object) between "have" and the past participle.

3: Using get-passive in formal writing

The legislation got approved by Parliament last month.


The legislation was approved by Parliament last month. ‚úì

In formal writing and academic contexts, use the be-passive rather than the more informal get-passive.

Useful Tips

üìù TIP 1: Use "get-passive" when you want to emphasise that something unexpected or surprising happened, especially in conversation.

üìù TIP 2: When describing a service you arranged and paid for (haircut, house repairs, etc.), the "have something done" structure is perfect.

üìù TIP 3: Remember that "get-passive" often carries emotional connotations (either positive or negative), while "be-passive" is more neutral and objective.
