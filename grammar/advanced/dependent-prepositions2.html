Dependent Prepositions — Part 2

Introduction

Just as many verbs have a fixed preposition (see Part 1), many adjectives and nouns do too. For example, proud of, married to, and the reason for. These are dependent prepositions — you must learn each pair as a fixed chunk.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the correct dependent preposition after common English adjectives.
  • Use the correct dependent preposition after common English nouns.
  • Recognise and avoid common preposition errors with adjectives and nouns.

Reminder from Part 1: Dependent prepositions are not the same as phrasal verbs. With a dependent preposition, the adjective or noun keeps its normal meaning — only the preposition is fixed.

Adjectives and Nouns Followed by "of"

Adjectives + of

Form: adjective + of + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example adjectives: proud of, afraid of, aware of, tired of, capable of, fond of, ashamed of.

  1. She is proud of her son.
  2. He is tired of waiting.

Nouns + of

Form: noun + of + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example nouns: cause of, fear of, advantage of, chance of, disadvantage of.

  1. What's the cause of the delay?
  2. There's no chance of winning now.

Adjectives and Nouns Followed by "for"

Adjectives + for

Form: adjective + for + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example adjectives: responsible for, famous for, ready for, grateful for, suitable for, sorry for.

  1. She is responsible for the whole team.
  2. He is famous for inventing new products.

Nouns + for

Form: noun + for + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example nouns: reason for, need for, demand for, respect for, excuse for.

  1. What's the reason for the delay?
  2. There's no excuse for being late.

Watch out — some words take more than one preposition:

Grateful takes for with a thing (grateful for the help) but to with a person (grateful to my teacher).

Need is also used as a verb. But need to + verb is verb + infinitive — the to here is not a dependent preposition.

Adjectives and Nouns Followed by "to"

Adjectives + to

Form: adjective + to + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example adjectives: married to, similar to, kind to, used to, accustomed to, opposed to.

  1. She is married to a doctor.
  2. He is not accustomed to working long hours.

Nouns + to

Form: noun + to + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example nouns: solution to, answer to, reply to, reaction to, key to.

  1. There's no simple answer to your question.
  2. Her approach to solving problems is unique.

Verb-ing rule: When to is a preposition (as in used to, accustomed to, opposed to, and look forward to from Part 1), the verb that follows must be in the verb-ing form.

✓ I'm used to working late.

❌ I'm used to work late.

Adjectives and Nouns Followed by "with"

Adjectives + with

Form: adjective + with + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example adjectives: pleased with, satisfied with, angry with, familiar with, disappointed with, bored with.

  1. I am pleased with the results.
  2. He is bored with studying every night.

Nouns + with

Form: noun + with + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example nouns: problem with, trouble with, relationship with, difficulty with.

  1. She has a problem with the new system.
  2. He has difficulty with speaking in public.

Direction matters with "familiar":

I'm familiar with the process. (the person is the subject.)

The process is familiar to me. (the thing is the subject.)

Adjectives and Nouns Followed by "in"

Adjectives + in

Form: adjective + in + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example adjectives: interested in, involved in, experienced in, successful in.

  1. She is interested in art.
  2. He is experienced in dealing with clients.

Nouns + in

Form: noun + in + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

Example nouns: increase in, decrease in, delay in, belief in, rise in, fall in.

  1. There has been an increase in prices.
  2. There was a long delay in processing the payment.

Common Errors

1: Using "with" instead of "to" after married

❌ She's married with a doctor.


✓ She's married to a doctor.

Married is always followed by "to" when referring to a spouse. "Married with" is a very common error, especially for speakers of Romance languages.

2: Using "of" instead of "to" after solution / answer

❌ There's no easy solution of this problem.


✓ There's no easy solution to this problem.

Solution, answer, reply, and reaction all take "to" when connected to a problem or question — never "of".

3: Using the infinitive instead of verb-ing after used to / accustomed to

❌ I'm used to work late.


✓ I'm used to working late.

The "to" in used to and accustomed to is a preposition, not the infinitive marker. Prepositions are always followed by a noun or verb-ing form.

Memory Aids

📌 Of — think of emotion or awareness towards a thing (proud of, afraid of, aware of; cause of, fear of).

📌 To — think of connecting people, things, or ideas (married to, similar to; solution to, answer to).

📌 With — think of how you feel or interact with a person or thing (pleased with, angry with; problem with, trouble with).
