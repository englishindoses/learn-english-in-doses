Dependent Prepositions ‚Äî Part 2

Introduction

In Part 1, you learned that many verbs are followed by fixed prepositions. The same is true for adjectives and nouns. Certain adjectives and nouns always require a specific preposition after them, and using the wrong one is a common mistake ‚Äî even for advanced learners. Just like with verbs, there is no general rule for these combinations. They must be learned as fixed pairs. This page covers the most important adjective + preposition and noun + preposition combinations.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the correct dependent preposition after common adjectives.
  • Use the correct dependent preposition after common nouns.
  • Form correct sentences using adjective/noun + preposition combinations followed by nouns and verb-ing forms.

Adjectives Followed by "of"

Form: be + adjective + of + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Many adjectives that describe feelings, awareness, or qualities are followed by of.

Key rules:

  • Afraid of ‚Äî feeling fear about something.
  • Aware of ‚Äî knowing about something.
  • Capable of ‚Äî having the ability to do something.
  • Fond of ‚Äî liking something (often used for habits or people).
  • Proud of ‚Äî feeling satisfaction about an achievement.
  • Tired of ‚Äî feeling bored or frustrated with something that has continued too long.
  1. She is very proud of her team's performance this quarter.
    (= she feels satisfaction about it.)
  2. He wasn't aware of the schedule change until this morning.
    (= he did not know about it.)
  3. They are tired of waiting for the repairs to be completed.
    (= they are frustrated by the long wait.)

Adjectives Followed by "about"

Form: be + adjective + about + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Adjectives followed by about usually express emotions or feelings towards a specific topic or situation.

Key rules:

  • Worried about ‚Äî feeling anxious about something.
  • Excited about ‚Äî feeling enthusiasm about something.
  • Serious about ‚Äî being genuine or committed to something.
  • Happy about ‚Äî feeling pleased about something.
  • Confused about ‚Äî not understanding something.
  • Curious about ‚Äî wanting to know more about something.
  1. She is excited about starting her new role next month.
    (= she feels enthusiastic about it.)
  2. He was confused about the directions to the hotel.
    (= he did not understand them.)
  3. We are serious about improving our customer service.
    (= we are committed to it.)

Adjectives Followed by "for"

Form: be + adjective + for + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Adjectives followed by for often describe suitability, responsibility, readiness, or feelings towards someone.

Key rules:

  • Responsible for ‚Äî having the duty or obligation for something.
  • Famous for ‚Äî well-known because of something.
  • Ready for ‚Äî prepared for something.
  • Sorry for ‚Äî feeling regret about something.
  • Good/bad for ‚Äî having a positive or negative effect on something.
  • Grateful for ‚Äî feeling thankful for something.
  1. She is responsible for managing the entire project.
    (= it is her duty.)
  2. The city is famous for its beautiful architecture.
    (= that is what it is known for.)
  3. I am grateful for all your help with the move.
    (= I feel thankful.)

Adjectives Followed by "to"

Form: be + adjective + to + noun / noun phrase

When to use it: Some adjectives are followed by the preposition to when describing a relationship or comparison between two things.

Key rules:

  • Similar to ‚Äî having things in common with something else.
  • Different from/to ‚Äî not the same as something else. (Both from and to are correct in British English.)
  • Accustomed to ‚Äî being used to something.
  • Married to ‚Äî being someone's husband or wife.
  • Addicted to ‚Äî being unable to stop doing or using something.
  • Committed to ‚Äî being dedicated to something.
  1. This system is quite similar to the one we used at the previous company.
    (= they have features in common.)
  2. She is fully committed to improving working conditions for her team.
    (= she is dedicated to it.)
  3. He is accustomed to travelling long distances for work.
    (= he is used to it.)

Native speaker note: In British English, both different from and different to are widely accepted. In American English, different from is strongly preferred. In formal writing, different from is the safest choice.

Nouns Followed by Prepositions

Form: noun + preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

When to use it: Just like verbs and adjectives, many nouns require a specific preposition. These combinations often appear in formal writing, business communication, and academic English. Below are some of the most important groups.

Nouns + for:

  • Reason for ‚Äî the cause or explanation for something.
  • Need for ‚Äî a requirement for something.
  • Demand for ‚Äî how much people want something.
  • Respect for ‚Äî admiration for someone or something.
  1. The reason for the delay was a technical problem.
    (= the cause of the delay.)
  2. There is a growing need for affordable housing in the city.
    (= a requirement that is increasing.)
  3. The demand for skilled workers has increased this year.
    (= people want more skilled workers.)

Nouns + of:

  • Advantage of ‚Äî a benefit of something.
  • Knowledge of ‚Äî understanding of a subject.
  • Fear of ‚Äî being afraid of something.
  • Lack of ‚Äî not having enough of something.
  1. One advantage of remote working is saving time on commuting.
    (= a benefit.)
  2. A good knowledge of local customs is helpful when travelling abroad.
    (= understanding the customs.)
  3. The lack of funding caused several projects to be cancelled.
    (= not enough funding.)

Nouns + in:

  • Interest in ‚Äî wanting to know about or do something.
  • Increase in ‚Äî a rise in something.
  • Difficulty in ‚Äî having trouble with something.
  • Experience in ‚Äî having knowledge gained from doing something.
  1. She has a strong interest in renewable energy.
    (= she wants to learn about it.)
  2. There has been a significant increase in online bookings this year.
    (= bookings have risen.)
  3. He has ten years of experience in project management.
    (= he has done it for ten years.)

Common Errors

1: Using "interested for" or "interested about" instead of "interested in"

‚ùå She is interested for learning more about the role.


‚úì She is interested in learning more about the role.

The adjective "interested" is always followed by "in". This is one of the most common preposition errors learners make.

2: Confusing "responsible of" with "responsible for"

‚ùå He is responsible of the marketing budget.


‚úì He is responsible for the marketing budget.

"Responsible" is always followed by "for", never "of". Many learners make this mistake because their first language uses the equivalent of "of".

3: Using a wrong preposition after a noun

‚ùå The increase of prices affected everyone.


‚úì The increase in prices affected everyone.

The noun "increase" is followed by "in", not "of". Similarly, "decrease" and "rise" are also followed by "in".

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: Learn adjective + preposition and noun + preposition combinations in exactly the same way as verb + preposition pairs: as fixed chunks. Write them on flashcards with example sentences so you remember the full combination, not just the individual words.

üìå TIP 2: When you are unsure which preposition follows an adjective or noun, try searching for the phrase in an online dictionary or corpus. Dictionaries almost always show the correct dependent preposition in their example sentences. Over time, you will build confidence with the most common combinations.

üìå TIP 3: Group similar adjectives by their preposition. For example, many emotion adjectives use about (worried about, excited about, happy about), while many ability or awareness adjectives use of (capable of, aware of, proud of). These groupings can help you guess the correct preposition even for combinations you have not memorised.
