Subjunctive Mood

Introduction

The subjunctive mood is a special verb form used to express wishes, suggestions, demands, hypothetical situations, and emotions. Unlike the indicative mood (which states facts), the subjunctive expresses ideas that are not necessarily real or factual. In English, the subjunctive appears in formal writing, fixed expressions, and certain grammatical structures.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use the present subjunctive correctly after verbs of suggestion, demand, and recommendation.
  • Apply the past subjunctive to express hypothetical and contrary-to-fact situations.
  • Recognise and use subjunctive forms in formal expressions and fixed phrases.

Present Subjunctive

Form: subject + suggest/recommend/demand/insist/require + that + subject + base form of verb

When to use it: Use the present subjunctive after certain verbs that express suggestions, demands, requirements, or recommendations. The subjunctive clause usually begins with "that."

Common verbs followed by present subjunctive: suggest, recommend, demand, insist, require, request, propose, advise, urge

  1. My manager suggests that I work overtime this week. (expressing a recommendation)
  2. The travel guide recommends that she book her hotel early. (expressing advice)
  3. The university requires that all students submit their essays by Friday. (expressing a requirement)
  4. I insist that he be more careful when driving. (expressing a demand)

Native speaker note: In informal speech, many native speakers use the normal indicative form instead of the subjunctive: "I suggest that he studies harder." However, in formal writing, business contexts, and academic English, the subjunctive is preferred and expected.

Past Subjunctive

Form: if + subject + were + (verb-ing/adjective/noun) OR subject + wish + subject + were + (verb-ing/adjective/noun)

When to use it: Use the past subjunctive to express hypothetical or unreal situations. It appears commonly in second conditionals and after "wish."

  1. If I were you, I would take that job offer. (giving advice about what to do in your situation)
  2. I wish I were on holiday in Spain right now. (expressing desire for something that isn't happening)
  3. If she were working here, she would help us with this project. (imagining an unreal work situation)
  4. If we were living in the city centre, we could walk to work. (imagining a different living situation and its benefits)

Native speaker note: In casual speech, many native speakers say "If I was you..." instead of "If I were you..." But "were" is more correct and always used in the fixed phrase "If I were you."

Subjunctive in Formal Expressions

Form: Fixed expressions using base form of verbs

When to use it: The subjunctive appears in many formal expressions, legal language, and traditional phrases. These are often fixed expressions that must be memorised.

  1. Long live the Queen! (Traditional expression - note "live," not "lives")
  2. Be that as it may, we still need to finish the project. (Formal expression meaning "even so")
  3. Come what may, we'll face it together. (Fixed expression meaning "whatever happens")
  4. Far be it from me to criticise your work. (Formal expression meaning "I don't want to")
  5. Suffice it to say that the meeting didn't go well. (Formal expression meaning "it's enough to say")

Native speaker note: These formal expressions are more common in writing than in speech. They may be used sometimes when speaking in formal situations or to add style/drama to speech. In everyday conversation, native speakers often use alternative constructions: "Suffice it to say..." -> "Let's just say..."

Subjunctive with "Wish" and "If only"

Form:
‚Ä¢ subject + wish + subject + past tense (for present situations)
‚Ä¢ subject + wish + subject + had + past participle (V3) (for past situations)
‚Ä¢ subject + wish + subject + would + base form (for future actions/complaints)

When to use it: Use the subjunctive after "wish" and "if only" to express regret, desires for different circumstances, or impossible wishes.

  1. I wish I had more free time for cooking. (Wish about current situation)
  2. If only I were travelling to Italy right now. (Strong wish about current situation)
  3. I wish I had taken that job in New York. (Regret about past decision)
  4. If only we had booked our holiday earlier. (Regret about past action)
  5. I wish my flatmate would wash the dishes more often. (Request for future change)

Native speaker note: "I wish" + past subjunctive is very common in everyday English. Native speakers use this structure regularly to express dissatisfaction with current situations or to talk about hypothetical scenarios. "If only" is more dramatic and emotional than "I wish."

Common Errors

1: Using normal verb forms instead of subjunctive

I suggest that he studies more.


I suggest that he study more. ‚úì

After verbs like "suggest," "recommend," and "demand," use the base form of the verb (subjunctive) for all subjects, not the normal present tense form.

2: Using "was" instead of "were" in hypothetical situations

If I was rich, I would travel the world.


If I were rich, I would travel the world. ‚úì

In formal English and hypothetical situations, use "were" for all subjects (I, he, she, it), not "was." This is especially important in the phrase "If I were you."

3: Confusing past and present wishes

I wish I would know the answer yesterday.


I wish I had known the answer yesterday. ‚úì

For wishes about past events, use "wish + past perfect." For present wishes, use "wish + past tense." For future wishes/complaints, use "wish + would."
