Gerund vs Infinitive: Verbs with Meaning Changes

Introduction

Some verbs in English can be followed by either a gerund (verb-ing) or an infinitive (to + verb), but the meaning changes depending on which form you use. Understanding these differences is essential for precise communication, as choosing the wrong form can completely change the meaning.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between the different meanings of key verbs when followed by gerund or infinitive.
  • Choose the correct form (gerund or infinitive) based on your intended meaning.
  • Use these verb patterns accurately in context.

Stop

Stop + verb-ing

Meaning: To finish or quit an activity permanently or for a period of time.

Form: stop + verb-ing

  1. He stopped smoking five years ago.
    (= He quit the habit completely.)
  2. Can you stop making that noise? I'm trying to concentrate.
    (= Please don't do that anymore.)
  3. It's stopped raining. Let's go outside.
    (= The rain has finished.)

Stop + to-infinitive

Meaning: To pause one activity in order to do something else.

Form: stop + to + verb

  1. We stopped to get petrol on the way to the airport.
    (= We paused our journey to get petrol.)
  2. She stopped to answer her phone during the meeting.
    (= She paused what she was doing to take the call.)
  3. I stopped to admire the view from the top of the hill.
    (= I paused my walk to look at the scenery.)

Note: This is one of the most commonly confused pairs. "He stopped smoking" means he quit cigarettes. "He stopped to smoke" means he paused what he was doing to have a cigarette ‚Äì the complete opposite!

Remember & Forget

These two verbs are opposites, but the meaning change between gerund and infinitive works in the same way for both.

Remember / Forget + verb-ing

Meaning: To have (or not have) a memory of something that happened in the past.

Form: remember/forget + verb-ing

  1. I remember locking the door before I left.
    (= I have a clear memory of doing this.)
  2. I'll never forget seeing the Northern Lights for the first time.
    (= This memory will stay with me forever.)
  3. Do you remember meeting him at Sarah's party?
    (= Do you have a memory of this?)
  4. She'll never forget visiting Japan. It was an amazing trip.
    (= The experience is unforgettable.)

Remember / Forget + to-infinitive

Meaning: To not forget (or to fail) to do a task or duty.

Form: remember/forget + to + verb

  1. Please remember to send the report by Friday.
    (= Don't forget to do this task.)
  2. I forgot to buy milk on the way home.
    (= I didn't remember to do this task.)
  3. Did you remember to book the restaurant?
    (= Did you do this task you were supposed to do?)
  4. Don't forget to turn off the lights when you leave.
    (= Remember to do this.)

Native speaker note: "Forget + verb-ing" is almost always used with "never" in the positive form: "I'll never forget visiting Paris." We rarely say "I forget visiting Paris" ‚Äì instead, we'd say "I don't remember visiting Paris."

Try

Try + verb-ing

Meaning: To experiment with something to see if it works or helps.

Form: try + verb-ing

  1. Have you tried restarting your computer?
    (= Have you tried this solution?)
  2. Try adding a bit more salt to the soup.
    (= Do this to see if it tastes better.)
  3. If you can't sleep, try drinking some warm milk before bed.
    (= Drink warm milk to see if it helps you sleep.)

Try + to-infinitive

Meaning: To make an effort or attempt to do something difficult.

Form: try + to + verb

  1. I tried to open the window, but it was stuck.
    (= I made an effort but couldn't do it.)
  2. She's trying to learn Japanese, but it's very challenging.
    (= She's making an effort to do something difficult.)
  3. We tried to contact you, but your phone was off.
    (= We attempted to reach you.)

Note: When giving advice about solutions, we use "try + verb-ing": "Try turning it off and on again." When talking about difficult attempts, we use "try + to-infinitive": "I tried to fix it myself, but I couldn't."

Regret

Regret + verb-ing

Meaning: To feel sorry about something you did (or didn't do) in the past.

Form: regret + verb-ing

  1. I regret telling her the secret. She told everyone.
    (= I told her the secret and now I regret it.)
  2. He regrets not studying harder at university.
    (= He didn't study hard and now he regrets it.)
  3. Do you regret leaving your previous job?
    (= Do you regret your decision?)

Regret + to-infinitive

Meaning: To feel sorry about what you are about to say (formal). Used to deliver bad news politely.

Form: regret + to + verb

  1. We regret to inform you that your application has been unsuccessful.
    (= We are sorry, but your application was not successful.)
  2. I regret to say that the event has been cancelled.
    (= I'm sorry about the sad news I'm announcing.)
  3. The airline regrets to announce a delay to Flight BA245.
    (= The airline is sorry to announce this bad news.)

Native speaker note: "Regret + to-infinitive" is quite formal and is typically used in official announcements, formal letters, or professional communication. In everyday speech, we'd simply say "I'm sorry to tell you that..."

Go on

Go on + verb-ing

Meaning: To continue doing the same activity.

Form: go on + verb-ing

  1. She went on talking for another hour.
    (= She continued the same activity ‚Äì talking.)
  2. Despite the rain, they went on playing football.
    (= They continued the same game.)
  3. He went on working until midnight.
    (= He continued his work without stopping.)

Go on + to-infinitive

Meaning: To do something different next; to move to a new activity or stage.

Form: go on + to + verb

  1. After finishing her degree, she went on to become a successful lawyer.
    (= She moved to a new stage in her career.)
  2. The band released their first album and went on to win several awards.
    (= They progressed to achieve something new.)
  3. He discussed the budget and then went on to explain the new strategy.
    (= He moved to a different topic.)

Native speaker note: "Go on + to-infinitive" is very common when talking about someone's career progression or life achievements: "She went on to become CEO" or "He went on to write several bestselling novels."

Mean

Mean + verb-ing

Meaning: To involve or result in something as a consequence.

Form: mean + verb-ing

  1. Taking this job means moving to another city.
    (= The job involves/requires relocation.)
  2. Being a doctor means working long hours.
    (= This is what being a doctor involves.)
  3. Missing the last train means waiting until morning.
    (= This will be the result/consequence.)

Mean + to-infinitive

Meaning: To intend or plan to do something.

Form: mean + to + verb

  1. I didn't mean to hurt your feelings.
    (= It wasn't my intention.)
  2. Sorry, I meant to call you yesterday but I forgot.
    (= I intended/planned to call.)
  3. She means to finish the project by Friday.
    (= She intends to do this.)

Native speaker note: "I didn't mean to..." is a very common way to apologise for unintentional actions: "I didn't mean to interrupt" or "I didn't mean to offend you."

Need

Need + verb-ing

Meaning: Something requires an action to be done to it. This has a passive meaning ‚Äì the subject receives the action.

Form: subject + need + verb-ing (= subject needs to be + past participle (V3))

  1. The car needs washing.
    (= The car needs to be washed.)
  2. This report needs checking before we send it.
    (= The report needs to be checked.)
  3. Your hair needs cutting.
    (= Your hair needs to be cut.)

Need + to-infinitive

Meaning: The subject must do an action. This has an active meaning ‚Äì the subject performs the action.

Form: subject + need + to + verb

  1. I need to wash the car this weekend.
    (= I must do this action.)
  2. You need to check your emails more regularly.
    (= You must do this.)
  3. She needs to cut the grass before it rains.
    (= She must perform this action.)

Native speaker note: "Need + verb-ing" is very common in British English for talking about things that require attention: "The house needs painting," "This shirt needs ironing." It's a shorter way of saying "needs to be painted/ironed."

Common Errors

1: Confusing "stop + verb-ing" and "stop + to-infinitive"

‚ùå I stopped to smoke last year. (when you mean you quit)


‚úì I stopped smoking last year.

"Stop + verb-ing" means to quit an activity. "Stop + to-infinitive" means to pause in order to do something.

2: Using "remember + to-infinitive" for past memories

‚ùå I remember to meet her at the party last year.


‚úì I remember meeting her at the party last year.

Use "remember + verb-ing" to talk about memories of past events. Use "remember + to-infinitive" for tasks you need to do.

3: Using "try + to-infinitive" when giving suggestions

‚ùå If you can't sleep, try to drink warm milk.


‚úì If you can't sleep, try drinking warm milk.

When suggesting an experiment or solution, use "try + verb-ing". Use "try + to-infinitive" when describing an attempt to do something difficult.

Useful Tips

üìå TIP 1: With "stop", ask yourself: "Did the activity end?" If yes, use verb-ing. "Did I pause to do something?" If yes, use to-infinitive.

üìå TIP 2: Think about time. With "remember" and "forget", the gerund refers to the PAST (something that already happened), while the infinitive refers to the FUTURE (something you need to do).

üìå TIP 3: With "try", think about the context: Is it a suggestion for a solution? Use verb-ing. Is it a difficult attempt? Use to-infinitive.
