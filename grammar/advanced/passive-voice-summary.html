Passive Voice: Complete Summary

Introduction

This comprehensive summary covers all aspects of passive voice in English, from basic structures to advanced constructions. The passive voice emphasises the receiver of an action rather than the doer, and is essential for formal writing, news reports, and academic communication.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use all forms of passive voice correctly in different contexts
  • Choose appropriately between active and passive constructions
  • Understand and use advanced passive structures confidently

Basic Passive Voice

Form: subject + be (conjugated) + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)

When to use:

  • Focus is on the action receiver, not the doer
  • The person doing the action is unknown or unimportant
  • You want to be more formal

Using "by": Include the agent when it's important or surprising information.

  1. The report was written by Sarah yesterday.
  2. These cakes are made with good ingredients.
  3. The flight has been delayed due to bad weather.

Alternative Passive Forms

Get-passive: subject + get + past participle (V3)

More informal, emphasises unexpected events

  1. My phone got stolen at the concert. (negative, unexpected)
  2. She got promoted after six months. (positive change)

Native speaker note: Get-passive is very common in spoken English.

Causative passive: subject + have/get + object + past participle (V3)

You arrange for someone else to do something for you

  1. I had my car repaired yesterday.
  2. We need to get our house painted.

Native speaker note: "had" = past/completed action; "get" = more informal, often future.

Advanced Passive Forms

Passive infinitives: to be + past participle (V3)

  1. The documents need to be signed.
  2. It's important to be vaccinated.

Passive gerunds: being + past participle (V3)

  1. I hate being interrupted.
  2. Being chosen for the team was exciting.

Native speaker note: Passive infinitives and gerunds are common after certain verbs and adjectives.

Passive reporting verbs: These report information without stating the source directly.

  • Present/future: subject + is/are + reporting verb (V3) + to be + past participle (V3)
  • Past: subject + is/are + reporting verb (V3) + to have been + past participle (V3)
  1. Present: The suspect is reported to be hiding in the mountains.
  2. Future: The bridge is expected to be completed next year.
  3. Past: The manuscript is believed to have been written in the 15th century.

Double Passive Constructions (Very Formal)

Two passive structures in one sentence. Common types:

  1. Passive reporting: The painting is believed to have been created by a famous artist.
  2. Passive + passive relative clause: The medicine was tested using methods that were approved by scientists.
  3. Passive modal + passive: The form must be signed before it can be processed.

Passive in Different Tenses

The verbs "be", "get", and "have" change with different tenses. The past participle (V3) stays the same.

  1. The company is being reorganised. (present continuous passive)
  2. All tickets had been sold. (past perfect passive)
  3. The bridge will be opened. (future simple passive)

Key points: Passive is more common in formal writing than conversation. Some complex passive forms are rarely used in everyday speech.

Summary: All Passive Voice Forms

Passive Type Structure Use Example
Basic Passive be + V3 Focus on receiver, formal The report was written by Sarah.
Get-Passive get + V3 Informal, unexpected events My phone got stolen.
Causative Passive have/get + object + V3 Arrange for someone else to do I had my car repaired.
Passive Infinitive to be + V3 After certain verbs/adjectives The documents need to be signed.
Passive Gerund being + V3 After certain verbs/prepositions I hate being interrupted.
Passive Reporting is/are + V3 + to be/have been + V3 Report without stating source The bridge is expected to be completed.
Double Passive Various combinations Very formal, academic writing The form must be signed before it can be processed.

Useful Tips

1. Notice Passive Voice in Real Life

Look for passive voice in news articles, scientific papers, and formal documents. Pay attention to when writers use passive instead of active voice - usually when the action is more important than who did it.

2. Active or Passive? Ask These Questions

Use passive when: Who did it is unknown/unimportant? Do you want to sound more formal? Is the receiver of the action more important than the doer? If yes to any, consider passive voice.

3. Start with Get-Passive in Speaking

In conversation, practise get-passive first (got stolen, got promoted) as it's very natural in spoken English. Then gradually add formal passive structures for writing and academic contexts.

Quick Practice

Test your understanding with these multiple-choice questions.

1. Complete the sentence: "The new policy _______ by the board of directors yesterday."

A. was approved
B. get approved
C. is approved
D. has approved

2. Which sentence is most appropriate for informal conversation?

A. My bicycle was stolen from outside the shop.
B. My bike got stolen from outside the shop.
C. My bicycle is reported to have been stolen.
D. The bicycle was taken by an unknown person.

3. Complete the sentence: "I need _______ before the wedding."

A. to get cut
B. to have cut
C. to get my hair cut
D. to be cut

4. Complete the sentence: "The lost documents _______ in the old filing cabinet."

A. are thought to be hidden
B. are thinking to be hidden
C. are thought to hide
D. think to be hidden

5. Complete the sentence: "She hates _______ while she's trying to concentrate."

A. to be disturbed
B. being disturbed
C. to disturb
D. being disturb

6. Which sentence would be most appropriate in a formal business report?

A. The team finished the project on time.
B. The project got completed ahead of schedule.
C. The project was completed ahead of schedule.
D. They got the project done early.

7. Complete the sentence: "The famous painting _______ by Leonardo da Vinci in the 16th century."

A. is believed to be painted
B. is believed to have been painted
C. was believed to be painted
D. believes to have been painted

8. Which sentence shows the correct causative passive structure?

A. I had repaired my computer yesterday.
B. I had my computer repaired yesterday.
C. I had my computer repair yesterday.
D. My computer had repaired yesterday.

9. Complete the sentence: "The documents must _______ before they can _______ to the client."

A. be reviewed / be sent
B. review / send
C. be reviewed / send
D. review / be sent

A. Telling a friend about your weekend plans
B. Writing a scientific research paper
