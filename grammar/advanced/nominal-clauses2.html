Wh-Clauses and Infinitive Clauses

Introduction

Wh-clauses and infinitive clauses are groups of words that work like nouns in sentences. They help you ask about unknown information and express purposes or goals. These clauses make your English sound more natural and help you express complex ideas clearly.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use wh-clauses to express unknown information in subject and object positions.
  • Form infinitive clauses to express purposes, goals, and general truths in different positions.
  • Choose correctly between wh-clauses and infinitive clauses in various situations.

Wh-Clauses

Form: wh-word + subject + verb

When to use it: Use wh-clauses when you don't know specific information. The wh-word (what, where, when, why, how, who) shows what information is missing.

Key rules:

  • Subject clause: Comes at the beginning, before the main verb
  • Object clause: Comes after verbs like know, understand, remember, forget

Subject clauses:

  1. What he said surprised everyone. (his words were surprising)
  2. Where she works is confidential. (her workplace is secret)
  3. How they solved the problem impressed the manager. (their solution was impressive)

Object clauses:

  1. I don't know what time the meeting starts. (the start time is unknown)
  2. She can't remember where she put her keys. (the location is forgotten)
  3. The tourists asked how much the ticket costs. (the price is unknown)

Native speaker note: In wh-clauses, always use normal word order (subject + verb), not question word order. Say "I know what time it is" (not "what time is it").

Infinitive Clauses

Form: to + verb

When to use it: Use infinitive clauses to express purposes, goals, general truths, or future actions. They often show what someone wants to do or what should be done.

Key rules:

  • Subject clause: Comes at the beginning to show general truths or goals
  • Object clause: Comes after verbs like want, decide, plan, hope

Subject clauses:

  1. To finish on time is our main goal. (finishing on time is the goal)
  2. To learn English takes dedication. (learning English requires effort)
  3. To save money requires planning. (saving money needs a plan)

Object clauses:

  1. The company wants to expand overseas. (expansion is the company's goal)
  2. She decided to change careers. (career change is her decision)
  3. We hope to travel next summer. (travel is our hope)

Native speaker note: Infinitive clauses as subjects often sound formal. In everyday speech, we usually start with "It + be + adjective + infinitive" (It's important to study vs. To study is important).

Common Errors

1. Using question word order in wh-clauses
I don't know what time is the meeting.
I don't know what time the meeting is.
Use normal word order (subject + verb) in wh-clauses, not question order.
2. Missing "to" in infinitive clauses
I want learn Spanish.
I want to learn Spanish.
Always include "to" before the verb in infinitive clauses.
3. Using infinitive instead of wh-clause for unknown information
I don't know to do what.
I don't know what to do.
Use wh-clauses when asking about unknown information, not infinitives.

Useful Tips

üìù Tip 1: Use "It + be + adjective + infinitive" for natural-sounding subjects

Instead of starting with an infinitive clause, use "It is..." for more natural speech.

Examples:

It's important to arrive early.
It's difficult to find parking.
It's expensive to live in the city.

üìù Tip 2: Remember: wh-clauses = unknown information, infinitives = purposes/goals

Choose based on whether you're expressing something unknown or a purpose.

Examples:

What she wants is unclear.
To help others makes me happy.
Where he lives is private.

üìù Tip 3: Use embedded questions (wh + to + verb) for advice and instructions

Combine wh-words with infinitives to ask for or give advice about what to do.

Examples:

I know what to do now.
She showed me how to use the machine.
Tell me where to go.

Summary of Wh-Clauses and Infinitive Clauses

Clause Type Form Use Example
Wh-clause (subject) wh-word + subject + verb Unknown information What he said surprised me.
Wh-clause (object) verb + wh-word + subject + verb Unknown information I know what time it is.
Infinitive clause (subject) to + verb Goals/general truths To study is important.
Infinitive clause (object) verb + to + verb Purposes/plans I want to travel.
Embedded question wh-word + to + verb Advice/instructions I know what to do.
