Possessives: My, Your, His, Her, Our, Their

Introduction

Possessive adjectives show who something belongs to. Instead of saying "the book of Maria", we say "her book". You use possessives every day when you talk about your things, your family, your work, and more. The biggest challenge for many students is choosing between "his" and "her".

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use all possessive adjectives (my, your, his, her, its, our, their) correctly.
  • Choose the right possessive based on the person who owns the thing.
  • Understand the difference between subject pronouns and possessive adjectives.

1. The Possessive Adjectives

Form: possessive adjective + noun

When to use it: Use a possessive adjective before a noun to show who it belongs to.

  1. I → my — This is my bag.
    (= The bag belongs to me.)
  2. you → your — Is this your passport?
    (= Does this passport belong to you?)
  3. he → his — He loves his job.
    (= The job belongs to him.)
  4. she → her — She is with her family.
    (= The family belongs to her.)
  5. it → its — The hotel changed its name.
    (= The name belongs to the hotel.)
  6. we → our — We need our tickets.
    (= The tickets belong to us.)
  7. they → their — They like their new house.
    (= The house belongs to them.)

Remember: The possessive adjective matches the OWNER, not the thing. If a man has a car, it is "his car" — even if the car is feminine in your language.

2. "His" vs "Her" — The Most Common Mistake

When to use it: "His" = it belongs to a man or boy. "Her" = it belongs to a woman or girl. Look at the OWNER, not the thing.

  1. Tom is a teacher. His students like him.
    (= Tom is male, so we use "his".)
  2. Maria is a doctor. Her office is on the second floor.
    (= Maria is female, so we use "her".)
  3. My brother lost his phone. My sister found her phone in her bag.
    (= Brother = his. Sister = her.)

Native speaker note: Many languages do not have different words for "his" and "her". If your language is like this, you need extra practice. Always think: is the owner male or female?

3. Don't Confuse Subject Pronouns and Possessive Adjectives

When to use it: Subject pronouns (I, you, he, she, it, we, they) are the person doing the action. Possessive adjectives (my, your, his, her, its, our, their) show who owns something.

  1. She is my friend. Her name is Ana.
    (= "She" does the action. "Her" shows the name belongs to Ana.)
  2. They are at work. Their office is in the centre.
    (= "They" does the action. "Their" shows the office belongs to them.)
  3. We are on holiday. Our hotel is near the beach.
    (= "We" does the action. "Our" shows the hotel belongs to us.)

4. "Its" vs "It's" — Be Careful!

When to use it: "Its" (no apostrophe) = possessive. "It's" (with apostrophe) = "it is". These are two different words.

  1. The cat is eating its food.
    (= The food belongs to the cat.)
  2. It's a beautiful day.
    (= It is a beautiful day.)
  3. The company changed its logo. It's a new design.
    (= The logo belongs to the company. It is a new design.)

Tip: If you can replace the word with "it is", use "it's". If you cannot, use "its".

Common Errors

Confusing "his" and "her"

Maria loves his job.

Maria loves her job.

Maria is female. Use "her" for women and girls, "his" for men and boys.

Using "it's" instead of "its"

The dog ate it's food.

The dog ate its food.

"Its" (no apostrophe) means "belonging to it". "It's" means "it is".

Using a subject pronoun instead of a possessive

They car is blue.

Their car is blue.

"They" is a subject pronoun (They are happy). "Their" is a possessive adjective (Their car).

How to Remember and Practice

📝 Tip: Always look at the OWNER to choose the possessive. Ask: "Who does this belong to?" If it belongs to a woman, use "her". If it belongs to a man, use "his".

📝 Tip: Describe your family using possessives: "My mother's name is... Her job is... My father works in... His hobby is..." This is great practice.

📝 Tip: For "its" vs "it's", always try replacing with "it is". If the sentence still makes sense, use "it's". If not, use "its".
