Subject Pronouns and the Verb "Be"

Introduction

The verb "be" is the most important verb in English. You use it every day to talk about yourself, other people, and things around you. In this lesson, you will learn subject pronouns (I, you, he, she, it, we, they) and how to use them with "am", "is", and "are".

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Use "am", "is", and "are" correctly with each subject pronoun.
  • Make simple sentences about people, places, and things.
  • Make negative sentences and simple questions with "be".

1. Subject Pronouns

What is a subject pronoun? A subject pronoun replaces a person's name or a thing. Instead of saying "Maria is happy", you can say "She is happy".

The subject pronouns are:

  1. I — the person speaking.
  2. you — the person listening (one person or more than one).
  3. he — one man or boy.
  4. she — one woman or girl.
  5. it — one thing, animal, or place.
  6. we — the person speaking + other people.
  7. they — other people or things (more than one).

Remember: "I" is always a capital letter, even in the middle of a sentence.

2. Positive Sentences with "Be"

Form: subject + am / is / are + rest of sentence.

When to use it: Use "be" to talk about names, ages, feelings, jobs, nationalities, and descriptions.

Use "am" with I:

  1. I am tired today.
    (= I feel tired.)
  2. I am an engineer.
    (= This is my job.)
  3. I am at the airport.
    (= This is my location.)

Use "is" with he, she, it:

  1. She is my sister.
    (= This woman is my sister.)
  2. He is the new manager.
    (= This man is the manager.)
  3. It is a nice hotel.
    (= The hotel is nice.)

Use "are" with you, we, they:

  1. You are very kind.
    (= I am describing you.)
  2. We are in a meeting.
    (= My colleagues and I are in a meeting.)
  3. They are from Brazil.
    (= Those people come from Brazil.)

Native speaker note: In everyday speaking, native speakers almost always use short forms: "I'm tired", "She's my sister", "We're in a meeting". The short forms are normal and natural.

3. Short Forms (Contractions)

Form: subject + 'm / 's / 're

When to use it: Use short forms when you speak and in informal writing. Use full forms in formal writing.

  1. I am → I'm — I'm happy to be here.
    (= I am happy to be here.)
  2. he is → he's / she is → she's / it is → it's — He's a good doctor.
    (= He is a good doctor.)
  3. you are → you're / we are → we're / they are → they're — They're at the station.
    (= They are at the station.)

4. Negative Sentences with "Be"

Form: subject + am / is / are + not

When to use it: Use "not" after "be" to make a sentence negative.

  1. I am not hungry.
    (= I don't want food.)
  2. She is not in the office today.
    (= She is somewhere else.)
  3. They are not from Japan.
    (= Japan is not their country.)

Short forms for negatives:

  1. I am not → I'm not.
  2. is not → isn't — She isn't ready.
  3. are not → aren't — We aren't late.

Remember: We do NOT say "I amn't". The only correct short form for "I am not" is "I'm not".

5. Questions with "Be"

Form: am / is / are + subject + rest of sentence + ?

When to use it: To make a yes/no question, put "be" before the subject.

  1. Are you OK?
    (= I want to know if you feel OK.)
  2. Is he your boss?
    (= I want to know about this man.)
  3. Is it far from here?
    (= I want to know the distance.)

Short answers:

  1. Are you tired? — Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.
  2. Is she a teacher? — Yes, she is. / No, she isn't.
  3. Are they at the hotel? — Yes, they are. / No, they aren't.

Remember: In short answers, we do NOT use contractions in positive short answers. Say "Yes, I am" (NOT "Yes, I'm").

Common Errors

Forgetting "be"

She happy.

She is happy.

In English, you always need the verb "be". You cannot leave it out.

Using the wrong form of "be"

He are a doctor.

He is a doctor.

Use "is" with he, she, and it. Use "are" with you, we, and they.

Wrong question word order

You are tired?

Are you tired?

In questions, put "be" before the subject. Say "Are you...?" not "You are...?".

How to Remember and Practice

📝 Tip: Think of "be" as an equals sign (=). "She is a teacher" means "She = a teacher". If you forget "be", there is no connection between the subject and the information.

📝 Tip: Practise the short forms (I'm, he's, they're) when you speak. They sound more natural and will help you speak faster.

📝 Tip: Every morning, say three sentences about yourself: "I'm [name]. I'm [nationality]. I'm [feeling]." This builds the habit of using "be" correctly.
