Present Simple

Introduction

The present simple is one of the first tenses you need to learn. You use it to talk about habits, routines, facts, and things that are always true. It is the tense you will use most often in everyday English.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Make positive sentences in the present simple, including the third person -s (he works, she lives).
  • Make negative sentences with "don't" and "doesn't".
  • Make questions with "do" and "does".

1. Positive Sentences

Form: subject + verb (add -s for he / she / it).

When to use it: Use the present simple for habits, routines, and things that are always true.

I / you / we / they + verb:

  1. I drink coffee every morning.
    (= This is my habit.)
  2. We start work at nine o'clock.
    (= This is our routine.)
  3. They speak English at the hotel.
    (= This is what they do.)

He / she / it + verb + s:

  1. She works in a hospital.
    (= This is her job.)
  2. He takes the bus to work.
    (= This is his routine.)
  3. The shop closes at six o'clock.
    (= This is always true.)

Spelling rules for he/she/it: Most verbs add -s (works, eats, plays). Verbs ending in -s, -sh, -ch, -x, -o add -es (goes, watches, finishes). Verbs ending in consonant + y change to -ies (study → studies, carry → carries).

2. Negative Sentences

Form: subject + don't / doesn't + verb (base form, NO -s).

When to use it: Use "don't" (I/you/we/they) or "doesn't" (he/she/it) to make negative sentences.

  1. I don't like cold weather.
    (= Cold weather is not nice for me.)
  2. She doesn't work on Saturdays.
    (= Saturday is her day off.)
  3. We don't have a map.
    (= We need to find a map.)

Important: After "doesn't", the verb goes back to the base form. Say "She doesn't work" (NOT "She doesn't works"). The -s moves to "does".

3. Questions

Form: do / does + subject + verb (base form, NO -s) + ?

When to use it: Use "Do" (I/you/we/they) or "Does" (he/she/it) at the beginning to make yes/no questions.

  1. Do you speak English?
    (= I want to know if you speak English.)
  2. Does she live near the office?
    (= I want to know where she lives.)
  3. Do they sell tickets here?
    (= I want to know if I can buy tickets.)

Short answers:

  1. Do you work here? — Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
  2. Does he like coffee? — Yes, he does. / No, he doesn't.
  3. Do they live in London? — Yes, they do. / No, they don't.

Remember: After "does", the verb has NO -s. Say "Does she live...?" (NOT "Does she lives...?").

4. Common Time Words with Present Simple

When to use it: These words often appear with the present simple because they describe how often something happens.

  1. I always eat breakfast at home.
    (= 100% of the time.)
  2. She usually takes the train to work.
    (= most of the time.)
  3. We never eat at that restaurant.
    (= 0% of the time.)

Word order: Frequency words (always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never) go BEFORE the main verb: "I always eat." But they go AFTER "be": "She is always happy."

Summary: Present Simple

Type I / you / we / they he / she / it
Positive I work. She works.
Negative I don't work. She doesn't work.
Question Do you work? Does she work?
Short answer (+) Yes, I do. Yes, she does.
Short answer (−) No, I don't. No, she doesn't.

Common Errors

Forgetting the -s with he/she/it

He work in a shop.

He works in a shop.

Always add -s to the verb when the subject is he, she, or it.

Adding -s after "doesn't"

She doesn't works on Sunday.

She doesn't work on Sunday.

After "doesn't", use the base form of the verb (no -s). The -s is already in "does".

Forgetting "do/does" in questions

You like pizza?

Do you like pizza?

In English, you need "Do" or "Does" at the beginning of yes/no questions in the present simple.

How to Remember and Practice

📝 Tip: Remember: "He, she, it — the -s must fit!" This little rhyme will help you remember to add -s for the third person.

📝 Tip: Describe your daily routine every evening. Say: "I wake up at 7. I have breakfast. I go to work..." This is excellent practice for the present simple.

📝 Tip: When you use "doesn't" or "does", the main verb ALWAYS goes back to the base form. Think of "does" as "stealing" the -s from the verb.
