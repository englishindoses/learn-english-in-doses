Still vs Yet

Many learners confuse still and yet because they both connect to the idea of something not happening. However, they have different meanings and they go in different positions in a sentence. This page will help you understand the difference clearly.

What you will learn:

  1. How to use still to talk about something that continues.
  2. How to use yet to talk about something you expect to happen.
  3. How to choose the right word for each situation.

1. Still — Something Continues

Meaning: We use still when a situation started in the past and it continues now. Nothing has changed.

When to use it: Use still when you want to say that something has not stopped or has not changed.

Still in Affirmative Sentences

Form: subject + still + verb

Position: still goes before the main verb.

  1. She still lives with her parents.
    (= She started living with them before. She continues to live with them now.)
  2. I still work at the same company.
    (= I have not changed jobs.)
  3. He still plays football every weekend.
    (= He continues to play. He has not stopped.)

With "be": still goes after the verb "be".

Form: subject + be + still

  1. She is still angry about what happened.
    (= She was angry before and she is angry now.)
  2. The shop is still open.
    (= It has not closed.)
  3. They are still at the restaurant.
    (= They have not left.)

Still in Negative Sentences

We can also use still in negative sentences to show surprise or frustration that something has not changed. This is stronger and more emotional than using yet.

Form: subject + still + haven't/hasn't + past participle (V3)

Position: still goes before the negative auxiliary verb (haven't, hasn't, don't, doesn't, can't, etc.).

  1. I still haven't received your email.
    (= I expected it a long time ago. I am frustrated.)
  2. She still doesn't understand the problem.
    (= This is surprising. I expected her to understand by now.)
  3. They still can't find their keys.
    (= They have been looking for a long time.)

Still in Questions

We can use still in questions when we are checking if a situation continues.

  1. Do you still live in London?
    (= I knew you lived there before. Do you continue to live there?)
  2. Is it still raining?
    (= It was raining before. Has it stopped?)
  3. Are you still waiting for the bus?
    (= You were waiting before. Are you continuing to wait?)

2. Yet — Expected But Not Done

Meaning: We use yet when we expect something to happen, but it has not happened up to now.

When to use it: Use yet in negative sentences and questions when you are talking about something you expect.

Yet in Negative Sentences

Form: subject + haven't/hasn't + past participle (V3) + yet

Position: yet goes at the end of the sentence.

  1. I haven't finished my homework yet.
    (= I expect to finish, but I haven't done it up to now.)
  2. She hasn't called me yet.
    (= I expect her to call, but she hasn't done it up to now.)
  3. We haven't decided yet.
    (= We need to decide, but we haven't done it up to now.)

Yet in Questions

We use yet in questions when we want to know if something expected has happened.

Form: have/has + subject + past participle (V3) + yet?

  1. Have you eaten yet?
    (= I expect you to eat. Have you done it?)
  2. Has the meeting started yet?
    (= I expect it to start. Has it happened?)
  3. Have they arrived yet?
    (= I expect them to arrive. Have they done it?)

Important: We do NOT normally use yet in affirmative sentences. "I have finished yet" is incorrect. Say "I have already finished" or "I have finished."

3. Still vs Yet — How to Choose

The key difference is the idea behind each word:

Still = a situation continues. Nothing has changed.

Yet = something expected has not happened up to now.

Comparing Still and Yet in Negative Sentences

Both still and yet can appear in negative sentences, but they have a different feeling.

  1. I haven't finished yet.
    (= Neutral. I'm simply saying I haven't done it up to now.)
  2. I still haven't finished.
    (= Stronger. I am surprised or frustrated that it is taking so long.)
  1. He hasn't replied yet.
    (= I expect him to reply. He hasn't done it up to now. This is neutral.)
  2. He still hasn't replied.
    (= He has had a long time to reply. I am frustrated or surprised.)

Summary Table

Word Meaning Sentence Type Position
Still Something continues. Affirmative, negative, questions. Before the main verb. After "be".
Yet Expected but not done. Negative and questions only. At the end of the sentence.

Quick Position Guide

  1. She still works here. (still + verb)
  2. She is still here. (be + still)
  3. She still hasn't left. (still + negative auxiliary)
  4. She hasn't left yet. (negative sentence + yet)
  5. Has she left yet? (question + yet)

Native speaker note: In everyday conversation, native speakers sometimes use "yet" at the beginning of a sentence to mean "but" or "however." For example: "It was raining. Yet, she went for a walk." This is a different meaning of "yet" and is not the same as the grammar on this page. This use of "yet" is more common in writing than in speech.

⚠️ Common Errors

"I have finished yet."

"I have already finished." ✓

Do not use "yet" in affirmative sentences. Use "already" instead.

"She yet hasn't called me."

"She hasn't called me yet." ✓

"Yet" goes at the end of the sentence, not before the verb.

"He is still don't understand."

"He still doesn't understand." ✓

"Still" goes before the negative auxiliary verb (doesn't, haven't, can't, etc.). Do not use "be" and "do" together.

Useful Tips

📝 TIP 1: Think of still as a "no change" word. If a situation is the same as before, use "still." If you are waiting for something to happen, use "yet."

📝 TIP 2: Remember the position rule — still goes in the middle of the sentence (before the verb or after "be"). Yet goes at the end.

📝 TIP 3: If you want to show frustration or surprise that something has not changed, use still + negative. "I still haven't heard back" sounds more frustrated than "I haven't heard back yet."
