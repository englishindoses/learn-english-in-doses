The Verb "To Get"

Introduction

"Get" is one of the most common and useful verbs in English. It appears in everyday conversation all the time, but it can be confusing because it has many different meanings. The meaning of "get" changes depending on the context — the words around it tell you which meaning is being used. In this lesson, you will learn the six main meanings of "get" and how to use them correctly.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify and use the six main meanings of "get" in everyday sentences.
  • Choose the correct meaning of "get" based on context.
  • Use "get" naturally instead of more formal alternatives like "receive," "become," and "arrive."

1. Get = Receive or Obtain

Form: subject + get + noun (the thing you receive or obtain)

When to use it: Use "get" with this meaning when someone receives something (like a letter, a gift, or a message) or obtains something through effort (like a job, a qualification, or a result). This is one of the most common meanings of "get."

  1. I got a letter from my friend.
    (= I received a letter from my friend.)
  2. She gets good grades in school.
    (= She receives good grades in school.)
  3. He got a new job last week.
    (= He obtained a new job last week.)

Native speaker note: In everyday English, native speakers almost always say "get" instead of "receive" or "obtain." Saying "I received your email" sounds quite formal. Most people would say "I got your email." You will hear "receive" and "obtain" more often in formal writing, business emails, and official documents.

2. Get = Buy

Form: subject + get + noun (the thing you buy)

When to use it: Use "get" with this meaning when you buy or purchase something. This is very common in casual, everyday English. You can usually tell it means "buy" because the sentence talks about shopping, stores, or spending money.

  1. I need to get some milk from the store.
    (= I need to buy some milk from the store.)
  2. We got a new car yesterday.
    (= We bought a new car yesterday.)
  3. She gets her coffee from the café every morning.
    (= She buys her coffee from the café every morning.)

Native speaker note: When talking about food and daily shopping, native speakers almost always use "get" instead of "buy." For example, "I'll get some bread on the way home" is much more natural than "I'll buy some bread on the way home." Both are correct, but "get" sounds more natural in casual conversation.

3. Get = Become (a Change of State)

Form: subject + get + adjective

When to use it: Use "get" + adjective when you want to describe a change. Something was one way, and now it is changing to something different. For example, the weather was warm, but now it is getting cold. This meaning is very common in spoken English.

  1. It gets cold in winter.
    (= It becomes cold in winter.)
  2. He got angry when he heard the news.
    (= He became angry when he heard the news.)
  3. I'm getting tired.
    (= I'm becoming tired.)

Here are some common adjectives used with "get" in this way: get hungry, get thirsty, get bored, get lost, get ready, get better, get worse, get dark, get old, get married, get dressed.

Native speaker note: "Get" + adjective is much more natural in spoken English than "become" + adjective. Saying "I'm getting hungry" sounds normal and casual, but "I'm becoming hungry" sounds very formal and unusual. However, in formal writing, "become" is still preferred.

4. Get = Arrive or Reach a Place

Form: subject + get + to + place (but: get home, get here, get there — no "to")

When to use it: Use "get" with this meaning when you talk about arriving at or reaching a place. It is also very common in questions about how to travel somewhere. Notice that we say "get home," "get here," and "get there" without "to."

  1. What time did you get home?
    (= What time did you arrive home?)
  2. We got to the airport early.
    (= We arrived at the airport early.)
  3. How do I get to the train station?
    (= How do I reach the train station?)

Native speaker note: When asking for directions, native speakers almost always use "get to" instead of "arrive at" or "reach." "How do I get to the museum?" is the most natural way to ask. You will also hear "get" used for the journey itself: "It takes an hour to get there" (= It takes an hour to travel there).

5. Get = Understand

Form: subject + get + noun / what-clause / it

When to use it: Use "get" with this meaning when you want to say you understand (or don't understand) something. This is informal English and is very common in conversation. You will hear it especially in questions and negative sentences.

  1. I don't get this maths problem.
    (= I don't understand this maths problem.)
  2. Do you get what I mean?
    (= Do you understand what I mean?)
  3. She didn't get the joke.
    (= She didn't understand the joke.)

Native speaker note: "I get it" is a very common way to say "I understand." You might also hear "I don't get it" (I don't understand) or "Got it!" (I understand — often used as a short reply). In formal situations, it is better to use "understand" instead of "get."

6. Get = Fetch or Bring

Form: subject + get + person/thing (+ for someone)

When to use it: Use "get" with this meaning when you ask someone to go somewhere and bring something (or someone) back. It means to go and collect something, then return with it. This is very common in everyday requests.

  1. Can you get me a glass of water?
    (= Can you bring me a glass of water?)
  2. I'll get the kids from school.
    (= I'll fetch the kids from school.)
  3. He went to get the doctor.
    (= He went to fetch the doctor.)

Native speaker note: "Get" is much more common than "fetch" in modern English. "Fetch" is still used, but it can sound a little old-fashioned. Most native speakers would say "Can you get me a coffee?" rather than "Can you fetch me a coffee?" In everyday requests, "get" is the natural choice.

Summary: The Verb "To Get"

Meaning Form Example More Natural Than
Receive / Obtain get + noun I got your message. I received your message.
Buy get + noun I need to get some bread. I need to buy some bread.
Become get + adjective It's getting cold. It's becoming cold.
Arrive / Reach get + to + place We got to the hotel late. We arrived at the hotel late.
Understand get + noun / clause I don't get it. I don't understand it.
Fetch / Bring get + person / thing Can you get me a glass of water? Can you bring me a glass of water?

Common Errors

1: Using "to" with home, here, and there

❌ What time did you get to home?


✓ What time did you get home?

We do not use "to" before "home," "here," or "there." These words already show direction, so "to" is not needed. Say "get home," "get here," and "get there."

2: Forgetting "to" before other places

❌ How do I get the station?


✓ How do I get to the station?

When "get" means "arrive" or "reach," you need "to" before most places. The only exceptions are "home," "here," and "there." For all other places, use "get to" + place.

3: Using "get" + verb instead of "get" + adjective for changes

❌ I'm getting to tire.


✓ I'm getting tired.

When "get" means "become," it is always followed by an adjective, not a verb. Use "get tired," "get hungry," "get angry," etc. The adjective describes the new state.

Useful Tips

📌 TIP 1: When you see "get" in a sentence, look at the word that comes after it. If an adjective follows (like cold, tired, angry), it means "become." If a place follows (like home, to the airport), it means "arrive." If a noun follows (like a letter, a job), it probably means "receive" or "buy." The word after "get" is your biggest clue.

📌 TIP 2: Start using "get" in your everyday English. Many students use "receive," "become," and "arrive" because they learned these words first. These are not wrong, but "get" is much more natural in spoken English. Try replacing one formal verb with "get" each day. For example, say "I got your email" instead of "I received your email."

📌 TIP 3: Keep a list of common "get" + adjective combinations. Write down pairs like "get hungry," "get lost," "get married," "get better," and "get worse." Practise them in sentences about your own life: "I always get hungry at 3 pm" or "The weather is getting worse." This will help you use them naturally.
