In Part 1, you learned that many verbs are followed by fixed prepositions. The same is true for adjectives and nouns. Certain adjectives and nouns always require a specific preposition after them, and using the wrong one is a common mistake â€” even for advanced learners. Just like with verbs, there is no general rule for these combinations. They must be learned as fixed pairs. This page covers the most important adjective + preposition and noun + preposition combinations.

**After this lesson, you will be able to:**

## Adjectives Followed by "of"

**Form:** be + adjective + of + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

**When to use it:**

Many adjectives that describe feelings, awareness, or qualities are followed by *of*.

**Key rules:**

- **Afraid of** â€” feeling fear about something.
- **Aware of** â€” knowing about something.
- **Capable of** â€” having the ability to do something.
- **Fond of** â€” liking something (often used for habits or people).
- **Proud of** â€” feeling satisfaction about an achievement.
- **Tired of** â€” feeling bored or frustrated with something that has continued too long.

1. She is very **proud of** her team's performance this quarter. (she feels satisfaction about it.)
2. He wasn't **aware of** the schedule change until this morning. (he did not know about it.)
3. They are **tired of** waiting for the repairs to be completed. (they are frustrated by the long wait.)

## Adjectives Followed by "about"

**Form:** be + adjective + about + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

**When to use it:**

Adjectives followed by *about* usually express emotions or feelings towards a specific topic or situation.

**Key rules:**

- **Worried about** â€” feeling anxious about something.
- **Excited about** â€” feeling enthusiasm about something.
- **Serious about** â€” being genuine or committed to something.
- **Happy about** â€” feeling pleased about something.
- **Confused about** â€” not understanding something.
- **Curious about** â€” wanting to know more about something.

1. She is **excited about** starting her new role next month. (she feels enthusiastic about it.)
2. He was **confused about** the directions to the hotel. (he did not understand them.)
3. We are **serious about** improving our customer service. (we are committed to it.)

## Adjectives Followed by "for"

**Form:** be + adjective + for + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

**When to use it:**

Adjectives followed by *for* often describe suitability, responsibility, readiness, or feelings towards someone.

**Key rules:**

- **Responsible for** â€” having the duty or obligation for something.
- **Famous for** â€” well-known because of something.
- **Ready for** â€” prepared for something.
- **Sorry for** â€” feeling regret about something.
- **Good/bad for** â€” having a positive or negative effect on something.
- **Grateful for** â€” feeling thankful for something.

1. She is **responsible for** managing the entire project. (it is her duty.)
2. The city is **famous for** its beautiful architecture. (that is what it is known for.)
3. I am **grateful for** all your help with the move. (I feel thankful.)

## Adjectives Followed by "to"

**Form:** be + adjective + to + noun / noun phrase

**When to use it:**

Some adjectives are followed by the preposition *to* when describing a relationship or comparison between two things.

**Key rules:**

- **Similar to** â€” having things in common with something else.
- **Different from/to** â€” not the same as something else. (Both *from* and *to* are correct in British English.)
- **Accustomed to** â€” being used to something.
- **Married to** â€” being someone's husband or wife.
- **Addicted to** â€” being unable to stop doing or using something.
- **Committed to** â€” being dedicated to something.

1. This system is quite **similar to** the one we used at the previous company. (they have features in common.)
2. She is fully **committed to** improving working conditions for her team. (she is dedicated to it.)
3. He is **accustomed to** travelling long distances for work. (he is used to it.)

**Native speaker note:** In British English, both *different from* and *different to* are widely accepted. In American English, *different from* is strongly preferred. In formal writing, *different from* is the safest choice.

## Nouns Followed by Prepositions

**Form:** noun + preposition + noun / noun phrase / verb-ing

**When to use it:**

Just like verbs and adjectives, many nouns require a specific preposition. These combinations often appear in formal writing, business communication, and academic English. Below are some of the most important groups.

**Nouns + for:**

- **Reason for** â€” the cause or explanation for something.
- **Need for** â€” a requirement for something.
- **Demand for** â€” how much people want something.
- **Respect for** â€” admiration for someone or something.

1. The **reason for** the delay was a technical problem. (the cause of the delay.)
2. There is a growing **need for** affordable housing in the city. (a requirement that is increasing.)
3. The **demand for** skilled workers has increased this year. (people want more skilled workers.)

**Nouns + of:**

- **Advantage of** â€” a benefit of something.
- **Knowledge of** â€” understanding of a subject.
- **Fear of** â€” being afraid of something.
- **Lack of** â€” not having enough of something.

1. One **advantage of** remote working is saving time on commuting. (a benefit.)
2. A good **knowledge of** local customs is helpful when travelling abroad. (understanding the customs.)
3. The **lack of** funding caused several projects to be cancelled. (not enough funding.)

**Nouns + in:**

- **Interest in** â€” wanting to know about or do something.
- **Increase in** â€” a rise in something.
- **Difficulty in** â€” having trouble with something.
- **Experience in** â€” having knowledge gained from doing something.

1. She has a strong **interest in** renewable energy. (she wants to learn about it.)
2. There has been a significant **increase in** online bookings this year. (bookings have risen.)
3. He has ten years of **experience in** project management. (he has done it for ten years.)

## Common Errors

**1: Using "interested for" or "interested about" instead of "interested in."**

âœ— She is interested for learning more about the role.

âœ“ She is interested in learning more about the role.

*The adjective *interested* is always followed by *in*. This is one of the most common preposition errors learners make.*

**2: Confusing "responsible of" with "responsible for."**

âœ— He is responsible of the marketing budget.

âœ“ He is responsible for the marketing budget.

*Responsible* is always followed by *for*, never *of*. Many learners make this mistake because their first language uses the equivalent of "of."*

**3: Using a wrong preposition after a noun.**

âœ— The increase of prices affected everyone.

âœ“ The increase in prices affected everyone.

*The noun *increase* is followed by *in*, not *of*. Similarly, *decrease* and *rise* are also followed by *in*.*

ðŸ“Œ **TIP 2:** When you are unsure which preposition follows an adjective or noun, try searching for the phrase in an online dictionary or corpus.
